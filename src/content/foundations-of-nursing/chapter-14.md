By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Describe the major body fluid compartments and explain how water is distributed between intracellular and extracellular spaces.
2. Explain the principles of osmosis, osmotic pressure, and hydrostatic pressure as they govern fluid movement between compartments.

9. Apply the ROME method to interpret arterial blood gas (ABG) values systematically, including identification of compensation.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| **Intracellular fluid (ICF)** | Fluid contained within cells, comprising approximately two-thirds of total body water; principal cations are potassium and magnesium |
| **Extracellular fluid (ECF)** | Fluid outside cells, comprising approximately one-third of total body water; includes plasma and interstitial fluid; principal cation is sodium |
| **Osmolality** | The concentration of solutes per kilogram of solvent, measured in mOsm/kg; serum osmolality normally 275–295 mOsm/kg and is the primary determinant of water distribution |
| **Osmosis** | The passive movement of water across a semipermeable membrane from an area of lower solute concentration to an area of higher solute concentration |
| **Osmotic pressure** | The pulling force exerted by solutes that draws water across a semipermeable membrane; determined by the number of solute particles in solution |
| **Hydrostatic pressure** | The pushing force exerted by fluid against vessel walls that drives filtration of fluid out of capillaries into interstitial spaces |
| **Oncotic pressure** | Osmotic pressure generated by plasma proteins (primarily albumin) that holds fluid within the vascular compartment; also called colloid osmotic pressure |
| **Tonicity** | The effective osmolality of a solution relative to plasma; describes the effect a solution has on cell volume when cells are exposed to it |
| **Isotonic** | A solution with osmolality equal to plasma (~285 mOsm/kg); cells placed in an isotonic solution neither swell nor shrink |
| **Hypotonic** | A solution with osmolality lower than plasma; causes water to move into cells by osmosis, leading to cell swelling |
| **Hypertonic** | A solution with osmolality higher than plasma; causes water to move out of cells by osmosis, leading to cell shrinkage |
| **Third-spacing** | Abnormal accumulation of fluid in body spaces where it is not readily available for physiological use, such as the peritoneal cavity, pleural space, or interstitial tissue |
| **Edema** | Visible swelling caused by excess fluid in the interstitial compartment; results from imbalance in the Starling forces governing fluid exchange |
| **Dehydration** | A state of negative fluid balance in which total body water is reduced; may be isotonic, hypotonic, or hypertonic depending on relative water and solute losses |
| **Hypervolemia** | Excess fluid volume in the vascular compartment, often manifesting as peripheral edema, pulmonary edema, and hypertension |
| **Hyponatremia** | Serum sodium below 135 mEq/L; may cause cerebral edema, confusion, seizures, and death if severe |
| **Hypernatremia** | Serum sodium above 145 mEq/L; indicates relative water deficit; may cause neurological manifestations including altered consciousness and seizures |
| **Hypokalemia** | Serum potassium below 3.5 mEq/L; causes muscle weakness, cardiac dysrhythmias, and paralytic ileus |
| **Hyperkalemia** | Serum potassium above 5.0 mEq/L; life-threatening cardiac dysrhythmias may occur; peaked T waves are an early ECG sign |
| **pH** | A measure of hydrogen ion (H⁺) concentration; the negative logarithm of [H⁺]; normal arterial pH is 7.35–7.45; values below 7.35 indicate acidosis and above 7.45 indicate alkalosis |
| **Buffer system** | A chemical system that resists changes in pH by accepting or donating hydrogen ions; principal physiological buffers include bicarbonate, proteins, and phosphate |
| **Respiratory acidosis** | A primary disorder in which arterial PaCO₂ is elevated (>45 mmHg), lowering pH; caused by hypoventilation |
| **Metabolic acidosis** | A primary disorder in which bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) is reduced (<22 mEq/L), lowering pH; caused by acid accumulation or base loss |
| **ROME method** | A mnemonic for ABG interpretation: Respiratory Opposite, Metabolic Equal; describes the directional relationship between pH and the primary parameter in each disorder |

## Introduction

A 68-year-old man arrives in the emergency department after three days of persistent vomiting. He is confused, tachycardic, and his skin tents when pinched. His arterial blood gas reveals pH 7.50, PaCO₂ 48 mmHg, and HCO₃⁻ 36 mEq/L. His serum potassium is 2.9 mEq/L. This clinical scenario — dehydration, electrolyte loss, and metabolic alkalosis from prolonged vomiting — illustrates how inseparably intertwined fluid balance, electrolyte homeostasis, and acid-base regulation truly are. A disruption in one domain invariably perturbs the others, and nurses who cannot interpret these disturbances cannot safely manage critically ill patients.

Water is the body's universal solvent, comprising 50–70% of total body weight and providing the medium in which every cellular reaction occurs. Dissolved within this water are electrolytes — charged particles that generate the electrical gradients necessary for nerve conduction, muscle contraction, and organ function. Superimposed upon this system is the precise regulation of hydrogen ion concentration, without which enzymes denature, membranes fail, and life becomes impossible.

This chapter builds on the pathophysiological foundations established in Chapter 13 and equips the nurse with the knowledge to assess fluid status, interpret laboratory values, recognize life-threatening electrolyte imbalances, and apply the ROME method to arterial blood gas interpretation. Clinical application is emphasized throughout, culminating in evidence-based nursing management strategies for the wide spectrum of patients who present with these disorders.

## 14.1 Body Fluid Compartments and Distribution

### 14.1.1 Total Body Water and Compartmentalization

Total body water (TBW) varies with age, sex, and body composition. In a healthy adult male, TBW approximates 60% of body weight; in adult females, 50%, due to higher adipose tissue content; and in neonates, as much as 75–80%, reflecting low fat stores and high metabolic activity. TBW is divided into two major compartments separated by the cell membrane. The **intracellular fluid (ICF)** constitutes approximately 40% of body weight (two-thirds of TBW), with potassium (K⁺, ~150 mEq/L) as the dominant cation and phosphate and proteins as the dominant anions; the ICF is the primary site of cellular metabolism. The **extracellular fluid (ECF)** constitutes approximately 20% of body weight (one-third of TBW) and is itself subdivided into three subcompartments: the **intravascular (plasma)** space (~5% of body weight, one-quarter of ECF), which contains sodium (Na⁺, ~140 mEq/L) as its dominant cation and plasma proteins that exert oncotic pressure; the **interstitial fluid** (~15% of body weight, three-quarters of ECF), which bathes cells and has a plasma-like electrolyte composition but is nearly protein-free; and the **transcellular fluid**, a small "third compartment" of approximately 1–2 L including cerebrospinal fluid, pleural fluid, peritoneal fluid, synovial fluid, and gastrointestinal secretions.

> **[Clinical Pearl]** Because sodium is the primary ECF cation, serum sodium concentration is the best single indicator of plasma osmolality and, therefore, of water distribution between compartments. Changes in serum sodium rarely reflect sodium balance alone — they almost always indicate a disturbance in water balance.

### 14.1.2 Regulation of Fluid Balance

Several hormonal and neurological mechanisms work in concert to maintain TBW within a narrow range. **Antidiuretic hormone (ADH/vasopressin)** is released from the posterior pituitary in response to increased plasma osmolality (detected by hypothalamic osmoreceptors) or significant decreases in circulating volume; it increases water reabsorption in the renal collecting duct, diluting plasma and concentrating urine. The **renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS)** is activated by decreased renal perfusion, sympathetic stimulation, or hyponatremia, with aldosterone promoting sodium (and secondarily water) reabsorption in the distal nephron, thereby expanding ECF volume. In opposition, **atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP)** is released from atrial cardiomyocytes in response to atrial stretch caused by volume overload, promoting sodium and water excretion. Finally, the **thirst mechanism** serves as the primary behavioral defense against hypertonicity, with hypothalamic osmoreceptors triggering thirst at plasma osmolality thresholds just above those that stimulate ADH release.

## 14.2 Fluid Dynamics: Osmotic and Hydrostatic Pressure

### 14.2.1 Starling Forces and Capillary Exchange

Fluid movement between the intravascular compartment and the interstitial space is governed by four Starling forces:

1. **Capillary hydrostatic pressure (Pc):** Pushes fluid out of the capillary into the interstitium; elevated by increased arterial pressure or venous congestion.
2. **Interstitial hydrostatic pressure (Pi):** Normally slightly negative (subatmospheric), which facilitates filtration out of the capillary; becomes positive as interstitial fluid accumulates, opposing further filtration.
3. **Plasma oncotic pressure (πc):** Generated by plasma proteins (primarily albumin, ~80%); pulls fluid back into the capillary; reduced by hypoalbuminemia.
4. **Interstitial oncotic pressure (πi):** Generated by proteins that escape into the interstitium; normally very low; draws fluid into the interstitium when elevated by protein-losing states.

Net filtration pressure = (Pc − Pi) − (πc − πi). At the arterial end of the capillary, net pressure favors filtration; at the venous end, net pressure (due to declining Pc) favors reabsorption. The lymphatic system drains excess interstitial fluid back to the circulation.

### 14.2.2 Osmolality, Osmolarity, and Tonicity

**Osmolarity** refers to the number of osmoles per liter of solution (mOsm/L), while **osmolality** is osmoles per kilogram of solvent (mOsm/kg); clinically, the terms are used interchangeably, with measured serum osmolality normally 275–295 mOsm/kg. **Calculated osmolality** is estimated by the formula 2[Na⁺] + [glucose (mg/dL)/18] + [BUN (mg/dL)/2.8], with sodium accounting for nearly all plasma osmolality under normal conditions. The **osmol gap** — the difference between measured and calculated osmolality, normally less than 10 mOsm/kg — indicates the presence of unmeasured osmoles (such as ethanol, methanol, ethylene glycol, or mannitol) when elevated. **Tonicity** (effective osmolality) differs from measured osmolality in that it considers only solutes that do not freely cross cell membranes and therefore exert osmotic force on cell volume; urea, for instance, freely crosses membranes, contributes to measured osmolality, but does not affect tonicity.

> **[Safety Alert]** Rapid correction of either hypernatremia or hyponatremia can be fatal. Cerebral osmotic adaptation occurs within hours to days; over-rapid correction reverses the adaptation and causes cerebral edema (in hypernatremia correction) or osmotic demyelination syndrome (in hyponatremia correction). Never correct serum sodium faster than 8–10 mEq/L per 24 hours in chronic states.

## 14.3 Third-Spacing: Pathophysiology of Edema and Ascites

### 14.3.1 Mechanisms of Edema Formation

Edema forms when the rate of capillary filtration exceeds the lymphatic drainage capacity. Four primary mechanisms drive edema:

| Mechanism | Example Conditions | Primary Starling Force Altered |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Increased capillary hydrostatic pressure | Heart failure, venous obstruction, portal hypertension | ↑ Pc |
| Decreased plasma oncotic pressure | Nephrotic syndrome, liver failure, malnutrition | ↓ πc |
| Increased capillary permeability | Sepsis, burns, anaphylaxis, ARDS | ↑ πi (protein leak) |
| Lymphatic obstruction | Cancer, filariasis, post-surgical | ↓ lymphatic drainage |

**Pitting edema** is graded on a 1+ (≤2 mm indentation, rapid rebound) to 4+ (≥8 mm, no rebound) scale. Dependent edema (ankles, sacrum in bedridden patients) is characteristic of systemic conditions. Pulmonary edema manifests as dyspnea, crackles, and pink frothy sputum, representing a life-threatening emergency.

### 14.3.2 Ascites and Pleural Effusion

**Ascites** is fluid accumulation in the peritoneal cavity. The most common cause in Western countries is cirrhosis (75%), which combines portal hypertension with hypoalbuminemia. The serum-ascites albumin gradient (SAAG = serum albumin − ascites albumin) distinguishes portal hypertension–related ascites (SAAG ≥1.1 g/dL) from exudative etiologies such as malignancy or tuberculosis (SAAG <1.1 g/dL).

Nursing assessment includes daily weight measurement (the most sensitive indicator of fluid accumulation), abdominal girth measurement, monitoring for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (fever, abdominal pain, altered mental status), and respiratory assessment since ascites can splint the diaphragm and impair ventilation.

**Pleural effusions** represent third-spacing into the pleural cavity. Transudates (low protein, caused by hydrostatic/oncotic imbalance) are distinguished from exudates (high protein, caused by inflammation or malignancy) using Light's criteria.

> **[Evidence-Based Practice]** Daily weight measurement is the most sensitive method of monitoring fluid shifts in hospitalized patients. A weight gain of more than 1 kg overnight reliably indicates fluid retention. Nurses should weigh patients at the same time each day (typically morning after voiding, before breakfast) using the same scale and with similar clothing to ensure consistency (Stanton, 2021).

## 14.4 Tonicity and Fluid Shifts: Isotonic, Hypotonic, Hypertonic

### 14.4.1 Isotonic Fluid Shifts

In isotonic volume depletion (e.g., hemorrhage, diarrhea with isotonic losses), water and sodium are lost in proportion, plasma osmolality remains normal, and cells neither swell nor shrink. The ECF volume contracts while the ICF is preserved. Clinical signs include tachycardia, decreased skin turgor, dry mucous membranes, and orthostatic hypotension. Intravenous replacement with isotonic crystalloids (0.9% NaCl or lactated Ringer's) restores ECF without shifting water into or out of cells.

In isotonic fluid excess (e.g., excessive isotonic IV fluid administration, inappropriate aldosterone secretion), ECF expands without osmolar disturbance; interstitial edema and pulmonary congestion may develop.

### 14.4.2 Hypotonic and Hypertonic Fluid Shifts

**Hypotonic states** (low plasma osmolality/hyponatremia) cause water to shift into cells by osmosis, leading to cellular swelling. In the brain, this manifests as cerebral edema — headache, nausea, lethargy, seizures, and herniation in severe cases. Hypotonic states arise from excess water intake relative to solute (SIADH, psychogenic polydipsia, excess hypotonic IV fluids) or from sodium loss exceeding water loss.

**Hypertonic states** (high plasma osmolality/hypernatremia) cause water to shift out of cells, leading to cellular shrinkage. In neurons, this produces brain shrinkage, tearing of bridging cerebral veins, and neurological manifestations (lethargy, irritability, seizures). Hypertonic states arise from water deficit (insensible losses, diabetes insipidus, inadequate intake) or from excess sodium gain.

*Figure 14.2 — Effect of Tonicity on Red Blood Cell Volume: Three panels showing red blood cells in isotonic (normal biconcave shape), hypotonic (swollen/crenated), and hypertonic (shrunken/crenated) solutions.*

> **[Patient Education]** Patients discharged with diuretic therapy should be instructed to weigh themselves daily and report a weight gain of more than 2–3 pounds in 24 hours or 5 pounds in one week, as this suggests fluid retention requiring medical evaluation. They should also know the symptoms of electrolyte imbalance (muscle cramps, weakness, palpitations) and when to seek care.

## 14.5 Fluid Imbalances: Dehydration and Fluid Overload

### 14.5.1 Dehydration (Fluid Volume Deficit)

**Dehydration** denotes a reduction in total body water. It may be classified by tonicity:

| Type | Mechanism | Serum Na⁺ | Clinical Example |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| Isotonic | Equal water and solute loss | Normal (135–145 mEq/L) | Hemorrhage, vomiting, diarrhea |
| Hypotonic | Solute loss exceeds water loss | Low (<135 mEq/L) | Diuretic use, adrenal insufficiency |
| Hypertonic | Water loss exceeds solute loss | High (>145 mEq/L) | Fever, diabetes insipidus, inadequate intake |

**Clinical manifestations** progress with severity: mild (thirst, dry mouth, decreased urine output) → moderate (orthostatic hypotension, tachycardia, decreased skin turgor, dark concentrated urine, BUN/creatinine ratio >20:1) → severe (hypotension, altered mental status, oliguria/anuria, shock).

**Nursing interventions** include accurate intake and output measurement, intravenous fluid replacement matched to the type of deficit, daily weights, reassessment of vital signs and skin turgor, oral rehydration when appropriate, and identification/treatment of the underlying cause.

### 14.5.2 Fluid Volume Excess (Hypervolemia)

Fluid volume excess results from retention of sodium and water, commonly caused by heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, or aggressive IV fluid administration. The clinical picture encompasses peripheral edema — dependent and pitting, appearing at the ankles in ambulatory patients and the sacrum in bedridden patients — as well as pulmonary edema manifesting as dyspnea, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, bilateral crackles, and pink frothy sputum. Elevated jugular venous pressure reflects right-sided congestion, while hypertension results from vascular volume overload; an S₃ gallop indicates ventricular volume overload in heart failure. Laboratory findings include dilutional hyponatremia, elevated BNP/NT-proBNP in heart failure, and low hematocrit from hemodilution.

Management centers on sodium and fluid restriction, diuretic therapy (loop diuretics such as furosemide are first-line), identification and treatment of the underlying cause, and close monitoring of urine output, daily weight, and electrolytes.

> **[Pharmacology Spotlight]** Loop diuretics (furosemide, bumetanide, torsemide) inhibit the Na⁺/K⁺/2Cl⁻ cotransporter in the thick ascending loop of Henle, producing potent natriuresis and diuresis. Major nursing considerations include: monitoring serum potassium (risk of hypokalemia), monitoring blood pressure (risk of hypotension), assessing for ototoxicity (especially with high doses or concurrent aminoglycoside use), and educating patients to weigh daily and report rapid weight changes.

## 14.6 Electrolyte Imbalances: Sodium, Potassium, Calcium, Magnesium, Phosphorus

### 14.6.1 Sodium Imbalances

**Sodium** (normal: 135–145 mEq/L) is the dominant ECF cation and the primary determinant of plasma osmolality and ECF volume.

**Hyponatremia** (<135 mEq/L) is the most common electrolyte disorder in hospitalized patients and must first be classified by tonicity. *Hypotonic hyponatremia* (true hyponatremia) results from excess water relative to sodium, with causes including SIADH, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency, heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, and excess hypotonic fluid administration. *Isotonic hyponatremia* (pseudohyponatremia) is artefactual, arising when extreme hyperlipidemia or hyperproteinemia displaces water from plasma. *Hypertonic hyponatremia* occurs when a solute such as glucose or mannitol draws water into the ECF, diluting sodium.

Symptoms depend on severity and rapidity of onset: nausea, headache, lethargy, confusion, seizures, and coma. Treatment of SIADH-related hyponatremia includes fluid restriction; hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) is reserved for severe symptomatic hyponatremia and must be administered with careful monitoring to avoid over-rapid correction.

**Hypernatremia** (>145 mEq/L) invariably indicates hyperosmolality. Most commonly it results from insensible water loss (fever, respiratory illness, burns) in patients unable to access water (elderly, intubated, cognitively impaired). Central or nephrogenic diabetes insipidus causes profound free water loss. Treatment involves replacing the water deficit using free water (oral/enteral) or hypotonic IV fluids (0.45% NaCl or 5% dextrose in water), with correction rate limited to ≤10 mEq/L per 24 hours to prevent cerebral edema.

### 14.6.2 Potassium Imbalances

**Potassium** (normal: 3.5–5.0 mEq/L) is the predominant ICF cation; 98% is intracellular. The ratio of intracellular to extracellular K⁺ determines the resting membrane potential of excitable cells, making even small changes in serum K⁺ clinically significant.

**Hypokalemia** (<3.5 mEq/L) arises most commonly from gastrointestinal losses such as vomiting, diarrhea, and NG suction; renal losses from diuretics, hyperaldosteronism, or metabolic alkalosis; poor dietary intake; or insulin- and β₂-agonist–mediated shifts of potassium into cells. Clinical manifestations include muscle weakness, cramps, fatigue, and constipation or ileus; ECG changes progress from flattening of T waves and prominent U waves to ST depression, with severe hypokalemia potentially causing ventricular arrhythmias and respiratory muscle paralysis. Nursing management prioritizes oral KCl supplementation when tolerated; when IV KCl is required for severe cases, it must never be given by IV push (which is fatal), the maximum peripheral infusion rate is 10 mEq/hour, rates exceeding 10 mEq/hour require a central line and continuous cardiac monitoring, and concurrent magnesium repletion is essential because hypomagnesemia perpetuates renal K⁺ wasting.

**Hyperkalemia** (>5.0 mEq/L) results from renal failure's inability to excrete potassium, tissue destruction such as rhabdomyolysis, hemolysis, or tumor lysis, acidosis-driven H⁺-K⁺ exchange that shifts K⁺ out of cells, ACE inhibitors or potassium-sparing diuretics, or pseudohyperkalemia from a hemolyzed specimen. Manifestations include paresthesias and weakness; ECG changes progress sequentially with rising K⁺ from peaked T waves to widened QRS, loss of P waves, a sine wave pattern, and ultimately ventricular fibrillation or asystole. Nursing management requires continuous cardiac monitoring throughout and proceeds with calcium gluconate for immediate cardiac membrane stabilization (onset within minutes, duration 30–60 minutes), insulin plus glucose to shift K⁺ into cells, sodium bicarbonate if acidosis is present, kayexalate or patiromer to bind K⁺ in the GI tract, and dialysis for refractory severe hyperkalemia, while always identifying and treating the underlying cause.

> **[Safety Alert]** Intravenous potassium chloride must never be administered undiluted or by IV push. Concentrated KCl given rapidly causes cardiac arrest. Always verify the dilution and infusion rate before administration. When infusing IV potassium peripherally, the maximum concentration is typically 40 mEq/100 mL and rate ≤10 mEq/hour. Central venous access and continuous cardiac monitoring are required for rates >10 mEq/hour.

### 14.6.3 Calcium Imbalances

**Calcium** (normal total: 8.5–10.5 mg/dL; ionized: 4.5–5.5 mg/dL) is critical for muscle contraction, nerve conduction, cardiac automaticity, and coagulation. Approximately 40% of total serum calcium is bound to albumin; correction is required for hypoalbuminemia (corrected Ca = measured Ca + 0.8 × [4 − albumin g/dL]).

**Hypocalcemia** (<8.5 mg/dL total): Caused by hypoparathyroidism (post-thyroidectomy), vitamin D deficiency, hypomagnesemia, pancreatitis, and massive blood transfusion (citrate chelation). Manifestations include neuromuscular excitability: paresthesias, muscle cramps, **Trousseau's sign** (carpal spasm with blood pressure cuff inflation), **Chvostek's sign** (facial twitch with tapping CN VII), laryngospasm, seizures, and prolonged QTc. Treatment includes calcium gluconate (IV preferred in symptomatic hypocalcemia) and oral calcium/vitamin D supplementation.

**Hypercalcemia** (>10.5 mg/dL): Caused most commonly by primary hyperparathyroidism and malignancy (PTH-related peptide or osteolytic metastases). Manifests as "bones, stones, groans, and psychic moans" — bone pain, renal calculi, constipation/nausea, and confusion/depression. Severe hypercalcemia causes shortened QTc, bradycardia, and renal failure. Treatment includes aggressive IV hydration (to promote calciuresis), loop diuretics, bisphosphonates, calcitonin, and treatment of the underlying cause.

### 14.6.4 Magnesium and Phosphorus Imbalances

**Magnesium** (normal: 1.5–2.5 mEq/L) is required for ATP production, protein synthesis, and is a cofactor for the sodium-potassium ATPase pump. Hypomagnesemia is the most common electrolyte disturbance in ICU patients and perpetuates both hypokalemia and hypocalcemia by impairing PTH secretion and renal K⁺ reabsorption. Causes include alcoholism, malabsorption, prolonged diarrhea, and loop/thiazide diuretic use. Manifestations mirror hypocalcemia: neuromuscular irritability, tremors, seizures, and cardiac arrhythmias (torsades de pointes). Treatment: IV magnesium sulfate for symptomatic cases; oral magnesium oxide for mild deficiency.

**Hypermagnesemia** is rare except in patients receiving magnesium sulfate (preeclampsia treatment) or with renal failure. Manifestations progress from loss of deep tendon reflexes → hypotension → respiratory depression → cardiac arrest. Calcium gluconate is the antidote for life-threatening hypermagnesemia.

**Phosphorus** (normal: 2.5–4.5 mg/dL) is essential for ATP synthesis, cellular signaling, and bone mineralization. **Hypophosphatemia** (common in refeeding syndrome, malnutrition, diabetic ketoacidosis treatment) causes muscle weakness, respiratory failure, hemolytic anemia, and altered mental status. **Hyperphosphatemia** (primarily in chronic kidney disease) promotes calcium-phosphate precipitation in soft tissues and drives secondary hyperparathyroidism.

> **[Cultural Considerations]** Dietary practices significantly influence electrolyte intake and balance. For example, high-potassium foods are culturally prominent in Caribbean, West African, and Latin American diets (plantains, beans, yams), which may be beneficial in preventing hypokalemia but require counseling for patients with renal insufficiency. Nurses should conduct culturally sensitive dietary assessments and collaborate with dietitians to provide culturally appropriate nutritional guidance.

## 14.7 Acid-Base Physiology: Respiratory and Renal Compensation

### 14.7.1 Buffer Systems and pH Regulation

The normal arterial pH is 7.35–7.45, representing a hydrogen ion concentration of approximately 40 nmol/L. Three buffer systems work together to minimize pH fluctuations:

1. **Bicarbonate buffer system** (the most clinically important): CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻. This reaction, catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase, allows the lungs to regulate CO₂ (an "acid equivalent") and the kidneys to regulate HCO₃⁻ (a "base"). The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation expresses this relationship: pH = 6.1 + log([HCO₃⁻] / [0.03 × PaCO₂]).
2. **Protein buffer system:** Histidine residues on hemoglobin and plasma proteins can accept or donate H⁺; accounts for the majority of intracellular buffering.
3. **Phosphate buffer system:** HPO₄²⁻ + H⁺ ⇌ H₂PO₄⁻; important intracellularly and in renal tubular buffering.

### 14.7.2 Respiratory and Renal Regulation of pH

The **respiratory system** responds within minutes: chemoreceptors in the medulla and carotid bodies detect changes in PaCO₂ and pH and adjust ventilation accordingly. Hyperventilation lowers PaCO₂ (removes carbonic acid) and raises pH; hypoventilation raises PaCO₂ and lowers pH.

The **renal system** responds over hours to days but exerts more powerful long-term control: the proximal tubule reabsorbs ~85% of filtered HCO₃⁻; the distal nephron secretes H⁺ (generating new HCO₃⁻) and excretes titratable acids (H₂PO₄⁻) and ammonium (NH₄⁺). In acidosis, the kidneys increase H⁺ secretion and HCO₃⁻ reabsorption; in alkalosis, HCO₃⁻ is excreted and H⁺ retention occurs.

**Compensation** is the normal physiological response that partially corrects pH: a primary respiratory disorder is compensated by the kidneys, and a primary metabolic disorder is compensated by the lungs. Compensation restores pH toward normal but never fully normalizes it (pH normalization would eliminate the drive to compensate).

## 14.8 Acidosis and Alkalosis: Respiratory and Metabolic

### 14.8.1 Respiratory Acidosis and Alkalosis

**Respiratory acidosis** (PaCO₂ > 45 mmHg, pH < 7.35) is caused by hypoventilation — any condition that reduces alveolar ventilation, including COPD exacerbation, severe asthma, respiratory muscle failure from Guillain-Barré syndrome or myasthenia gravis, opioid or sedative overdose, obesity hypoventilation syndrome, and other neuromuscular diseases. The renal compensatory response, which develops over hours to days in chronic respiratory acidosis, involves retention of HCO₃⁻, rising approximately 3.5 mEq/L for every 10 mmHg increase in PaCO₂. Clinical manifestations include dyspnea, headache, confusion, somnolence, asterixis (so-called "CO₂ narcosis"), and papilledema, with severe hypercapnia potentially progressing to coma. Nursing management focuses on treating the underlying cause, supporting ventilation through positioning and supplemental oxygen (used cautiously in COPD patients), non-invasive positive pressure ventilation such as BiPAP or CPAP, or intubation when necessary, while avoiding respiratory depressants and monitoring serial ABGs.

**Respiratory alkalosis** (PaCO₂ < 35 mmHg, pH > 7.45) results from hyperventilation driven by anxiety or pain, over-ventilation during mechanical ventilation, hypoxia from high altitude or pulmonary embolism, pregnancy, hepatic failure, early sepsis, or the early phase of salicylate toxicity. Chronic compensation occurs through renal excretion of HCO₃⁻, which falls approximately 5 mEq/L for every 10 mmHg decrease in PaCO₂. Clinical manifestations include lightheadedness, perioral and extremity paresthesias, carpopedal spasm from ionized calcium shift, and syncope. Nursing management centers on addressing the underlying cause; for anxiety-driven hyperventilation, coaching slow breathing is effective, while mechanically ventilated patients require adjustment of ventilator settings.

### 14.8.2 Metabolic Acidosis and Alkalosis

**Metabolic acidosis** (HCO₃⁻ < 22 mEq/L, pH < 7.35) is further characterized by calculating the anion gap (AG = Na⁺ − [Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻]; normal 8–12 mEq/L). An elevated anion gap indicates accumulation of unmeasured anions from lactic acid, ketoacids, renal failure acids, or toxic ingestions — recalled by the mnemonic "MUDPILES" (Methanol, Uremia, DKA, Propylene glycol, Isoniazid/Infection, Lactic acidosis, Ethylene glycol, Salicylates). A normal anion gap (hyperchloremic) metabolic acidosis, by contrast, reflects loss of bicarbonate or gain of chloride, as occurs in diarrhea, renal tubular acidosis, or excessive normal saline infusion. The respiratory compensatory response is hyperventilation (Kussmaul respirations), with each 1 mEq/L decrease in HCO₃⁻ lowering PaCO₂ by approximately 1.2 mmHg; Winter's formula (expected PaCO₂ = 1.5[HCO₃⁻] + 8 ± 2) quantifies the expected compensation. Manifestations include Kussmaul respirations, fatigue, confusion, hyperkalemia from H⁺-K⁺ exchange, and negative inotropy when pH falls below 7.20. Management targets the underlying cause; sodium bicarbonate is reserved for pH below 7.10 or life-threatening acidosis and must be used with caution as it can paradoxically worsen intracellular acidosis and generate CO₂.

**Metabolic alkalosis** (HCO₃⁻ > 26 mEq/L, pH > 7.45) results from loss of hydrogen ions through vomiting, NG suction, diuretics, or hyperaldosteronism, or from gain of bicarbonate through excess antacid or bicarbonate administration. A critical distinction guides management: *chloride-responsive* alkalosis (urine Cl⁻ < 20 mEq/L) is perpetuated by volume depletion and responds to saline administration, and is most commonly caused by vomiting, NG suction, or diuretics; *chloride-resistant* alkalosis (urine Cl⁻ > 20 mEq/L), caused by primary hyperaldosteronism, severe hypokalemia, or Cushing's syndrome, does not respond to saline. Respiratory compensation through hypoventilation is limited because the hypoxic drive eventually overrides it; the expected PaCO₂ rises approximately 0.7 mmHg per 1 mEq/L rise in HCO₃⁻. Manifestations are often absent but may include hypocalcemic symptoms from ionized calcium shift, dysrhythmias, neuromuscular irritability, and concurrent hypokalemia. Management of chloride-responsive alkalosis involves IV normal saline and potassium replacement, while chloride-resistant types require addressing the underlying aldosterone excess.

## 14.9 ABG Interpretation: Step-by-Step Approach (ROME Method)

### 14.9.1 Normal ABG Values

| Parameter | Normal Range | What It Reflects |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| pH | 7.35–7.45 | H⁺ concentration; overall acid-base status |
| PaCO₂ | 35–45 mmHg | Respiratory component; alveolar ventilation |
| HCO₃⁻ | 22–26 mEq/L | Metabolic component; renal regulation |
| PaO₂ | 80–100 mmHg | Oxygenation (not acid-base, but reported on ABG) |
| SaO₂ | 95–100% | Oxygen saturation of hemoglobin |
| Base excess (BE) | −2 to +2 mEq/L | Net metabolic deviation from normal; positive = alkalosis |

### 14.9.2 The ROME Method: A Systematic Approach

The **ROME mnemonic** (Respiratory Opposite, Metabolic Equal) describes the directional relationship between pH change and the primary abnormal parameter. In **respiratory disorders**, the primary parameter (PaCO₂) moves in the *opposite* direction to pH: respiratory acidosis is characterized by a falling pH alongside a rising PaCO₂, while respiratory alkalosis shows a rising pH with a falling PaCO₂. In **metabolic disorders**, the primary parameter (HCO₃⁻) moves in the *same* direction as pH: metabolic acidosis presents with both pH and HCO₃⁻ falling together, and metabolic alkalosis presents with both rising together.

**Step-by-step ABG interpretation:**

**Step 1 — Evaluate the pH:** Is it acidotic (<7.35), normal (7.35–7.45), or alkalotic (>7.45)? If normal, is it trending toward the acidotic (<7.40) or alkalotic (>7.40) side? This reveals which primary disorder is driving the picture.

**Step 2 — Evaluate the PaCO₂:** Is it low (<35), normal, or high (>45)? Using ROME: if PaCO₂ moves opposite to pH → respiratory cause.

**Step 3 — Evaluate the HCO₃⁻:** Is it low (<22), normal, or high (>26)? Using ROME: if HCO₃⁻ moves in the same direction as pH → metabolic cause.

**Step 4 — Determine the primary disorder:** Match the pH with the parameter that explains it. Only one disorder is primary (unless mixed).

**Step 5 — Assess for compensation:** Is the non-primary parameter moving in the direction expected for compensation? Calculate whether compensation is appropriate using expected compensation formulas. Appropriate compensation = simple disorder; inappropriate compensation = mixed disorder.

**Step 6 — Evaluate oxygenation:** Is PaO₂ normal (80–100 mmHg)? Note that acid-base interpretation does not require normal oxygenation; they are independent assessments.

**Worked Example:**
pH = 7.28, PaCO₂ = 58 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ = 26 mEq/L, PaO₂ = 62 mmHg

Applying the six-step method: the pH of 7.28 indicates acidosis (Step 1). The elevated PaCO₂ of 58 mmHg moves opposite to the pH, identifying a respiratory component (Step 2), while HCO₃⁻ of 26 mEq/L is normal, confirming no metabolic contribution (Step 3). The primary disorder is therefore **respiratory acidosis** (Step 4). To assess compensation (Step 5), the expected HCO₃⁻ for chronic respiratory acidosis is calculated as 24 + 3.5 × (58 − 45)/10 = 24 + 4.6 ≈ 28.6 mEq/L; the actual HCO₃⁻ of 26 mEq/L is lower than expected, indicating **acute or partially compensated respiratory acidosis**. Finally, the PaO₂ of 62 mmHg confirms the presence of hypoxemia (Step 6).

> **[Clinical Pearl]** A normal pH in the setting of abnormal PaCO₂ and HCO₃⁻ should raise suspicion for a **mixed acid-base disorder** in which two primary disturbances partially offset each other. For example, a patient with COPD (chronic respiratory acidosis) who develops vomiting (metabolic alkalosis) may have a deceptively near-normal pH despite significant physiological derangement. Always correlate ABG findings with the clinical picture.

## 14.10 Nursing Management of Fluid and Electrolyte Disorders

### 14.10.1 Assessment and Monitoring

Comprehensive fluid and electrolyte assessment begins with a focused history and physical examination and is guided by serial laboratory monitoring:

**History:** Recent fluid intake and losses (vomiting, diarrhea, diaphoresis, polyuria); dietary sodium and potassium intake; medications (diuretics, ACE inhibitors, NSAIDs, corticosteroids); chronic diseases (heart failure, cirrhosis, renal disease, diabetes); recent surgeries or trauma.

**Physical examination** should be systematic and thorough. Vital signs provide immediate direction: tachycardia and hypotension suggest volume depletion, while hypertension may indicate volume excess, pain, or anxiety. Daily weight — with 1 kg approximating 1 L of fluid — is the most reliable ongoing indicator of fluid balance. Skin assessment includes testing turgor over the clavicle or sternum in elderly patients, inspecting the moisture of mucous membranes, and evaluating capillary refill. Jugular venous pressure (JVP) elevated above 3 cm above the sternal angle suggests right-sided volume overload. Auscultation may reveal crackles consistent with pulmonary edema or an S₃ gallop reflecting volume overload. Neuromuscular findings are equally important: deep tendon reflexes that are hypo- or hyperreflexive may indicate magnesium or calcium disorders, and Chvostek's and Trousseau's signs, muscle strength, and level of consciousness should all be assessed.

**Laboratory monitoring** is guided by the clinical picture. The basic metabolic panel (BMP) — measuring sodium, potassium, chloride, bicarbonate, BUN, creatinine, and glucose — is ordered frequently in acutely ill patients and forms the foundation of electrolyte assessment. Measured and calculated osmolality enable osmol gap calculation to detect unmeasured osmoles. Urine sodium and osmolality help differentiate the causes of hyponatremia and assess renal tubular function. Serum lactate is measured when tissue hypoperfusion and lactic acidosis are suspected, and arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis provides definitive assessment of acid-base status and oxygenation in critically ill or at-risk patients.

### 14.10.2 Interprofessional Collaboration and Evidence-Based Interventions

Fluid and electrolyte management is inherently interprofessional. The nurse coordinates care among physicians, pharmacists, dietitians, and respiratory therapists.

**Key nursing interventions** span assessment, administration, education, and communication. Nurses maintain accurate intake and output records that capture all oral, IV, and enteral inputs alongside all urinary, fecal, nasogastric, wound, and insensible outputs. IV fluids and electrolyte replacements are administered per order only after verifying the correct fluid type, concentration, rate, and route; IV access sites are assessed regularly for patency and signs of infiltration or phlebitis, particularly when infusing concentrated solutions. Patient and family education addresses dietary modifications specific to the patient's condition, including sodium restriction in heart failure and hypertension, potassium-rich foods for hypokalemia, and avoidance of high-potassium foods in renal failure. The nurse must recognize early warning signs of deterioration — including increasing edema, worsening dyspnea, declining urine output, and ECG changes — and escalate promptly. Fluid balance trends, including cumulative balance over 24 hours, are documented and communicated across shifts to ensure continuity of care.

> **[Evidence-Based Practice]** Goal-directed fluid therapy (GDFT) — titrating IV fluids to specific hemodynamic endpoints such as stroke volume variation, pulse pressure variation, or central venous oxygen saturation — reduces postoperative complications and ICU length of stay compared with liberal fluid strategies in surgical and critically ill patients (Messina et al., 2017). Nurses in perioperative and critical care settings should be familiar with GDFT monitoring parameters and the hemodynamic devices used to guide therapy.

## Case Study: "Fluid and Electrolyte Crisis"

**Patient:** Mr. James Okafor, 72-year-old male with a history of heart failure, hypertension, and type 2 diabetes. He is admitted after five days of nausea, vomiting, and inability to take his oral medications.

---

**Scene 1: Emergency Department Presentation**

On arrival, Mr. Okafor is lethargic. Vital signs: BP 86/52 mmHg, HR 118 bpm, RR 24/min, SpO₂ 90% on room air. His skin is cool and dry with poor turgor; mucous membranes are parched. ECG shows peaked T waves. His daughter reports he has been taking his full dose of spironolactone and lisinopril throughout the illness despite being unable to keep fluids down.

Laboratory results revealed serum Na⁺ 131 mEq/L, K⁺ 6.2 mEq/L, Cl⁻ 92 mEq/L, and HCO₃⁻ 16 mEq/L; BUN 64 mg/dL and creatinine 3.1 mg/dL (baseline 1.4 mg/dL); glucose 210 mg/dL; and ABG values of pH 7.22, PaCO₂ 30 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 12 mEq/L, and PaO₂ 72 mmHg.

**Discussion Questions:**

1. **Interpret Mr. Okafor's ABG using the ROME method. Identify the primary disorder and assess whether compensation is appropriate.**

   *Guidance:* pH 7.22 (acidosis) → HCO₃⁻ 12 mEq/L (low, moves same direction as pH) → **metabolic acidosis**. Expected PaCO₂ = 1.5(12) + 8 ± 2 = 26 ± 2; actual PaCO₂ 30 mmHg — slightly higher than expected, suggesting a concurrent respiratory process or incomplete compensation.

2. **Calculate the anion gap and determine the likely cause of Mr. Okafor's metabolic acidosis.**

   *Guidance:* AG = 131 − (92 + 16) = 23 mEq/L (elevated). Elevated AG acidosis in a diabetic with acute renal failure: consider diabetic ketoacidosis (glucose 210 — DKA possible even with relatively mild hyperglycemia in Type 2; check ketones), lactic acidosis from hypoperfusion, and uremic acidosis.

---

**Scene 2: ICU Stabilization**

Mr. Okafor is transferred to the ICU. He receives IV normal saline boluses, continuous cardiac monitoring, and his ACE inhibitor and potassium-sparing diuretic are held. Calcium gluconate 1 g IV is administered for the hyperkalemia-induced ECG changes. Two hours later his K⁺ is 5.4 mEq/L, BP 102/64 mmHg. Urine output has been 20 mL/hour.

**Discussion Questions:**

3. **Mr. Okafor's Na⁺ is 131 mEq/L. Classify his hyponatremia and explain the most likely mechanism given his clinical presentation.**

   *Guidance:* With hypovolemia (poor turgor, hypotension, elevated BUN/creatinine ratio >20:1), this is most consistent with **hypovolemic hypotonic hyponatremia** — sodium and water losses (from vomiting and inability to drink) with the body's ADH response retaining water relative to sodium. Management is isotonic saline to restore volume, which will allow ADH to fall and sodium to correct gradually.

4. **Describe three priority nursing interventions for Mr. Okafor during this stabilization phase and provide the rationale for each.**

   *Guidance:* (1) Continuous cardiac monitoring — hyperkalemia (K⁺ 6.2 → 5.4) and acidosis both threaten cardiac conduction; (2) Accurate hourly urine output monitoring — oliguric AKI requires careful fluid balance to avoid both under-resuscitation and fluid overload; (3) Frequent neurological assessment — hyponatremia (Na⁺ 131) places him at risk for cerebral complications if Na⁺ is corrected too rapidly or worsens.

---

**Scene 3: Step-Down and Discharge Planning**

After 48 hours, Mr. Okafor's laboratory values are normalizing: Na⁺ 138, K⁺ 4.1, HCO₃⁻ 22 mEq/L, creatinine 1.7 mg/dL. He is tolerating oral intake. The team plans to restart his heart failure medications at adjusted doses. He asks why he "got so sick so fast."

The nurse explains that vomiting led to fluid and electrolyte loss; without being able to drink, his body became dehydrated; the medications that help his heart — particularly spironolactone and lisinopril — contributed to potassium retention and affected kidney function when he was dehydrated. The nurse provides written instructions for monitoring his weight daily, recognizing early signs of dehydration, and knowing when to hold medications and seek medical attention.

## Chapter Summary

### Key Takeaways

1. Total body water (TBW) constitutes 50–70% of body weight and is divided into intracellular fluid (ICF, ~40% body weight) and extracellular fluid (ECF, ~20%); sodium is the dominant ECF cation and the primary determinant of plasma osmolality.

2. Fluid movement between compartments is governed by Starling forces: capillary hydrostatic pressure and interstitial oncotic pressure favor filtration; plasma oncotic pressure (primarily albumin) and interstitial hydrostatic pressure favor reabsorption. Disruption of any Starling force can produce edema.

3. Third-spacing — accumulation of fluid in transcellular compartments (ascites, pleural effusion, interstitial edema) — reduces effective circulating volume even when total body water is increased; daily weights and serial assessment are essential monitoring tools.

4. Tonicity (effective osmolality) determines the direction of cell volume change: isotonic fluids cause no change; hypotonic solutions cause cells to swell; hypertonic solutions cause cells to shrink. Rapid correction of chronic sodium disorders risks brain injury — cerebral edema with hypernatremia correction and osmotic demyelination syndrome with hyponatremia correction.

5. Hypokalemia and hyperkalemia are the most immediately life-threatening electrolyte imbalances due to their effects on cardiac conduction. ECG monitoring is essential; IV potassium must never be administered undiluted or by rapid bolus.

6. Hypocalcemia manifests with neuromuscular hyperexcitability (Trousseau's and Chvostek's signs); hypercalcemia presents with "bones, stones, groans, and psychic moans." Concurrent hypomagnesemia perpetuates both hypokalemia and hypocalcemia.

7. Three buffer systems (bicarbonate, protein, phosphate) resist pH changes. The lungs regulate CO₂ within minutes; the kidneys regulate HCO₃⁻ over hours to days. Compensation partially restores pH but never fully normalizes it.

8. The ROME method (Respiratory Opposite, Metabolic Equal) provides a systematic framework for determining whether an acid-base disorder is respiratory or metabolic: in respiratory disorders, pH and PaCO₂ move in opposite directions; in metabolic disorders, pH and HCO₃⁻ move in the same direction.

9. Assessment of anion gap (Na⁺ − [Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻]; normal 8–12 mEq/L) is essential in metabolic acidosis: an elevated AG indicates accumulation of unmeasured acids (MUDPILES mnemonic); a normal AG indicates bicarbonate loss or chloride gain.

10. Evidence-based nursing management of fluid and electrolyte disorders requires systematic assessment (history, physical examination, accurate I&O, daily weights, laboratory trends), prompt recognition of life-threatening changes (ECG changes, neurological deterioration, hemodynamic instability), and interprofessional collaboration to identify and treat underlying causes.

## Answer Key

1. **B** — Decreased plasma oncotic pressure. In heart failure combined with hypoalbuminemia (serum albumin 2.1 g/dL), the oncotic pressure that normally holds fluid within the capillary is reduced, allowing fluid to accumulate in the interstitium. While increased capillary hydrostatic pressure (from heart failure/venous congestion) also contributes, the low albumin directly reduces oncotic pressure, making B the best single answer here. Options A, C, and D describe changes that would reduce rather than promote edema.

2. **C** — Respiratory acidosis. pH 7.30 (acidosis) + PaCO₂ 55 (elevated) move in **opposite** directions → ROME = Respiratory. HCO₃⁻ 26 is normal, confirming no metabolic contribution. This is respiratory acidosis, likely acute or partially compensated.

3. **B** — Infuse at 10 mEq/hour with continuous cardiac monitoring. This is the maximum safe rate for peripheral IV potassium administration. Rapid infusion (Options A, C, D) risks cardiac arrest. IV push potassium is never acceptable (Option C is dangerous).

4. **B** — Risk for cerebral edema and seizures. Hyponatremia (Na⁺ 118 mEq/L) causes water to shift into neurons by osmosis, producing cerebral edema. Priority concerns are neurological: headache, nausea, confusion, seizures, and potential brain herniation. This is a medical emergency.

5. **A, B, E** — Positive Chvostek's sign, positive Trousseau's sign, and muscle cramps and paresthesias are all signs of neuromuscular irritability from hypocalcemia. Peaked T waves (C) and shortened QTc (D) are findings of hyperkalemia and hypercalcemia, respectively; hypocalcemia causes **prolonged** QTc.

6. **C** — Chronic respiratory acidosis with renal compensation. pH 7.31 (acidosis), PaCO₂ 62 (elevated) → respiratory acidosis. HCO₃⁻ 30 (elevated) → renal compensation (kidneys retaining bicarbonate). Expected HCO₃⁻ for chronic respiratory acidosis = 24 + 3.5 × (62−45)/10 = 24 + 5.95 ≈ 30 — matches, confirming appropriate chronic compensation.

7. **C** — Metabolic alkalosis. Prolonged vomiting causes loss of hydrochloric acid (HCl) from the stomach, resulting in loss of hydrogen ions. With H⁺ loss, HCO₃⁻ accumulates, raising pH → metabolic alkalosis. Concurrent hypokalemia and volume depletion (chloride-responsive) are typical.

8. **C** — Increased capillary permeability allowing protein leak. Burns cause extensive tissue injury that markedly increases capillary permeability, allowing plasma proteins to leak into the interstitium. This raises interstitial oncotic pressure, drawing fluid out of the vasculature. The concurrent hypoalbuminemia (from protein loss into burn exudate) further reduces plasma oncotic pressure, compounding edema.

9. **C** — Absence of deep tendon reflexes. Loss of deep tendon reflexes (DTRs) is an early sign of magnesium toxicity and is the clinical threshold above which respiratory depression and cardiac toxicity can occur. DTR monitoring is the standard safety check. Serum level of 4 mEq/L (A) is within the therapeutic range for preeclampsia (4–7 mEq/L). Urine output of 35 mL/hour (B) is acceptable. Facial flushing (D) is a benign side effect.

10. **A, B, D** — Peaked T waves, serum potassium 5.8 mEq/L, and widened QRS complex are all consistent with hyperkalemia. Serum K⁺ 2.9 mEq/L (C) indicates hypokalemia. Prominent U waves (E) are characteristic of **hypokalemia**, not hyperkalemia.
