## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| **Ventilation** | The mechanical process of moving air into and out of the lungs, dependent on pressure gradients generated by respiratory muscle contraction |
| **Diffusion** | The passive movement of oxygen and carbon dioxide across the alveolar–capillary membrane along their respective concentration gradients |
| **Perfusion** | The flow of blood through the pulmonary capillaries, which must be matched to alveolar ventilation for effective gas exchange |
| **Hypoxemia** | An abnormally low partial pressure of oxygen in arterial blood (PaO₂ < 80 mmHg); the most common indication for supplemental oxygen therapy |
| **Hypoxia** | Inadequate oxygen delivery to tissues; may result from hypoxemia, anemia, impaired circulation, or impaired cellular oxygen utilization |
| **Hypercapnia** | An abnormally elevated partial pressure of carbon dioxide in arterial blood (PaCO₂ > 45 mmHg); indicates inadequate alveolar ventilation |
| **SpO₂** | Peripheral oxygen saturation measured by pulse oximetry; a noninvasive estimate of arterial oxyhemoglobin saturation |
| **PaO₂** | Partial pressure of oxygen dissolved in arterial blood; measured by arterial blood gas analysis; normal range 80–100 mmHg |
| **PaCO₂** | Partial pressure of carbon dioxide in arterial blood; reflects adequacy of alveolar ventilation; normal range 35–45 mmHg |
| **FiO₂** | Fraction of inspired oxygen; expressed as a decimal (0.21 = 21% atmospheric oxygen); increased FiO₂ is the primary mechanism of oxygen therapy |
| **Low-flow oxygen system** | A delivery device (e.g., nasal cannula, simple mask) that supplements—but does not control—the patient's inspired gas; actual FiO₂ varies with the patient's respiratory rate and tidal volume |
| **High-flow oxygen system** | A delivery device that meets or exceeds the patient's inspiratory flow demand, delivering a controlled FiO₂ independent of breathing pattern |
| **Venturi mask** | A high-flow, fixed-concentration oxygen mask that uses the Bernoulli principle and a color-coded air entrainment adapter to deliver a precise, predetermined FiO₂; also called an air entrainment mask |
| **Air entrainment** | The process by which high-velocity oxygen flow through a narrow jet creates a zone of low pressure that draws in (entrains) a controlled volume of room air, establishing a precise oxygen–air mixture |
| **Nasal cannula** | A low-flow oxygen delivery device consisting of two prongs that fit into the nares; delivers approximately 1–6 L/min, providing an estimated FiO₂ of 0.24–0.44 |
| **Non-rebreather mask** | A low-flow mask with a one-way valve and reservoir bag; designed to deliver near-100% oxygen at 10–15 L/min in emergency situations |
| **High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC)** | A device delivering heated, humidified oxygen at flow rates of 20–60 L/min through large-bore nasal cannulae, allowing precise FiO₂ and reducing work of breathing |
| **Oropharyngeal airway (OPA)** | A curved plastic device inserted through the mouth to maintain an open airway by preventing the tongue from occluding the posterior pharynx; used only in unconscious patients |
| **Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA)** | A soft rubber or plastic tube inserted through the naris to maintain a patent airway; better tolerated than an OPA in semi-conscious patients |
| **Suctioning** | The application of negative pressure to clear secretions from the airway; may be oropharyngeal, nasopharyngeal, or endotracheal (via artificial airway) |
| **Pulse oximetry** | A noninvasive monitoring technique that uses differential light absorption by oxyhemoglobin and deoxyhemoglobin to estimate arterial oxygen saturation (SpO₂) |
| **Arterial blood gas (ABG)** | A laboratory analysis of arterial blood measuring pH, PaO₂, PaCO₂, HCO₃⁻, and base excess; the gold standard for assessing gas exchange and acid-base status |
| **Impaired gas exchange** | A NANDA-I nursing diagnosis describing excess or deficit in oxygenation and/or carbon dioxide elimination at the alveolar–capillary membrane |
| **Ineffective airway clearance** | A NANDA-I nursing diagnosis describing the inability to clear secretions or obstructions from the respiratory tract to maintain a patent airway |
| **Work of breathing** | The effort required by the respiratory muscles to overcome airway resistance and lung–chest wall elastance; elevated in obstructive and restrictive disorders |

## Introduction

Ms. Chen, a 72-year-old woman with a history of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and heart failure, is admitted following two days of worsening dyspnea, productive cough, and decreased activity tolerance. Her SpO₂ on room air is 86%, her respiratory rate is 28 breaths per minute, and she is using accessory muscles visibly. The admitting physician orders "oxygen titrated to maintain SpO₂ 88–92%." The nurse pauses: *Which oxygen delivery device is most appropriate? Why is the target SpO₂ lower than the typical normal range? What are the risks of giving too much—or too little—oxygen to a patient with COPD?*

These questions lie at the heart of clinical oxygenation management, one of the most consequential and nuanced skills in nursing practice. Virtually every acutely ill patient requires some assessment of oxygenation; many require supplemental oxygen; and a significant subset require airway management interventions ranging from positioning to suctioning to artificial airway placement. Errors in oxygen delivery — administering too little in hypoxemic patients, or excessive concentrations in COPD patients who depend on a hypoxic drive — can cause direct patient harm. Understanding the physiology of gas exchange, the properties of each oxygen delivery system, and the principles guiding their selection is therefore a core nursing competency.

This chapter begins with a review of the physiology of ventilation and gas exchange (Section 33.1) and proceeds to systematic assessment of oxygenation (Section 33.2). Section 33.3 covers the major oxygen delivery systems in detail, with particular attention to the Venturi mask — the preferred device when a precise, fixed FiO₂ is required. Section 33.4 describes airway management interventions, including positioning, suctioning, and artificial airways. Section 33.5 integrates this content into nursing care plans for the three primary respiratory nursing diagnoses.

## Section 33.1 — Physiology of Oxygenation: A Clinical Review

### 33.1.1 Ventilation, Diffusion, and Perfusion

Adequate tissue oxygenation requires the successful completion of three linked processes:

**Ventilation** is the bulk movement of air between the atmosphere and the alveoli, driven by the pressure gradient generated when the diaphragm and intercostal muscles contract. Inspiration is active (requires muscular work); expiration is normally passive (elastic recoil). Normal tidal volume is approximately 500 mL; normal respiratory rate is 12–20 breaths per minute. The product of these values, the **minute ventilation** (approximately 6 L/min at rest), must be sufficient to eliminate CO₂ and deliver O₂ to the alveoli.

**Diffusion** describes the passive movement of gases across the alveolar–capillary membrane along their partial pressure gradients. Oxygen moves from alveoli (PO₂ ≈ 104 mmHg) into pulmonary capillary blood (PO₂ ≈ 40 mmHg). Carbon dioxide moves in the opposite direction from capillary blood (PCO₂ ≈ 46 mmHg) into alveolar gas (PCO₂ ≈ 40 mmHg). Diffusion is impaired by membrane thickening (e.g., pulmonary fibrosis), increased diffusion distance (e.g., pulmonary edema), and reduced alveolar surface area (e.g., emphysema).

**Perfusion** refers to blood flow through the pulmonary capillaries. Optimal gas exchange requires close matching of ventilation (V) and perfusion (Q). A **V/Q mismatch** — in which some alveoli are ventilated but not perfused (dead space, as in pulmonary embolism) or perfused but not ventilated (shunt, as in pneumonia or atelectasis) — is the most common cause of clinical hypoxemia.

### 33.1.2 Oxygen Delivery and Cellular Metabolism

Oxygen transported to tissues is bound to hemoglobin (approximately 98%) and dissolved in plasma (approximately 2%). The **oxygen–hemoglobin dissociation curve** illustrates the relationship between PaO₂ and hemoglobin saturation (SaO₂). The curve's sigmoid shape has important clinical implications:

| Region of the Curve | Clinical Implication |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Flat upper portion** (PaO₂ > 60 mmHg) | Large decreases in PaO₂ produce small decreases in SaO₂; SpO₂ ≥ 90% corresponds to PaO₂ ≥ 60 mmHg |
| **Steep lower portion** (PaO₂ < 60 mmHg) | Small decreases in PaO₂ cause large decreases in SaO₂; patients decompensate rapidly |
| **Right shift** (acidosis, hyperthermia, hypercapnia, elevated 2,3-DPG) | Hemoglobin releases O₂ more readily to tissues; beneficial in peripheral tissue hypoxia |
| **Left shift** (alkalosis, hypothermia, hypocapnia, CO poisoning) | Hemoglobin holds O₂ more tightly; may impair tissue oxygen delivery |

**Tissue oxygen delivery** (DO₂) depends on both oxygen saturation and cardiac output: DO₂ = Cardiac Output × (Hgb × 1.34 × SaO₂ + 0.003 × PaO₂). A patient may have a normal SpO₂ but still have inadequate tissue oxygenation if cardiac output or hemoglobin is severely reduced — a critical distinction nurses must recognize.

## Section 33.2 — Assessment of Oxygenation

### 33.2.1 Systematic Respiratory Assessment

A thorough respiratory assessment precedes any intervention. The nurse systematically evaluates:

**Inspection:** The nurse observes respiratory rate, depth, and rhythm and notes any use of accessory muscles (sternocleidomastoid, scalene, or intercostal retractions). Pursed-lip breathing — common in COPD — helps maintain positive airway pressure during exhalation, while paradoxical breathing (inward chest movement during inspiration) signals respiratory muscle fatigue. Chest symmetry and shape are assessed: a barrel chest suggests emphysema, and asymmetry may indicate pneumothorax. Skin and mucous membrane color are inspected for central cyanosis (a marker of severe hypoxemia) or peripheral cyanosis (which may reflect poor perfusion). Nasal flaring and finger clubbing suggest chronic hypoxemia.

**Auscultation:** Listen systematically, comparing side to side, anterior and posterior:

| Breath Sound | Characteristics | Clinical Association |
|-------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Vesicular** | Soft, low-pitched; inspiration longer than expiration | Normal peripheral lung fields |
| **Bronchovesicular** | Moderate pitch; equal inspiration and expiration | Normal over main bronchi |
| **Bronchial** | Loud, high-pitched; expiration longer than inspiration | Normal over trachea; abnormal elsewhere (consolidation) |
| **Crackles (rales)** | Discontinuous, nonmusical; fine (fluid) or coarse (secretions) | Pulmonary edema, pneumonia, atelectasis, fibrosis |
| **Wheezes** | Continuous, musical, high-pitched; usually on expiration | Bronchospasm, asthma, COPD |
| **Rhonchi** | Continuous, low-pitched (sonorous); clear with coughing | Secretions in large airways |
| **Stridor** | High-pitched, inspiratory; audible without stethoscope | Upper airway obstruction (croup, epiglottitis, foreign body) |
| **Pleural friction rub** | Grating or creaking sound in phase with breathing | Pleuritis |
| **Diminished/absent** | Reduced or absent air entry | Pneumothorax, pleural effusion, atelectasis, consolidation |

### 33.2.2 Pulse Oximetry

Pulse oximetry (SpO₂) is the most widely used noninvasive monitor of oxygenation. A probe (finger, toe, earlobe, or forehead) emits two wavelengths of light; the ratio of oxyhemoglobin to deoxyhemoglobin absorption determines the displayed saturation.

**Limitations nurses must recognize:** Motion artifact from shivering or movement can produce false readings; using an alternate probe site or a motion-tolerant device mitigates this. Poor perfusion states — including vasoconstriction, hypothermia, and hypotension — reduce the pulse signal, making earlobe or forehead probes more reliable than finger probes. Dyshemoglobinemias pose a critical limitation: carboxyhemoglobin (present in CO poisoning) reads as oxyhemoglobin on standard pulse oximeters, producing a falsely normal SpO₂ despite severe tissue hypoxia; a co-oximeter from arterial blood is required to detect it. Studies document SpO₂ overestimation in patients with darker skin tones using standard devices, and dark-pigmented nail polish (especially blue, green, or black) also interferes with light transmission; clinical judgment and ABG correlation remain important. Finally, severe anemia can reduce total oxygen-carrying capacity substantially even when SpO₂ is normal.

**Normal SpO₂:** 95–100%. Target SpO₂ 88–92% is appropriate for patients with chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure (type 2 COPD) to avoid suppressing hypoxic respiratory drive.

### 33.2.3 Arterial Blood Gas Interpretation

Arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis provides the definitive assessment of gas exchange and acid-base status.

| Parameter | Normal Range | Significance |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **pH** | 7.35–7.45 | Overall acid-base status; < 7.35 = acidosis; > 7.45 = alkalosis |
| **PaO₂** | 80–100 mmHg | Oxygenation; < 60 mmHg = significant hypoxemia |
| **PaCO₂** | 35–45 mmHg | Adequacy of ventilation; elevated = hypoventilation; decreased = hyperventilation |
| **HCO₃⁻** | 22–26 mEq/L | Metabolic component; elevated = metabolic alkalosis or compensation for respiratory acidosis |
| **SaO₂** | 95–100% | Hemoglobin saturation from direct co-oximetry |
| **Base excess** | −2 to +2 mEq/L | Degree of metabolic deviation from normal |

A systematic approach to ABG interpretation: (1) assess pH; (2) determine primary disorder (respiratory vs. metabolic) by examining PaCO₂ and HCO₃⁻; (3) determine if compensation is present; (4) assess PaO₂ and calculate the A-a gradient if needed.

## Section 33.3 — Oxygen Delivery Systems

### 33.3.1 Indications and Goals of Oxygen Therapy

Supplemental oxygen is indicated when a patient cannot maintain adequate tissue oxygenation from room air (FiO₂ 0.21). Clinical indications include an SpO₂ below 94% (or below 88–92% in patients with COPD-related chronic hypercapnia), a PaO₂ below 60 mmHg on ABG, and acute hypoxemic conditions such as pneumonia, pulmonary edema, pulmonary embolism, and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS). Perioperative and post-procedural hypoxemia, carbon monoxide poisoning (for which 100% O₂ is required regardless of SpO₂), and cardiopulmonary resuscitation are additional indications.

The goal is to achieve adequate tissue oxygenation with the lowest effective FiO₂, thereby minimizing risks of oxygen toxicity, absorption atelectasis, and — in susceptible patients — suppression of hypoxic drive.

### 33.3.2 Low-Flow Delivery Devices

Low-flow systems supplement room air; the patient inhales a mixture of delivered oxygen and entrained room air, so the actual FiO₂ varies with breathing pattern.

**Nasal Cannula:** Delivers 1–6 L/min with an estimated FiO₂ of 0.24–0.44 (approximately +4% per 1 L/min above atmospheric). It is comfortable, allows eating and talking, and is appropriate for mild-to-moderate hypoxemia. The delivered FiO₂ is unreliable with mouth breathing, tachypnea, or high minute ventilation. Nasal mucosa desiccation occurs at flows of 4 L/min or greater, so humidification should be added at those levels. Flows above 6 L/min do not increase FiO₂ reliably and are not recommended.

**Partial Rebreather Mask:** This mask includes a reservoir bag but has no one-way valve between the bag and the mask, allowing the first third of exhaled air (anatomic dead space gas, which is oxygen-rich) to recirculate into the reservoir. It delivers 6–10 L/min with an estimated FiO₂ of 0.40–0.70.

**Non-Rebreather Mask (NRM):** The NRM combines a reservoir bag with one-way valves that prevent rebreathing of exhaled air, delivering 10–15 L/min with an estimated FiO₂ up to 0.80–0.90. It is indicated for emergency management of severe hypoxemia and CO poisoning. The reservoir bag must remain inflated at all times; complete deflation during inspiration signals insufficient flow and requires an immediate increase in the flow rate.

### 33.3.3 Venturi (Air Entrainment) Mask

The **Venturi mask** — also called an **air entrainment mask** — is the device of choice when a precise, fixed FiO₂ is required, regardless of the patient's breathing pattern. It is particularly important in patients with chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure (e.g., COPD exacerbation), in whom uncontrolled high-flow oxygen can suppress the hypoxic drive and cause worsening CO₂ retention and respiratory acidosis.

**Mechanism — The Bernoulli Principle and Air Entrainment:**

The Venturi mask operates on the Bernoulli principle: when a gas accelerates through a narrow constriction (jet orifice), its velocity increases and lateral pressure decreases, creating a zone of relative negative pressure that entrains (draws in) room air through side ports. The ratio of entrained room air to delivered oxygen is determined by the size of the jet orifice and air entrainment ports. Because this ratio is fixed by device geometry, the resulting FiO₂ is constant and independent of the patient's respiratory rate, tidal volume, or minute ventilation — provided the total gas flow (oxygen + entrained air) meets or exceeds the patient's inspiratory flow demand (typically ≥ 30–40 L/min in an adult).

**Color-Coded Adapters:**

Venturi adapters are standardized by color and labeled with both the prescribed FiO₂ and the required oxygen flow rate:

| Adapter Color | FiO₂ Delivered | Required O₂ Flow Rate |
|--------------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Blue** | 0.24 (24%) | 2 L/min |
| **White** | 0.28 (28%) | 4 L/min |
| **Yellow** | 0.35 (35%) | 8 L/min |
| **Red** | 0.40 (40%) | 10 L/min |
| **Green** | 0.60 (60%) | 15 L/min |

> **Clinical Note:** The flow rate printed on the adapter refers to the *oxygen* flow at which that adapter delivers the stated FiO₂. Setting a higher oxygen flow than specified does *not* increase the delivered FiO₂; it only increases total flow, which may actually reduce FiO₂ by altering the entrainment ratio. The nurse must set the flowmeter to exactly the rate specified on the adapter in use.

**Nursing Responsibilities for Venturi Mask Therapy:**

1. **Verify the order** — confirm the prescribed FiO₂ and select the corresponding color-coded adapter.
2. **Set the correct flow rate** — set the oxygen flowmeter to the exact rate printed on the adapter.
3. **Confirm mask fit** — the mask should fit snugly over nose and mouth; gaps reduce delivered FiO₂.
4. **Keep entrainment ports clear** — do not occlude side ports with bed linens or the patient's hand; obstruction disrupts air entrainment and increases FiO₂ unpredictably.
5. **Monitor SpO₂** — titrate to prescribed target (commonly 88–92% for COPD, 94–98% for most other patients).
6. **Assess for CO₂ retention** — monitor for signs of hypercapnia (somnolence, confusion, asterixis, respiratory rate changes); obtain ABG as ordered.
7. **Provide oral care and skin protection** — masks cause pressure on the bridge of the nose and cheeks; apply foam padding as needed and assess skin integrity every 2–4 hours.
8. **Remove for meals** — use nasal cannula at an equivalent or prescribed flow during meal times, noting that FiO₂ accuracy is lost.
9. **Humidification** — unlike nasal cannulae at high flows, Venturi masks at standard flows do not typically require added humidification, as entrained room air provides sufficient ambient moisture.

> **Safety Alert — Venturi Masks and COPD:** In patients with chronic hypercapnic COPD, the goal of oxygen therapy is *normalization of SpO₂ to 88–92%*, not to 95–100%. Delivering excess oxygen to these patients can suppress the hypoxic drive, leading to worsening hypoventilation, CO₂ narcosis, and respiratory arrest. The Venturi mask's ability to deliver a precise, fixed low FiO₂ makes it the preferred device for this population. Always clarify the target SpO₂ range with the prescriber if it is not specified in the order.

### 33.3.4 High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC)

High-flow nasal cannula therapy delivers heated (37 °C), humidified oxygen at flow rates of 20–60 L/min through wide-bore nasal cannulae. HFNC offers several physiological advantages over conventional low-flow devices:

- **Precise FiO₂:** Titrated from 0.21 to 1.0
- **Meets inspiratory demand:** High flow rates exceed typical adult inspiratory flow, preventing room air dilution
- **Positive airway pressure effect:** Creates 1–2 cmH₂O of positive end-expiratory pressure, reducing work of breathing and improving alveolar recruitment
- **Mucociliary clearance:** Heated humidification improves ciliary function and secretion clearance
- **Dead space washout:** High flow flushes CO₂ from the nasopharynx

HFNC is used in acute hypoxemic respiratory failure, post-extubation support, and in patients who cannot tolerate non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (NIPPV). It offers several physiological advantages over conventional low-flow devices: FiO₂ can be titrated precisely from 0.21 to 1.0; the high flow rates meet or exceed typical adult inspiratory demand, preventing room air dilution; a 1–2 cmH₂O positive end-expiratory pressure effect reduces work of breathing and improves alveolar recruitment; heated humidification enhances mucociliary clearance; and high flow washes CO₂ from the nasopharyngeal dead space. Nursing responsibilities include monitoring for increasing work of breathing that may signal HFNC failure and the need for escalation of care.

### 33.3.5 Non-Invasive Positive Pressure Ventilation (NIPPV)

NIPPV delivers positive airway pressure through a tight-fitting mask (nasal, oronasal, or total face), augmenting ventilation without endotracheal intubation. The two main modes are:

- **CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure):** Delivers a single constant pressure throughout the respiratory cycle; used for obstructive sleep apnea and cardiogenic pulmonary edema
- **BiPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure):** Delivers a higher inspiratory pressure (IPAP) and lower expiratory pressure (EPAP); reduces work of breathing and augments tidal volume; used in COPD exacerbations, hypercapnic respiratory failure, and neuromuscular disease

Nursing care priorities include mask fit, skin integrity assessment, monitoring for aspiration risk, and patient tolerance.

### 33.3.6 Oxygen Safety Considerations

> **Safety Alert:** Oxygen is not flammable itself, but it is a powerful **oxidizer** that supports and accelerates combustion of other materials. Strict precautions are required in all areas where oxygen is in use:
> - Post "No Smoking / Oxygen in Use" signage
> - Keep oxygen cylinders secured and away from heat sources
> - Avoid petroleum-based lubricants near oxygen equipment (use water-soluble alternatives)
> - Ground electrical equipment to prevent spark generation
> - Never place oxygen masks or tubing near open flames, cautery, or sparks

## Section 33.4 — Airway Management

### 33.4.1 Positioning for Airway Patency

Positioning is the simplest and first-line intervention for airway management.

**In the unconscious or obtunded patient:**
- **Head-tilt–chin-lift:** Extends the neck and lifts the tongue off the posterior pharynx; contraindicated if cervical spine injury is suspected
- **Jaw thrust:** Displaces the mandible anteriorly without extending the neck; used with suspected cervical spine injury

**Positioning to optimize ventilation:**
- **High Fowler's (60–90°) or semi-Fowler's (30–45°):** Reduces pressure of abdominal contents on the diaphragm, allowing maximal diaphragmatic excursion; the preferred position for most patients with dyspnea
- **Lateral (recovery) position:** Prevents aspiration in unconscious patients without cervical spine injury
- **Prone positioning:** Used in ARDS management to recruit dependent alveoli; requires specialized team training

### 33.4.2 Suctioning

Suctioning clears secretions the patient cannot remove independently. It is not a benign procedure; potential complications include hypoxemia (during suction application), cardiac dysrhythmia, vagal stimulation, mucosal trauma, and infection. Suctioning is performed only when clinically indicated — not on a fixed schedule.

**Indications:** Visible secretions in airway; audible gurgling or rattling; coarse crackles or rhonchi that do not clear with coughing; oxygen desaturation thought to be due to retained secretions; patient's inability to cough effectively.

**Oropharyngeal Suctioning (Yankauer):**
Used to clear secretions from the mouth and oropharynx. The Yankauer suction catheter is rigid; it is appropriate for thick oral secretions. Technique:
1. Explain the procedure; position patient in high Fowler's if possible
2. Preoxygenate if SpO₂ is marginal (apply 100% O₂ via mask for 30–60 seconds)
3. Connect Yankauer to suction; set wall suction to 100–150 mmHg (adult)
4. Insert catheter into oral cavity; apply suction intermittently while moving catheter; limit each suction pass to ≤ 15 seconds
5. Rinse catheter with water between passes; re-assess respiratory status

**Nasopharyngeal Suctioning:**
Used when oral suctioning is insufficient or poorly tolerated. A soft flexible catheter is inserted through the naris into the nasopharynx or hypopharynx. Technique is similar to oropharyngeal suctioning; catheter size is approximately (patient's height in inches ÷ 2 – 2) in centimeters for insertion depth. Contraindicated with suspected basal skull fracture, anticoagulation, nasal polyps, or epiglottitis.

**Endotracheal/Tracheostomy Suctioning:**
Performed for patients with artificial airways (endotracheal tube or tracheostomy). Requires sterile technique (closed or open suction system). Preoxygenation with 100% O₂ before and after each pass is standard practice.

### 33.4.3 Oropharyngeal and Nasopharyngeal Airways

**Oropharyngeal Airway (OPA):**
Indicated in unconscious patients without a gag reflex to maintain airway patency. Correctly sized by measuring from the corner of the mouth to the earlobe or the center of the lips to the angle of the jaw.

*Insertion technique:* Insert with the curve pointing toward the palate, then rotate 180° as it passes the hard palate so the curve follows the tongue into position. Reassess after insertion; improper placement can worsen obstruction.

**Contraindication:** Intact gag reflex (will cause vomiting and aspiration).

**Nasopharyngeal Airway (NPA):**
Tolerated in semi-conscious patients with intact gag reflex. Size selected based on diameter of the naris (little finger approximation) and length (naris to tragus). Lubricate with water-soluble lubricant before insertion. Flange must remain outside the naris to prevent aspiration of the device.

### 33.4.4 Endotracheal Intubation and Mechanical Ventilation (Overview)

Endotracheal intubation is performed by a physician, advanced practice provider, or respiratory therapist; it is a registered nursing competency to assist with intubation and to maintain the endotracheal tube (ETT) after placement. Nursing responsibilities include:

- Confirming tube placement: auscultate bilateral breath sounds immediately after intubation; confirm with end-tidal CO₂ (capnography) and chest radiograph
- Securing the ETT and documenting cm marking at the teeth or lips
- Maintaining appropriate cuff pressure (20–30 cmH₂O) to prevent aspiration and tracheal mucosal ischemia
- Assessing for self-extubation risk; using limb restraints only when clinically necessary and per institutional protocol
- Performing oral care per VAP (ventilator-associated pneumonia) prevention bundle: chlorhexidine rinse, head-of-bed elevation 30–45°, daily sedation interruption, and daily assessment for readiness to extubate
- Suctioning the endotracheal tube using sterile technique as described above

## Section 33.5 — Nursing Process: Care of the Patient with Altered Oxygenation

### 33.5.1 NANDA-I Nursing Diagnoses

Three nursing diagnoses most commonly apply to patients with oxygenation impairment:

1. **Impaired gas exchange** (related to V/Q mismatch, diffusion impairment, or hypoventilation; evidenced by abnormal ABG values, hypoxemia, hypercapnia, altered mental status)
2. **Ineffective airway clearance** (related to retained secretions, bronchospasm, or impaired cough; evidenced by abnormal breath sounds, dyspnea, inability to clear secretions)
3. **Ineffective breathing pattern** (related to pain, neuromuscular impairment, or altered level of consciousness; evidenced by tachypnea, use of accessory muscles, paradoxical breathing)

### 33.5.2 Care Planning

**For Impaired Gas Exchange:**

| Nursing Intervention | Rationale |
|---------------------|-----------|
| Apply prescribed oxygen delivery device at correct FiO₂ | Corrects hypoxemia; Venturi mask ensures precise FiO₂ in COPD |
| Monitor SpO₂ continuously or per frequency ordered | Detects deterioration; guides titration to target range |
| Obtain ABG as ordered; report abnormal values promptly | Confirms and quantifies gas exchange disturbance |
| Position patient in high Fowler's or semi-Fowler's | Maximizes diaphragmatic excursion |
| Administer bronchodilators and corticosteroids as prescribed | Reduces bronchospasm and inflammation in obstructive disease |
| Minimize activity during acute phase; schedule rest periods | Reduces oxygen demand |
| Educate patient to report increased dyspnea promptly | Early reporting enables timely intervention |

**For Ineffective Airway Clearance:**

| Nursing Intervention | Rationale |
|---------------------|-----------|
| Encourage and assist with deep breathing and coughing | Mobilizes secretions toward the oropharynx |
| Perform incentive spirometry hourly while awake | Prevents atelectasis by sustaining alveolar expansion |
| Provide adequate hydration (unless contraindicated) | Reduces secretion viscosity, facilitating expectoration |
| Suction as indicated (not routinely) | Clears secretions the patient cannot mobilize independently |
| Position with head elevated; turn every 2 hours | Gravity-assisted secretion drainage; prevents stasis |
| Administer mucolytics as ordered (e.g., acetylcysteine) | Reduces disulfide bonds in mucus, decreasing viscosity |
| Chest physiotherapy (CPT) where indicated | Percussive vibration mobilizes secretions in large airways |

### 33.5.3 Evaluation

Evaluate the effectiveness of interventions by assessing:
- SpO₂ within prescribed target range
- Respiratory rate, depth, and effort returned toward normal
- Breath sounds clear or improved
- Patient reports decreased dyspnea
- ABG values within acceptable range for the patient's clinical context
- Secretions reduced or patient able to clear independently

Document findings and responses to interventions systematically. Report deterioration — rising respiratory rate, declining SpO₂ despite therapy, new onset of altered mental status — immediately to the responsible provider, as these may signal respiratory failure requiring escalation of care.

## Chapter Summary

1. **Gas exchange** depends on the integration of ventilation (air movement), diffusion (gas movement across the alveolar–capillary membrane), and perfusion (blood flow through pulmonary capillaries). V/Q mismatch is the most common cause of clinical hypoxemia.

2. **Respiratory assessment** is systematic and includes inspection, auscultation, pulse oximetry, and arterial blood gas interpretation. Nurses must understand the limitations of pulse oximetry — including its inability to detect CO poisoning, inaccuracy in poor perfusion states, and potential overestimation in patients with darker skin tones.

3. **Oxygen delivery systems** are classified as low-flow (variable FiO₂) or high-flow (fixed FiO₂). Selection is based on the required FiO₂, the need for precision, and the patient's clinical condition.

4. **The Venturi (air entrainment) mask** delivers a precise, fixed FiO₂ using the Bernoulli principle to entrain a controlled ratio of room air. Color-coded adapters specify both the FiO₂ and the required oxygen flow rate. It is the preferred device for patients with COPD and chronic hypercapnia who require a specific, low FiO₂ to prevent suppression of hypoxic drive.

5. **High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC)** delivers heated, humidified oxygen at 20–60 L/min, providing precise FiO₂, meeting inspiratory demand, reducing work of breathing, and washing out nasopharyngeal dead space. It is used in acute hypoxemic respiratory failure.

6. **Airway management** spans a continuum from positioning and suctioning through artificial airway insertion and endotracheal intubation. Suctioning is performed only when clinically indicated; pre-oxygenation and limiting suction application to ≤ 15 seconds are key safety principles.

7. **Nursing diagnoses** most common in patients with oxygenation impairment include impaired gas exchange, ineffective airway clearance, and ineffective breathing pattern. Nursing interventions are evidence-based and must be evaluated systematically against defined, measurable outcomes.

## NCLEX-Style Review Questions

**1.** A nurse is caring for a patient with a COPD exacerbation. The physician orders oxygen at FiO₂ 0.28 via Venturi mask. Which of the following actions by the nurse is correct?

A. Select the white (28%) adapter and set the flowmeter to 4 L/min  
B. Select the yellow (35%) adapter and set the flowmeter to 8 L/min  
C. Select the white (28%) adapter and set the flowmeter to 8 L/min  
D. Select the blue (24%) adapter and set the flowmeter to 2 L/min  

**Answer: A.** The white adapter delivers 28% (FiO₂ 0.28) at the specified flow rate of 4 L/min. Using a higher flow rate with the 28% adapter would not increase the FiO₂ because it is fixed by the air entrainment ratio; it would only increase total flow.

---

**2.** A nurse is applying a Venturi mask to a patient. Which finding requires immediate nursing intervention?

A. The patient's SpO₂ is 91% with the 24% adapter in place  
B. The patient's bed linen is covering the side air entrainment ports of the mask  
C. The patient is breathing at a rate of 22 breaths per minute  
D. The patient requests to remove the mask briefly to drink water  

**Answer: B.** Occluding the entrainment ports disrupts the air entrainment ratio, causing the FiO₂ to rise unpredictably above the prescribed level — a critical concern in COPD patients requiring precise low-concentration oxygen. Option A represents an expected SpO₂ within the target range for COPD. Option C is elevated but not directly attributable to the mask. Option D is managed by temporarily using nasal cannula.

---

**3.** A nurse is interpreting the following ABG results for a patient with acute pneumonia: pH 7.32, PaCO₂ 50 mmHg, PaO₂ 58 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 24 mEq/L. Which interpretation is correct?

A. Respiratory alkalosis with hypoxemia  
B. Metabolic acidosis with normal oxygenation  
C. Respiratory acidosis with hypoxemia  
D. Combined respiratory and metabolic acidosis  

**Answer: C.** pH 7.32 (acidotic) with elevated PaCO₂ 50 mmHg (primary respiratory acidosis) and normal HCO₃⁻ 24 mEq/L (no metabolic compensation yet, indicating acute process). PaO₂ 58 mmHg is below 60 mmHg, confirming hypoxemia.

---

**4.** A patient's SpO₂ reads 99% but arterial blood gases reveal a PaO₂ of 62 mmHg and marked elevation in carboxyhemoglobin. What is the most likely explanation for this discrepancy?

A. The pulse oximeter probe was placed incorrectly  
B. Standard pulse oximeters cannot distinguish carboxyhemoglobin from oxyhemoglobin  
C. The ABG machine is inaccurate  
D. Pulse oximetry is always more accurate than ABG for oxygenation assessment  

**Answer: B.** Standard pulse oximeters use only two wavelengths of light and cannot differentiate carboxyhemoglobin (COHgb) from oxyhemoglobin. COHgb binds hemoglobin with high affinity and reads as saturated on the oximeter, causing falsely normal SpO₂ despite inadequate oxygen delivery. A co-oximeter is required to detect COHgb.

---

**5.** The nurse is preparing to suction a postoperative patient who is unable to cough effectively. Which action should the nurse take first?

A. Insert the suction catheter into the nasopharynx  
B. Set wall suction to 200 mmHg  
C. Preoxygenate the patient with supplemental oxygen  
D. Position the patient supine  

**Answer: C.** Preoxygenation minimizes the risk of hypoxemia during suctioning. Suctioning temporarily reduces the patient's effective ventilation. Wall suction for adult nasopharyngeal suctioning should be set to 100–150 mmHg, not 200 mmHg. High Fowler's is the preferred position, not supine.

**6.** Which of the following patients would benefit MOST from a Venturi mask rather than a nasal cannula?

A. A 45-year-old with asthma requiring 2 L/min oxygen to maintain SpO₂ 96%  
B. A 68-year-old with COPD requiring precise 28% FiO₂ to maintain SpO₂ 89–91%  
C. A 30-year-old postoperative patient requiring 4 L/min oxygen to maintain SpO₂ 95%  
D. A 55-year-old with anemia requiring supplemental oxygen during ambulation  

**Answer: B.** The Venturi mask is specifically indicated when a precise, fixed FiO₂ is required. The COPD patient with chronic hypercapnia requires a carefully controlled FiO₂ to maintain adequate — but not excessive — oxygenation and avoid suppressing hypoxic drive. The other patients can be managed effectively with nasal cannula.

**7.** A nurse is caring for an unconscious patient following a seizure. The patient is breathing spontaneously with audible gurgling respirations. Which action is the priority?

A. Insert an oropharyngeal airway  
B. Apply a Venturi mask at FiO₂ 0.35  
C. Position the patient in the lateral recovery position and suction the oropharynx  
D. Prepare for emergent endotracheal intubation  

**Answer: C.** Gurgling respirations indicate secretions or fluid in the upper airway. The immediate priority is to clear the airway and position the patient laterally to prevent aspiration while protecting the airway. An OPA may be inserted after clearing the oropharynx if the gag reflex is absent. Endotracheal intubation may be required if the airway cannot be maintained, but less invasive measures are attempted first.

**8.** Which of the following is a TRUE statement about the Venturi mask?

A. Increasing the oxygen flow above the adapter's specified rate increases the delivered FiO₂  
B. The FiO₂ delivered varies with the patient's respiratory rate  
C. The color-coded adapter determines both the FiO₂ and the required oxygen flow rate  
D. The Venturi mask must always include heated humidification  

**Answer: C.** Each color-coded adapter specifies the FiO₂ delivered and the exact oxygen flow rate required to achieve it. Option A is false — excess flow above the specified rate does not increase FiO₂ (which is fixed by the air entrainment ratio) and may dilute the mixture. Option B is false — the Venturi mask's fixed entrainment ratio makes FiO₂ independent of breathing pattern. Option D is false — humidification is not routinely required with Venturi masks.

**9.** A patient on a non-rebreather mask at 12 L/min has a reservoir bag that is deflating completely during inspiration. What action should the nurse take?

A. Reduce the oxygen flow to 8 L/min  
B. Increase the oxygen flow to maintain the reservoir at least one-third full  
C. Switch to a Venturi mask at FiO₂ 0.40  
D. Document the finding and continue monitoring  

**Answer: B.** The reservoir bag on a non-rebreather mask must remain at least one-third to one-half full during inspiration to provide an adequate oxygen reserve. Complete deflation indicates insufficient flow; the nurse should increase the flow rate. Reducing the flow would worsen the problem.

**10.** A nurse is caring for a patient receiving high-flow nasal cannula (HFNC) at 40 L/min and FiO₂ 0.60. Which assessment finding should prompt the nurse to notify the provider immediately?

A. SpO₂ 94% with respiratory rate 18 breaths per minute  
B. Patient reports nasal dryness despite humidification  
C. Respiratory rate increasing from 22 to 32 breaths per minute with accessory muscle use  
D. Patient prefers to have the HFNC flow reduced slightly for comfort  

**Answer: C.** A rising respiratory rate with accessory muscle use on maximum or near-maximum HFNC settings signals impending respiratory failure and the need for escalation to non-invasive or invasive ventilation. This is an urgent finding requiring immediate provider notification. The other findings require nursing management but are not immediately life-threatening.

## Extended Case Study: Ms. Chen

### Scene 1 — Initial Assessment and Oxygen Selection

Ms. Chen is a 72-year-old woman with COPD (FEV₁/FVC 0.45 on last spirometry), heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (EF 35%), and type 2 diabetes. She presents with two days of worsening dyspnea, a productive cough of yellow-green sputum, and SpO₂ 86% on room air. Respiratory rate is 28/min; she is using sternocleidomastoid muscles with inspiration. Auscultation reveals decreased breath sounds bilaterally at the bases with coarse crackles to the mid-zones and expiratory wheezes throughout. ABG: pH 7.34, PaCO₂ 54 mmHg, PaO₂ 52 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 28 mEq/L (elevated bicarbonate consistent with chronic compensation). The admitting physician orders: "O₂ via Venturi mask, target SpO₂ 88–92%. FiO₂ 24% to start; titrate per protocol."

**Discussion Questions:**
1. Why did the physician order a Venturi mask rather than nasal cannula or a non-rebreather mask for Ms. Chen? What features of her history and ABG results justify this choice?
2. What ABG pattern do you recognize? Classify the acid-base disorder and explain what the elevated HCO₃⁻ indicates about the chronicity of Ms. Chen's hypercapnia.
3. Which adapter would you select and at what flowmeter setting? What would you do if Ms. Chen's SpO₂ remains at 86% after 30 minutes on this setting?

### Scene 2 — Twelve Hours Later: Worsening and Escalation

Twelve hours after admission, Ms. Chen has received nebulized bronchodilators, IV methylprednisolone, and IV ceftriaxone. However, her SpO₂ has decreased to 84% despite a Venturi mask at FiO₂ 0.35 (yellow adapter, 8 L/min). Her respiratory rate is 34/min; she is increasingly somnolent and her use of accessory muscles has worsened. Repeat ABG: pH 7.26, PaCO₂ 68 mmHg, PaO₂ 50 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 29 mEq/L. The team is considering escalation to non-invasive positive pressure ventilation (BiPAP).

**Discussion Questions:**
1. Analyze the repeat ABG. What has changed from the admission ABG and what does it indicate about Ms. Chen's trajectory?
2. Why would BiPAP be preferred over endotracheal intubation as the next step in this patient? What nursing responsibilities are associated with BiPAP initiation?
3. Using SBAR format, communicate Ms. Chen's clinical change to the on-call physician.

### Scene 3 — Recovery and Discharge Planning

After 48 hours of BiPAP, antibiotics, and corticosteroids, Ms. Chen's ABG has improved to: pH 7.38, PaCO₂ 48 mmHg, PaO₂ 68 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 27 mEq/L. SpO₂ is 91% on Venturi mask FiO₂ 0.24. She is alert, comfortable, and able to converse. The team plans to discharge her home with long-term oxygen therapy at 2 L/min via nasal cannula, prescribed after a formal outpatient titration study.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. What teaching does Ms. Chen require regarding her home oxygen prescription? Include safe use, fire prevention, and activity guidelines.
2. The pulmonologist documents that the target SpO₂ at home is 88–92%. How would you explain to Ms. Chen and her family why this target is lower than the 95–100% they may expect?
3. What NANDA-I nursing diagnosis(es) remain applicable at the time of discharge, and what discharge-focused outcomes would you establish?
