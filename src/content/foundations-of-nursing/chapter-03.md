## Introduction

On a busy medical-surgical unit, a 67-year-old patient named Mr. Alvarez is scheduled for a laparoscopic cholecystectomy. His surgeon stopped by early that morning, spoke with him for fewer than five minutes, and left a consent form for him to sign. The night nurse, Jenna, is about to witness the signature when Mr. Alvarez quietly tells her, "I'm not sure I understand what they're going to do to me. The doctor spoke so fast." Jenna pauses. In that moment, she faces a question that lies at the intersection of law, ethics, and professional nursing practice: What is her obligation to this patient?

The answer is not found solely in policy manuals or legal statutes, though both are important. It is found in the foundational understanding that patients are not passive recipients of care — they are autonomous individuals with rights, preferences, and dignity that nursing must actively protect. **Patient advocacy** is not a supplemental nursing skill; it is central to the profession's identity and ethical mandate.

This chapter builds on the legal and ethical frameworks established in Chapter 2 to examine the rights of patients in the health care system and the nurse's role in upholding those rights. Beginning with the historical and legal evolution of the Patient Bill of Rights, the chapter moves through the theory and practice of informed consent, explores the complexities of decision-making capacity and advance care planning, and addresses the critical obligations of confidentiality and privacy under federal law. Special attention is given to vulnerable populations — minors, cognitively impaired patients, prisoners, and others — whose rights require heightened vigilance. The chapter concludes with a framework for navigating the ethical conflicts that inevitably arise when patient autonomy collides with clinical judgment or institutional pressures. Together, these topics equip the nurse to be not merely a caregiver, but a genuine advocate for each patient's rights and dignity.

## Section 3.1 — The Patient Bill of Rights and Legal Foundations

### 3.1.1 Historical Development

The formal recognition of patient rights in the United States emerged gradually from multiple converging forces: the civil rights movement of the 1960s, growing consumer advocacy, and high-profile ethical failures that exposed patients to exploitation and neglect. Prior to this era, the prevailing ethic of medicine was deeply **paternalistic** — physicians and institutions were presumed to know what was best for patients, and patient participation in decision-making was minimal.

A pivotal turning point came in 1972 when the American Hospital Association (AHA) published the first *Patient's Bill of Rights*, a twelve-point document affirming patients' rights to information, privacy, and respectful care. Although not legally binding, the document signaled a cultural shift in the patient-provider relationship. Two years earlier, in 1970, the National Welfare Rights Organization had demanded a patient bill of rights from hospitals serving low-income communities, reflecting how civil rights activism directly shaped health care policy (Annas, 2004).

The legal landscape shifted more dramatically with the passage of federal and state legislation in subsequent decades. The Patient Self-Determination Act (PSDA) of 1990 required all health care facilities receiving Medicare or Medicaid funding to inform patients of their right to make health care decisions, including the right to formulate advance directives. The Affordable Care Act of 2010 incorporated a Patient's Bill of Rights specifically addressing insurance coverage, including protections against arbitrary coverage denials and the right to appeal insurance decisions.

> **Historical Spotlight: The Willowbrook Studies**  
> Between 1956 and 1970, researchers at the Willowbrook State School in New York intentionally infected children with intellectual disabilities with hepatitis to study the disease's progression. Parents were coerced into consenting. The subsequent public outrage contributed directly to federal regulations governing human subjects research and accelerated the patient rights movement. This case illustrates what is possible when patient rights are unprotected by law or professional advocacy (Robinson & Unruh, 2008).

### 3.1.2 The Current Legal Framework

Today, the rights of patients are codified across multiple legal instruments: federal statutes, state laws, accreditation standards, and institutional policies. The **Patient Self-Determination Act (1990)** requires Medicare- and Medicaid-participating institutions to provide written information about patients' rights to make health care decisions and to document advance directives in medical records. The **Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (1996)** establishes privacy and security standards for protected health information (discussed in depth in Section 3.6). **Joint Commission Standards** require accredited hospitals to maintain a written statement of patient rights, which must be communicated to patients at the time of admission. Additionally, **state law** plays a significant role, as individual states maintain statutes governing informed consent, advance directives, and surrogate decision-making, which may extend or supplement federal protections.

The AHA updated its Patient's Bill of Rights in 2003 under the title "The Patient Care Partnership," which sets expectations for patients regarding their hospital stay, including the right to high-quality care, a clean environment, privacy, help with discharge planning, and assistance with billing (American Hospital Association, 2003).

### 3.1.3 Rights in Practice: What Patients Are Entitled To

Across all sources, certain rights appear consistently and are considered fundamental to ethical nursing practice. Patients have the right to **receive information** about their diagnosis, treatment options, and prognosis in language they can understand, and to **participate in decisions** about their own care, including the right to consent to or refuse treatment. They are entitled to **privacy and confidentiality** regarding their health information, as well as **respectful, non-discriminatory care** regardless of race, ethnicity, religion, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, or gender identity. Furthermore, patients have the right to **access their medical records** within the timeframes established by HIPAA, and to **file a complaint** without fear of retaliation or adverse change in their care.

For nurses, these rights translate into concrete professional obligations: explaining procedures, ensuring interpreters are available, protecting the integrity of the medical record, and advocating when rights are violated. The nurse is frequently the patient's most consistent and accessible point of contact in the health care system, making the nurse's advocacy role uniquely powerful.

> **Legal Spotlight: The Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act (EMTALA)**  
> EMTALA (1986) requires hospital emergency departments to screen and stabilize any patient who presents, regardless of ability to pay. This law protects the right to emergency care — a right that nurses must understand when a patient presents in extremis and a provider is reluctant to treat. Nurses have a professional and legal obligation to ensure EMTALA compliance and to escalate concerns when patients are turned away inappropriately (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services, 2012).

## Section 3.2 — The Nurse's Role as Patient Advocate

### 3.2.1 Defining Patient Advocacy

**Patient advocacy** is defined by the American Nurses Association (2015a) as the nurse's protection of the patient's human and legal rights and the representation of the patient's interests to the health care team. Unlike many professional functions, advocacy is not confined to a procedure or a task — it is a stance, a disposition, and an ongoing commitment embedded in every patient interaction.

Advocacy can be understood at multiple levels. At the **individual level**, advocacy means ensuring a specific patient understands their treatment options, has their pain addressed, or has their cultural or religious preferences respected. At the **institutional level**, advocacy involves participating in policy development, quality committees, and shared governance structures that shape how patient rights are protected systemically. At the **societal level**, nurses advocate through political engagement, professional organizations, and community education to advance health equity and patient protections broadly (Bu & Jezewski, 2007).

### 3.2.2 Advocacy as Ethical Imperative

The ANA *Code of Ethics for Nurses* (2015a) explicitly frames advocacy as a professional obligation. Provision 3 states that "the nurse promotes, advocates for, and protects the rights, health, and safety of the patient." This framing positions advocacy not as a supererogatory virtue but as a minimum ethical standard — something nurses must do, not merely something exemplary nurses choose to do.

The ethical foundation of advocacy draws from multiple frameworks discussed in Chapter 2. The principle of **autonomy** — respect for the patient's right to self-determination — is perhaps the most direct mandate for advocacy. When a patient cannot speak for themselves, is intimidated by authority, or lacks the knowledge to navigate the health care system, the nurse becomes the voice through which autonomy is exercised.

The principle of **non-maleficence** also supports advocacy. When the nurse witnesses a care practice that poses harm, silence is complicity. Advocacy requires the courage to speak — to the bedside provider, to the charge nurse, to the supervisor, or through a formal incident report system — whenever patient safety or rights are at risk.

> **Clinical Tip: Recognizing Situations That Require Advocacy**  
> Common situations demanding nursing advocacy include: a patient who says "I don't understand what's happening to me"; a patient who is being pressured to consent; a patient whose pain is undertreated; a patient whose cultural or religious needs are dismissed; and a patient whose family is making decisions that conflict with the patient's stated wishes. Advocacy begins with attentiveness — the nurse who notices is the nurse who acts.

### 3.2.3 Barriers to Advocacy and How to Overcome Them

Despite its centrality to nursing ethics, advocacy is not always easy to practice. Nurses face real barriers: hierarchical workplace cultures that discourage speaking up, fear of retaliation, moral distress from repeated ethical violations, time constraints, and uncertainty about professional authority. Research identifies the power differential between nurses and physicians as one of the most significant structural barriers to effective advocacy (Hanks, 2010).

Nurses can employ several strategies to overcome these barriers. **Developing assertive communication skills** is essential — using "I" statements, focusing on patient welfare, and employing scripted phrases such as "I have a concern about this patient's safety that I need to raise." **Leveraging institutional structures** is equally important, as ethics committees, patient representatives, and rapid response teams provide formal channels for escalating concerns. In addition, **maintaining clinical knowledge** ensures that a nurse's advocacy is most persuasive when grounded in clinical evidence and clearly articulated. **Building collegial relationships** helps as well — nurses who establish trust with physicians and advanced practice providers are better positioned to raise concerns without triggering defensiveness. Finally, **documenting thoroughly** through objective, factual documentation protects both the patient and the nurse and creates a legal record of advocacy efforts.

### 3.2.4 Cultural Dimensions of Advocacy

Effective advocacy requires the nurse to understand that patient preferences are shaped by culture, family structure, and past experiences with health care. In many cultures, medical decisions are made collectively by the family rather than by the individual patient — a practice that may conflict with the Western bioethical emphasis on individual autonomy. The culturally informed advocate does not impose a single model of decision-making but works to understand the patient's values and facilitate a process that honors those values within the legal framework.

Interpreters are essential advocates in their own right. Federal law (Title VI of the Civil Rights Act) requires health care facilities receiving federal funding to provide language access services. Nurses must ensure that professional interpreters — not family members — are used for clinical conversations involving consent, diagnosis, or significant care decisions, both to protect accuracy and to prevent the burdening of family members with the role of delivering difficult information.

## Section 3.3 — Informed Consent: Principles and Process

### 3.3.1 The Doctrine of Informed Consent

**Informed consent** is both a legal requirement and an ethical cornerstone of health care practice. Its legal roots trace to the landmark 1914 case *Schloendorff v. Society of New York Hospital*, in which Justice Benjamin Cardozo wrote: "Every human being of adult years and sound mind has a right to determine what shall be done with his own body." This declaration established the patient's right to refuse treatment as a foundational principle of American law.

Informed consent requires three essential elements:

1. **Disclosure:** The patient must receive adequate information about the proposed treatment or procedure, including its nature, purpose, risks, benefits, alternatives (including the option to decline), and expected outcomes.
2. **Comprehension:** The information must be provided in language the patient understands. Jargon, rushed delivery, and failure to assess understanding render consent ethically and legally invalid.
3. **Voluntariness:** Consent must be given freely, without coercion, manipulation, or undue influence. Patients must understand they retain the right to refuse without penalty to their care.

> **EBP Connection: The "Teach-Back" Method and Informed Consent**  
> Research demonstrates that patients retain as little as 20% of verbal information provided during medical encounters (Kessels, 2003). The teach-back method — asking patients to explain what they have been told in their own words — significantly improves comprehension and is strongly associated with higher quality informed consent. Nurses play a critical role in verifying patient understanding before witnessing consent signatures.

### 3.3.2 Who Obtains Informed Consent?

The legal responsibility for obtaining informed consent rests with the **physician or licensed independent practitioner** who will perform the procedure. This is a point of significant confusion in nursing practice. Nurses do not obtain surgical or procedural consent — they *witness* the signing of the consent document after consent has been appropriately obtained.

However, the nurse's role is far from passive. The nurse is responsible for verifying that the consent form is complete and that the procedure described matches the planned procedure. The nurse must also confirm that the patient was given adequate information prior to signing and assess whether the patient demonstrates understanding and is signing voluntarily. If any concerns exist about the validity of consent, the nurse is responsible for notifying the physician or charge nurse before the procedure proceeds. Throughout this process, the nurse must document all assessment findings related to the consent process.

If a nurse witnesses a consent that appears to have been obtained without adequate disclosure or under coercion, the nurse has a professional and ethical obligation to stop the process and escalate concerns. This may require significant courage, but it is precisely the kind of advocacy the Code of Ethics requires.

### 3.3.3 Special Forms of Consent

Several clinical scenarios involve consent processes that differ from the standard elective procedure. In situations involving **emergency consent**, when a patient is incapacitated and a life-threatening emergency exists, treatment may proceed without consent under the doctrine of **implied consent** — it is presumed that a reasonable person would consent to life-saving treatment; this exception is narrow and does not apply to non-urgent procedures. **Research consent** requires a separate, more rigorous process governed by federal regulations (the "Common Rule," 45 CFR §46) and overseen by Institutional Review Boards (IRBs), with key additional protections including the right to withdraw at any time without penalty. Furthermore, **photography and video consent** must be obtained separately for any photography or video recording of patient care, even for educational purposes. Finally, when Jehovah's Witness patients invoke **blood transfusion refusal** on religious grounds, a competent adult's refusal must be honored even if the clinical team believes it is medically inadvisable (see Section 3.8 for ethical analysis).

### 3.3.4 Documentation of Consent

Proper documentation of the informed consent process is both a legal protection and a communication tool. Nursing documentation should include the name of the person who provided the patient with information and the patient's stated level of understanding, using direct quotes when possible. Any interpreter services used should be recorded, along with the patient's signature, date, and time, and the nurse witness's signature. Additionally, any concerns noted during the process and the actions taken in response must be documented.

Documentation should reflect the process, not merely the outcome. A chart entry reading "consent form signed" communicates little about whether consent was valid. A chart entry reading "Patient verbalized understanding of laparoscopic cholecystectomy procedure, risks including bile duct injury and infection, and right to refuse; interpreter services not required; consent form signed in the presence of the nurse witness" creates a meaningful clinical and legal record.

## Section 3.4 — Capacity, Competence, and Decision-Making

### 3.4.1 Distinguishing Capacity from Competence

Two terms that are frequently confused — and the distinction matters enormously in clinical practice — are **decision-making capacity** and **legal competence**. Understanding the difference protects both the patient's autonomy and the nurse's legal standing.

**Decision-making capacity** is a *clinical* determination made by a qualified health care provider (in most states, a physician or psychologist) at a specific point in time regarding a specific decision. A patient may have capacity to decide whether to refuse a medication but lack capacity to consent to a complex surgical procedure. Capacity is decision-specific, time-specific, and can fluctuate — a patient with delirium may lack capacity in the morning and regain it by afternoon.

**Legal competence** is a *judicial* determination — a court ruling that permanently affects a person's legal standing to make decisions. Very few patients in clinical settings have had their competence adjudicated by a court. The practical implication is clear: most patients you encounter, even those with cognitive impairment, psychiatric illness, or intellectual disabilities, retain legal competence until a court rules otherwise. Nurses must not assume that a patient "lacks competence" based on diagnosis alone.

### 3.4.2 Assessing Decision-Making Capacity

The clinical assessment of decision-making capacity evaluates four key functional abilities (Appelbaum, 2007):

1. **Understanding:** Can the patient comprehend the relevant information? (e.g., "Can you tell me what the procedure involves?")
2. **Appreciation:** Does the patient appreciate that the information applies to their own situation? (e.g., "What do you think will happen if you refuse?")
3. **Reasoning:** Can the patient engage in a reasoning process and weigh options? (e.g., "Can you walk me through how you made this decision?")
4. **Expression of a consistent choice:** Can the patient communicate and sustain a decision?

Nurses are not formally responsible for making legal capacity determinations, but they are often the first to identify that a patient's decision-making appears impaired. Nursing assessment findings — documenting a patient's ability to follow instructions, recall recent events, or engage coherently in conversation — contribute to the capacity evaluation.

> **Safety Alert: Cognitive Impairment and Consent**  
> Never assume a patient with dementia, intellectual disability, or mental illness lacks capacity without a formal clinical evaluation. Conversely, never allow urgency or convenience to substitute for a proper assessment. Proceeding with a significant procedure without adequate capacity evaluation exposes the patient to rights violations and the facility to legal liability. When in doubt, consult the attending physician and, if appropriate, the ethics committee.

### 3.4.3 Surrogate Decision-Making

When a patient lacks decision-making capacity and has not designated a health care agent, the health care team must identify a **surrogate decision-maker** — an individual authorized by law or by the patient's relationships to make decisions on the patient's behalf. Most states establish a statutory hierarchy of surrogates: typically, legal spouse or domestic partner, adult children, parents, adult siblings, and then other close relatives.

Surrogate decision-makers are ethically and legally required to apply one of two standards. The **substituted judgment standard** directs the surrogate to make the decision the patient would have made, based on knowledge of the patient's values, beliefs, and past statements; this standard is preferred when the patient's preferences are known or can be inferred. When the patient's preferences are unknown, the surrogate instead applies the **best interest standard**, making the decision that best promotes the patient's welfare and minimizes harm — a fallback approach, not a first resort.

Nurses frequently interact with surrogates and can support them by providing clear clinical information, acknowledging the burden of surrogate decision-making, and gently redirecting conversations toward "What would your loved one want?" rather than "What do you want for your loved one?" — a shift in framing that can meaningfully realign discussions with the substituted judgment standard.

## Section 3.5 — Advance Directives and End-of-Life Preferences

### 3.5.1 Types of Advance Directives

**Advance directives** are legal documents through which competent adults express their health care preferences in anticipation of future incapacity. The Patient Self-Determination Act (1990) mandates that health care facilities inform patients of their right to complete advance directives at the time of admission. Despite this requirement, research consistently shows that fewer than 30% of Americans have completed an advance directive (Yadav et al., 2017).

The primary types of advance directives each serve distinct purposes. A **living will** documents specific treatment preferences — typically addressing mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition and hydration, cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR), and dialysis — under defined circumstances such as terminal illness or permanent unconsciousness. A **durable power of attorney for health care (DPAHC)** designates a health care proxy who is authorized to make health care decisions if the patient becomes incapacitated; it is the most flexible type of advance directive because it can address situations not anticipated in a living will. The **Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST)** — also called MOLST or MOST in some states — differs from advance directives in that it is a medical order, not merely a planning document, that translates patient preferences into actionable clinical instructions; it travels with the patient across care settings and does not require physician re-activation in an emergency. Finally, the **Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) / Do Not Attempt Resuscitation (DNAR) order** is a physician order — not an advance directive — that instructs the care team not to perform CPR if the patient experiences cardiopulmonary arrest. Nurses must verify a current DNR order exists in the chart before acting on a patient's or family's verbal statement that a DNR is in place.

### 3.5.2 The Nurse's Role in Advance Care Planning

Nurses play a pivotal role in advance care planning — a process of reflection, communication, and documentation that occurs over time, ideally well before a health crisis. This role begins with **screening and documentation**: asking patients at admission whether they have an advance directive, documenting the response, and ensuring any existing directive is placed in the medical record. The nurse also provides **education** by explaining the types of advance directives and the importance of making preferences known. Through **facilitation**, the nurse encourages patients to discuss end-of-life preferences with their families and to consult the facility's patient advocate or social worker for assistance completing documents. Finally, the nurse's **advocacy** function requires ensuring that existing advance directives are honored; when clinical management conflicts with a patient's documented preferences, the nurse must escalate the concern immediately.

> **Patient Voice: "No One Asked Me"**  
> A study by Heyland et al. (2013) found that more than half of seriously ill hospitalized patients reported that no member of the care team had discussed their goals of care with them. Many patients want to have these conversations but wait for the health care team to initiate them. Nurses — who spend more time with patients than any other provider — are uniquely positioned to open the door to these conversations with a simple question: "Have you thought about what kind of care would be most important to you if you were seriously ill?"

### 3.5.3 Honoring and Challenging Advance Directives

Advance directives must be honored unless they are legally invalid, the patient has revoked them, or they do not address the clinical situation at hand. A patient may revoke an advance directive at any time, verbally or in writing, as long as they have decision-making capacity. The revocation supersedes any prior document.

Conflicts can arise when family members override a patient's documented wishes, when a directive is ambiguous in the current clinical context, or when a provider's conscience objection conflicts with the patient's directive. In these situations, the nurse should notify the attending physician, document the concern, and request ethics consultation if the conflict cannot be resolved through direct communication.

## Section 3.6 — Confidentiality, Privacy, and HIPAA

### 3.6.1 The Ethical and Legal Foundations of Confidentiality

**Confidentiality** — the obligation to protect information shared by a patient from disclosure without consent — is both an ethical duty rooted in trust and a legal requirement under federal and state law. The nurse-patient relationship depends on patients feeling safe to disclose sensitive information; without confidentiality, patients withhold symptoms, medications, and risk behaviors that are essential to safe clinical care.

The Hippocratic tradition established confidentiality as a medical virtue more than 2,500 years ago. In contemporary nursing ethics, the ANA Code of Ethics (2015a) addresses confidentiality in Provision 3, affirming that nurses must protect patient privacy and confidentiality. Legally, confidentiality is governed primarily by HIPAA at the federal level and by additional state statutes that may impose stricter requirements.

### 3.6.2 HIPAA: The Privacy and Security Rules

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 and its subsequent implementing regulations — the **Privacy Rule** (effective 2003) and the **Security Rule** (effective 2005) — establish the national standard for the protection of **protected health information (PHI)**.

PHI is defined as any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a **covered entity** (health care providers, health plans, health care clearinghouses) or their **business associates** in any form: paper, electronic, or oral. Individually identifiable information includes not only name and diagnosis but also date of birth, geographic identifiers, account numbers, and any other information that could reasonably identify a patient.

Several key provisions of the HIPAA Privacy Rule are directly relevant to nursing practice. The **minimum necessary standard** requires that nurses access and disclose only the minimum PHI necessary to accomplish a task; reviewing a patient's entire medical history when performing a routine medication administration is inconsistent with this standard. Regarding **permitted disclosures**, PHI may be shared for treatment, payment, and health care operations (TPO) without specific patient authorization, but outside these purposes, written authorization is required with limited exceptions. Additionally, **patient rights under HIPAA** are broad: patients have the right to access their records, request amendments, receive an accounting of disclosures, and request restrictions on certain uses.

> **Legal Spotlight: HIPAA Violations in Clinical Practice**  
> Common HIPAA violations in nursing include: discussing patient information in public areas (elevators, cafeterias, hallways); leaving computer screens with PHI visible; sending unencrypted patient information via personal email or text; and accessing records of patients not under the nurse's care (including celebrity patients or family members). Penalties range from corrective action plans to criminal prosecution. Nurses have lost their licenses following HIPAA violations — this is not a theoretical risk.

### 3.6.3 Exceptions to Confidentiality

Confidentiality is not absolute. Several legally mandated and ethically justified exceptions require or permit disclosure without patient consent. **Mandatory reporting laws** require nurses to report certain communicable diseases (e.g., tuberculosis, sexually transmitted infections), child abuse and neglect, elder or vulnerable adult abuse, and gunshot or knife wounds to appropriate authorities; these obligations vary by state but override confidentiality. Similarly, **public health reporting** requirements under CDC-mandated disease surveillance programs require the reporting of certain conditions. In most states, the **duty to protect** — derived from the *Tarasoff* doctrine — obligates providers to take reasonable steps to protect identifiable third parties from a patient's credible, specific threats of serious harm, which may include warning the potential victim and/or notifying law enforcement. Finally, a valid **court-ordered disclosure** requires the release of PHI even without patient consent.

Nurses navigating these exceptions should consult their facility's legal and compliance resources, document their reasoning, and involve supervisors as appropriate.

### 3.6.4 Electronic Health Records and Social Media

The proliferation of electronic health records (EHRs) and social media has created new vectors for confidentiality breaches. The EHR's audit trail — which records every access to a patient's record — means that inappropriate access is more detectable than ever. Nurses must understand that accessing a patient's EHR without a clinical need is a HIPAA violation regardless of intent.

Social media presents particular risks. Posting any patient information — even without names — on social media platforms may constitute a HIPAA violation if the combination of details could identify the patient. The ANA's *Principles for Social Networking and the Nurse* (2011) provides guidance, including the recommendation to never photograph patients without explicit consent and never share patient stories publicly, even when intended as educational content.

## Section 3.7 — Special Populations and Vulnerable Patients

### 3.7.1 Minors and the Question of Consent

The default legal rule is that **minors** (persons under 18 in most states) cannot consent to medical treatment on their own behalf — parental or guardian consent is required. However, this rule is subject to important exceptions that nurses must understand.

Most states recognize the rights of **emancipated minors** to consent to their own care. Emancipation typically applies to minors who are married, on active military duty, economically self-supporting and living independently, or declared emancipated by a court. Additionally, most states permit minors to consent to specific categories of care without parental involvement, including sexual and reproductive health (contraception, STI testing and treatment, and pregnancy care), mental health and substance use disorder treatment, and emergency care.

The **mature minor doctrine**, recognized in some states, holds that minors who demonstrate sufficient intelligence and maturity to understand the nature and consequences of a proposed treatment may consent independently, regardless of age. This doctrine requires careful clinical assessment and legal consultation.

Distinct from consent is **assent** — the minor's affirmative agreement to participate in a procedure. Even when parental consent is legally sufficient, the nurse and care team have an ethical obligation to seek the minor's assent for non-emergency procedures, to the extent the minor is capable of providing it.

### 3.7.2 Patients with Cognitive Impairment

Patients with dementia, intellectual disability, acquired brain injury, or severe psychiatric illness may have fluctuating or persistently reduced decision-making capacity. As discussed in Section 3.4, diagnosis alone does not establish incapacity. Nurses must individually assess each patient's ability to participate in specific decisions.

For patients with mild to moderate cognitive impairment, supported decision-making — providing information in simplified language, using pictures, allowing extra time, and involving trusted support persons — can enhance participation and preserve autonomy. The disability rights community advocates strongly for supported decision-making as an alternative to surrogate decision-making whenever possible.

### 3.7.3 Prisoners and Incarcerated Patients

Incarcerated patients retain constitutional rights to health care under the Eighth Amendment's prohibition of cruel and unusual punishment. The landmark Supreme Court case *Estelle v. Gamble* (1976) established that deliberate indifference to a prisoner's serious medical needs constitutes an Eighth Amendment violation. When nurses provide care to incarcerated patients — whether in a prison health clinic or a community hospital — the nurse's primary obligation is to the patient's health and rights, not to the correctional system.

Specific challenges include ensuring privacy during examinations (correctional officers should not be present during clinical assessments), protecting the confidentiality of health information from correctional staff, and resisting pressure to deny care or medication based on security concerns rather than clinical judgment. Nurses caring for incarcerated patients should be familiar with both the facility's policies and professional nursing standards governing this population.

### 3.7.4 Patients Experiencing Domestic Violence and Human Trafficking

Nurses are mandatory reporters of suspected child abuse in all states and mandatory reporters of elder and vulnerable adult abuse in most states. For adult victims of domestic violence, mandatory reporting laws vary — some states require reporting of injuries caused by weapons; others prioritize victim-centered approaches that allow the patient to decide whether to involve law enforcement.

Human trafficking victims may present in health care settings with injuries, malnutrition, signs of sexual violence, or controlled access by an accompanying person. Nurses should be trained to recognize trafficking indicators and to conduct private screening assessments. The Trafficking Victims Protection Act (2000) and subsequent reauthorizations provide federal protections, and the National Human Trafficking Hotline is available at 1-888-373-7888.

## Section 3.8 — Ethical Conflicts in Patient Rights

### 3.8.1 When Autonomy Conflicts with Beneficence

The most common ethical conflict in patient rights arises when a patient makes a decision that the clinical team believes is contrary to the patient's best interests. A patient with heart failure who refuses a recommended procedure, a patient with severe depression who declines psychiatric hospitalization, a patient with end-stage cancer who chooses aggressive treatment that oncology considers futile — these situations pit the ethical principles of **autonomy** against **beneficence** and create moral distress for nurses who are committed to both.

The resolution of these conflicts begins with a careful capacity assessment. If the patient has decision-making capacity, the patient's autonomous choice must be honored, even if the team disagrees. The nurse's role shifts to ensuring the patient has all available information, exploring the patient's reasoning, addressing correctable barriers (e.g., untreated depression, misinformation), and providing support regardless of the decision made.

If the patient lacks capacity, the analysis becomes more complex and should involve the attending physician, the patient's surrogate, and — in persistent conflicts — the ethics committee.

### 3.8.2 Futility and the Limits of Patient Demands

A different type of conflict arises when patients or families request treatments the clinical team considers medically futile — interventions unlikely to produce any clinical benefit. Families demanding "everything" be done for a patient with an unrecoverable condition are exercising a form of surrogate decision-making that may conflict with both medical judgment and the patient's own prior expressed wishes.

Medical futility is a contested concept, and nurses should approach these situations with humility about the limits of prognostic certainty. Families demanding aggressive care are almost always expressing love and grief, not clinical expertise. Nurses can provide support, facilitate family meetings with palliative care specialists, and ensure the conversation centers on what the patient would want rather than what can technically be done.

> **EBP Connection: Palliative Care Consultations and Family Conflict**  
> Research by Temel et al. (2010) demonstrated that early palliative care consultation significantly improved quality of life, reduced aggressive end-of-life interventions, and — counterintuitively — was associated with longer survival in patients with non-small cell lung cancer. Nurses can advocate for early palliative care consultation as an evidence-based strategy to reduce futility conflicts and center the patient's goals.

### 3.8.3 Conscientious Objection and the Limits of Nurse Advocacy

Nurses, like all professionals, have a conscience. The ANA Code of Ethics (2015a) acknowledges that nurses may decline assignments that conflict with their personal moral beliefs, provided they do not abandon patients in urgent need and that patient care is transferred to another provider. This is known as **conscientious objection**.

However, conscientious objection is not unlimited. A nurse who objects to participating in a legal abortion may withdraw from that assignment if appropriate transfer is arranged. A nurse who objects to honoring a patient's DNR order based on personal religious beliefs does not have the right to perform CPR in violation of the patient's advance directive — here, the nurse's personal belief yields to the patient's legal right. The critical distinction is between objecting to participating in a procedure (permissible with appropriate transfer) and overriding a patient's rights based on personal belief (not permissible).

### 3.8.4 A Framework for Ethical Decision-Making in Patient Rights Conflicts

When confronted with a complex patient rights conflict, nurses can apply the following structured framework (American Association of Colleges of Nursing, 2021):

1. **Identify the ethical conflict:** What competing values or principles are in tension?
2. **Gather the clinical facts:** What is the patient's diagnosis, prognosis, and clinical status? What are the realistic treatment options?
3. **Assess decision-making capacity:** Does the patient have capacity for this decision?
4. **Clarify the patient's values and preferences:** What does the patient want? What did they say in advance directives?
5. **Identify the stakeholders:** Who is affected by this decision? What are their interests and relationships?
6. **Consult available resources:** Ethics committee, social work, chaplain, legal counsel.
7. **Act and document:** Implement the decision, document the process and rationale, and continue monitoring.

This framework does not eliminate the discomfort of ethical conflicts, but it provides a disciplined process that protects the patient's rights and the nurse's professional integrity.

## Case Study: The Signature That Wasn't Ready

**Setting:** Medical-surgical unit, community hospital

**Scenario:**

**Scene 1:** At 0630, Nurse Maya is performing morning assessments when she enters the room of Ms. Chen, a 74-year-old patient with early-stage Alzheimer's disease who was admitted the previous evening for elective repair of a hiatal hernia. The surgical consent form, completed in English, is already signed with Ms. Chen's name, though Ms. Chen's primary language is Cantonese. When Maya greets the patient in English, Ms. Chen looks confused and asks for her daughter. Maya reviews the chart and discovers that the surgeon obtained consent the previous evening at 2200 — shortly after admission and before any nursing assessment of the patient's cognitive or language status. The interpreter services documentation is absent from the chart.

**Scene 2:** Ms. Chen's daughter, Lucy, arrives at 0700. She speaks both Cantonese and English fluently. She tells Maya: "My mother signed the form, but I was in the room and she kept looking to me for answers. I'm not sure she understood — she asked me three times what the paper was about." Lucy adds that her mother has seemed confused since arrival and that her Alzheimer's is "not that bad, but she gets flustered with strangers." Maya contacts the charge nurse and then calls the surgeon to report her concerns. The surgeon responds: "She signed the form. We're scheduled for 0800 — I don't want to push the case." Maya documents the exchange and contacts the anesthesiologist, who agrees to delay the case pending a capacity evaluation and proper interpreter-assisted re-consent.

**Scene 3:** The attending hospitalist performs a capacity assessment. Ms. Chen, when addressed through the hospital's Cantonese interpreter, is able to state what the procedure involves but expresses significant uncertainty about why it is necessary and asks whether she could "just go home." The hospitalist determines that Ms. Chen has diminished capacity for this elective decision and contacts Lucy as the patient's designated DPAHC. Lucy and Ms. Chen's other adult children convene in the morning. After a discussion with the surgeon using the interpreter, they agree that the procedure is consistent with their mother's previously stated desire to manage her reflux symptoms, and Lucy signs as surrogate decision-maker with proper documentation. The case proceeds at noon with no clinical complications.

**Discussion Questions:**

1. What specific patient rights were at risk in Scene 1, and what nursing actions — if performed the previous evening — could have prevented the situation?
2. Applying the criteria for valid informed consent (disclosure, comprehension, voluntariness), analyze whether Ms. Chen's initial consent was legally and ethically valid. Explain your reasoning.
3. Maya faced pushback from the surgeon when she raised her concerns. Using the ANA Code of Ethics and the advocacy strategies discussed in Section 3.2, how should a nurse respond when advocacy is resisted by a physician or team member?
4. In Scene 3, the hospitalist determined that Ms. Chen lacked capacity for this elective decision. What standard should Lucy apply as surrogate decision-maker — substituted judgment or best interest — and what evidence from the scenario supports your choice?

## Chapter Summary

This chapter explored the rights of patients in health care settings and the nurse's professional, legal, and ethical obligations to uphold those rights. Beginning with the historical evolution of the Patient Bill of Rights, the chapter traced the legal framework that now governs patient rights across federal statutes, state law, and accreditation standards. Nursing advocacy was examined as both an ethical imperative and a practical skill, requiring assertive communication, clinical knowledge, and institutional engagement. The chapter examined informed consent in depth — its elements, its legal requirements, and the nurse's specific role in protecting the integrity of the consent process. The clinical assessment of decision-making capacity and the legal concept of competence were distinguished, with practical application to surrogate decision-making. Advance directives — living wills, durable powers of attorney for health care, and POLST forms — were reviewed with attention to the nurse's role in advance care planning. HIPAA's Privacy and Security Rules were applied to everyday nursing scenarios, and the exceptions to confidentiality that carry mandatory reporting obligations were discussed. Special populations — minors, cognitively impaired patients, prisoners, and trafficking victims — were examined to highlight how patient rights protections must be tailored to vulnerability. The chapter concluded with an ethical decision-making framework for navigating the conflicts that inevitably arise when autonomy, beneficence, and institutional pressures collide.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. The Patient Bill of Rights has evolved from a voluntary AHA statement (1972) into a legally enforceable framework governed by federal statutes including the PSDA and HIPAA.
2. Nursing advocacy is a professional and ethical obligation requiring action at the individual, institutional, and societal levels, even when structural barriers create resistance.
3. Valid informed consent requires disclosure, comprehension, and voluntariness; the nurse's role is to verify these elements and escalate concerns before a procedure proceeds.
4. Decision-making capacity is a clinical determination that is decision-specific and time-specific; legal competence requires judicial adjudication and should never be assumed from diagnosis alone.
5. Advance directives — including living wills, durable powers of attorney for health care, and POLST forms — must be documented, honored, and integrated into each patient's plan of care.
6. HIPAA's Privacy and Security Rules require nurses to apply the minimum necessary standard, protect PHI in all its forms, and understand both permitted disclosures and patient rights under the law.
7. Special populations including minors, cognitively impaired patients, prisoners, and victims of violence require heightened attention to rights protections and specific legal knowledge.
8. Ethical conflicts in patient rights are navigated through a structured framework that begins with capacity assessment, centers the patient's values, and leverages institutional resources including ethics consultation.
