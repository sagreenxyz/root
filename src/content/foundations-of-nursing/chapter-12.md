## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Identify the major macronutrients and micronutrients, describe their physiological functions, and explain the nursing implications of deficiency or excess.
2. Apply validated nutritional assessment tools — including the Mini Nutritional Assessment, MUST, and SGA — to identify patients at nutritional risk.
3. Recognize the clinical presentations of malnutrition, describe evidence-based nursing interventions to prevent and treat it, and identify patients requiring interprofessional nutrition support.
4. Discuss the nursing assessment and non-judgmental care approach for patients with obesity and eating disorders.

6. Apply knowledge of evidence-based therapeutic diets to patients with cardiac disease, chronic kidney disease, and diabetes mellitus.
7. Describe how nutritional needs and nursing approaches change across major lifespan stages.
8. Identify clinically important food-drug interactions and explain how nurses can prevent and monitor for them.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| **Macronutrient** | A nutrient required in large amounts to provide energy and structural components: carbohydrates, proteins, and fats |
| **Micronutrient** | A nutrient required in small amounts to regulate metabolic processes: vitamins and minerals |
| **Malnutrition** | A broad term encompassing both undernutrition and overnutrition; in clinical use, often refers to inadequate protein-energy intake or utilization |
| **Protein-energy malnutrition (PEM)** | A condition of insufficient protein and caloric intake characterized by muscle wasting, hypoalbuminemia, impaired immunity, and poor wound healing |
| **Nutritional screening** | A brief, validated process to identify individuals who are malnourished or at risk for malnutrition |
| **Nutritional assessment** | A comprehensive, multidimensional evaluation of nutritional status performed when screening identifies risk |

| **Enteral nutrition** | Delivery of nutrients directly into the gastrointestinal tract via a tube when oral intake is inadequate but gut function is preserved |
| **Parenteral nutrition** | Delivery of nutrients directly into the bloodstream (intravenous) when the gastrointestinal tract cannot be used |
| **Refeeding syndrome** | A potentially fatal metabolic complication of rapid nutrition repletion in severely malnourished patients, characterized by hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, fluid shifts, and cardiac arrhythmias |
| **Body mass index (BMI)** | A measure of weight relative to height (kg/m²) used to classify underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obesity; limited by inability to distinguish fat mass from lean mass |

## Introduction

Mr. Osei is a 67-year-old retired teacher who has been admitted to the medical floor with a hip fracture sustained in a fall. As the admitting nurse completes her assessment, she notes that Mr. Osei is thin — he reports he has lost 12 pounds over the past three months "without trying" — and his clothes appear loose on his frame. He tells her he lives alone since his wife died 18 months ago and that he "doesn't bother to cook much anymore." His skin is dry and tenting is present on the dorsum of the hand. His serum albumin returns at 2.7 g/dL (normal 3.5–5.0 g/dL). He is taking warfarin for atrial fibrillation, atorvastatin for hyperlipidemia, and metformin for type 2 diabetes.

Mr. Osei's situation illustrates a reality that nurses encounter daily: **nutritional status is a critical determinant of clinical outcomes** across virtually every diagnostic category. Malnourished patients have longer hospital stays, higher rates of infection and pressure injuries, impaired wound healing, increased falls risk, and higher 30-day readmission rates than well-nourished patients with comparable diagnoses (Cederholm et al., 2017). And yet nutritional assessment remains one of the most underperformed components of nursing care.

This chapter provides the foundational knowledge and clinical skills to assess, recognize, and address nutritional problems across the care continuum. Section 12.1 reviews the macronutrients and micronutrients essential to health and the clinical consequences of their deficiency or excess. Section 12.2 presents the validated screening and assessment tools that identify nutritional risk. Section 12.3 addresses malnutrition — its recognition, causes, and nursing interventions. Section 12.4 examines obesity and eating disorders with a focus on non-stigmatizing, evidence-based care. Section 12.5 describes enteral and parenteral nutrition support with emphasis on nursing management and complication prevention. Section 12.6 reviews therapeutic diets for the most common chronic conditions. Section 12.7 addresses nutritional needs across the lifespan. Section 12.8 covers the food-drug interactions of greatest clinical importance.

## Section 12.1 — Macronutrients and Micronutrients: Functions and Sources

### 12.1.1 Macronutrients: Carbohydrates, Proteins, and Fats

**Macronutrients** are the energy-providing nutrients required in relatively large quantities. Understanding their functions and clinical significance enables nurses to interpret dietary histories, identify deficiency risks, and support patient nutrition counseling.

**Carbohydrates** are the body's preferred energy substrate. Each gram of carbohydrate provides 4 kcal. Carbohydrates are classified by molecular complexity: monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose), disaccharides (sucrose, lactose, maltose), oligosaccharides, and polysaccharides (starches, dietary fiber). Dietary fiber — both soluble (oats, legumes, fruits) and insoluble (whole grains, vegetables) — is not digestible but provides important physiological benefits: soluble fiber reduces serum LDL cholesterol and slows glucose absorption; insoluble fiber promotes bowel regularity and prevents constipation. The Dietary Reference Intake (DRI) for carbohydrates is 130 g/day for adults (the minimum required for adequate brain glucose supply), with a recommended intake of 45–65% of total calories.

**Proteins** are composed of amino acids, of which 9 are essential (cannot be synthesized by the body): histidine, isoleucine, leucine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine, threonine, tryptophan, and valine. Each gram of protein provides 4 kcal. Proteins serve structural functions (collagen, keratin, muscle), enzymatic functions (all enzymes are proteins), transport functions (hemoglobin, albumin), immunological functions (antibodies, complement), and signaling functions (many hormones are peptides). The DRI for protein in healthy adults is 0.8 g/kg of body weight per day; requirements increase substantially with illness, surgery, burns, and other catabolic states — often to 1.2–2.0 g/kg per day — to support tissue repair and immune function.

**Dietary fats** provide 9 kcal per gram and serve essential roles in cell membrane structure, fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), hormone synthesis, and neural development. Fats are classified by their fatty acid composition. **Saturated fats** — found in animal fats, coconut oil, and palm oil — are solid at room temperature; excessive intake is associated with elevated LDL cholesterol and cardiovascular disease risk. **Monounsaturated fats** (found in olive oil, avocado, and nuts) are associated with reduced LDL and preserved HDL, forming the foundation of the Mediterranean dietary pattern. **Polyunsaturated fats** include omega-6 fatty acids from vegetable oils and omega-3 fatty acids from fatty fish, flaxseed, and walnuts; omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) have well-documented anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective effects. **Trans fats**, derived from partially hydrogenated vegetable oils, raise LDL, lower HDL, and increase inflammation; the FDA has removed their GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe) status, and they have been largely eliminated from the U.S. food supply.

### 12.1.2 Micronutrients: Vitamins and Minerals

**Vitamins** are organic compounds required in small amounts for enzymatic and regulatory functions. They are classified as water-soluble (B vitamins, vitamin C) or fat-soluble (vitamins A, D, E, K). Fat-soluble vitamins are stored in adipose tissue and can accumulate to toxic levels with supplementation; water-soluble vitamins are largely excreted in the urine and carry lower toxicity risk (with notable exceptions such as vitamin B6 toxicity with prolonged excessive supplementation).

Key clinically relevant vitamin deficiencies include:

| Vitamin | Key Functions | Deficiency Syndrome | High-Risk Populations |
|---------|--------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Vitamin D | Calcium absorption, bone mineralization, immune regulation, muscle function | Rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults); muscle weakness, increased fracture risk | Older adults, dark skin in low-sun environments, nursing home residents, malabsorption |
| Vitamin B12 | DNA synthesis, myelin synthesis, red blood cell maturation | Megaloblastic anemia, peripheral neuropathy, subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord | Strict vegans, older adults (atrophic gastritis reduces intrinsic factor), post-gastrectomy patients |
| Folate (B9) | DNA synthesis, cell division, neural tube development | Megaloblastic anemia; neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly) if deficient periconceptionally | Women of childbearing age, patients on methotrexate or phenytoin |
| Vitamin C | Collagen synthesis, antioxidant, iron absorption enhancement | Scurvy (perifollicular hemorrhages, gingival bleeding, impaired wound healing) | Older adults, smokers, limited fresh fruit/vegetable intake |
| Vitamin K | Coagulation factors II, VII, IX, X; bone mineralization | Coagulopathy (elevated PT/INR), increased fracture risk | Newborns (given IM Vitamin K at birth), patients on broad-spectrum antibiotics |
| Iron | Oxygen transport (hemoglobin), cellular energy (cytochromes) | Iron-deficiency anemia (microcytic, hypochromic); fatigue, pallor, tachycardia | Women of reproductive age, infants 6–12 months, vegetarians, chronic blood loss |

**Minerals** are inorganic elements required for structural, enzymatic, and regulatory functions. Macrominerals (calcium, phosphorus, magnesium, sodium, potassium, chloride, sulfur) are required in amounts greater than 100 mg/day; trace minerals (iron, zinc, iodine, selenium, fluoride, chromium, copper, manganese, molybdenum) are required in smaller amounts.

> **Clinical Tip: Calcium and Bone Health**  
> Adequate calcium intake across the lifespan is essential for bone mineral density maximization and osteoporosis prevention. Daily requirements: infants (200–260 mg), toddlers (700 mg), children 4–8 (1,000 mg), children 9–18 (1,300 mg), adults 19–50 (1,000 mg), adults 51–70 women and 71+ all adults (1,200 mg). Calcium absorption is enhanced by vitamin D and reduced by excess fiber, phytates, and oxalates. Vitamin D deficiency is epidemic in the United States, affecting an estimated 35–40% of adults; nursing assessment should include dietary history and risk factor identification for deficiency in all patients.

## Section 12.2 — Nutritional Assessment Tools

### 12.2.1 Nutritional Screening vs. Assessment

**Nutritional screening** and **nutritional assessment** are distinct processes with different purposes, time requirements, and practitioner roles.

**Nutritional screening** is a brief, validated process designed to rapidly identify individuals who are malnourished or at risk for malnutrition. Screening is typically performed by nurses or other frontline staff at the point of admission or first contact. Results determine whether a comprehensive nutritional assessment is needed. Screening tools must be quick, simple, valid, and reliable across diverse patient populations.

**Nutritional assessment** is a comprehensive, multidimensional evaluation of nutritional status performed by or in collaboration with a registered dietitian (RD) when screening identifies risk. Assessment incorporates anthropometric measurements (weight, height, BMI, mid-arm circumference, skinfold thickness), biochemical data (albumin, prealbumin, transferrin, complete blood count), clinical examination (hair, skin, nails, mucous membranes, edema, muscle wasting), and dietary history (24-hour recall, food frequency questionnaire, dietary history interview).

**The nurse's role** is to perform initial screening at admission, document and communicate screening results, monitor nutritional intake and tolerance throughout hospitalization, facilitate registered dietitian referral and interprofessional care plan implementation, and educate patients and families about nutritional goals.

### 12.2.2 Validated Screening and Assessment Tools

**Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST):** A five-step tool developed for use in community and hospital settings. Steps include: (1) calculate BMI; (2) calculate unplanned weight loss percentage; (3) establish acute disease effect; (4) add scores; (5) use management guidelines. Overall risk is classified as low (0), medium (1), or high (≥2). MUST demonstrates strong validity and reliability across diverse clinical populations.

**Mini Nutritional Assessment (MNA):** A two-stage tool developed specifically for older adults. The six-item MNA-Short Form (MNA-SF) screens for malnutrition or risk; scores of 11 or less trigger the full 18-item MNA for comprehensive assessment. The MNA is the most widely validated nutritional tool for elderly populations worldwide and is endorsed by the European Society for Clinical Nutrition and Metabolism (ESPEN).

**Subjective Global Assessment (SGA):** A clinical assessment tool that integrates history (weight change, dietary intake change, gastrointestinal symptoms, functional capacity, metabolic demands of underlying disease) and physical examination (loss of subcutaneous fat, muscle wasting, edema, ascites) to classify nutritional status into three categories: **SGA A** (well-nourished), **SGA B** (mildly to moderately malnourished, or suspected malnutrition), and **SGA C** (severely malnourished).

The SGA is validated in multiple clinical populations including surgical patients, dialysis patients, oncology patients, and hospitalized older adults. Unlike MUST and MNA, the SGA requires trained clinician administration.

## Section 12.3 — Malnutrition: Recognition, Risk Factors, and Nursing Interventions

### 12.3.1 Recognizing Malnutrition in Clinical Settings

**Malnutrition** — in its clinical sense of inadequate protein-energy intake or utilization — is a pervasive problem in hospitalized patients. Systematic reviews indicate that 20–50% of hospitalized patients are malnourished on admission, and nutritional status often deteriorates further during hospitalization due to nothing-by-mouth (NPO) orders, procedure-related fasting, anorexia from medications, and inadequate monitoring of oral intake (Cederholm et al., 2017).

The consequences of malnutrition are severe and well-documented. **Impaired immune function** results from protein deficiency, which reduces T-lymphocyte function, antibody production, and mucosal barrier integrity, increasing susceptibility to infection. Additionally, **impaired wound healing** occurs because protein deficiency disrupts collagen synthesis, fibroblast proliferation, and angiogenesis, while vitamin C deficiency directly impairs collagen crosslinking. Malnutrition is also an independent risk factor for **pressure injury** development and impairs healing of existing wounds. Furthermore, respiratory muscle wasting from protein-energy malnutrition **prolongs mechanical ventilation** dependence. Finally, malnutrition is independently associated with **increased in-hospital and 30-day mortality** across diagnostic categories.

The 2018 **Global Leadership Initiative on Malnutrition (GLIM) Criteria** established a standardized framework for diagnosing malnutrition based on three phenotypic criteria (weight loss, low BMI, reduced muscle mass) and two etiologic criteria (reduced food intake/assimilation; disease burden/inflammation). Diagnosis requires at least one phenotypic and one etiologic criterion.

### 12.3.2 Nursing Interventions for Malnutrition

The nurse's nutritional interventions begin with consistent, accurate intake-and-output documentation. Calorie counts — tracking the percentage of meals consumed — are an important nursing responsibility and should be ordered for all patients identified as at nutritional risk. Common barriers to adequate oral intake in hospitalized patients include:

Common barriers to adequate oral intake in hospitalized patients include NPO or clear liquid diet orders that extend longer than clinically necessary, meal delivery timing that conflicts with procedures or tests, poorly fitting or absent dentures, uncontrolled pain or nausea preventing eating, dysphagia without appropriate diet texture modification, and lack of assistance for patients who cannot self-feed.

Nurses can directly address many of these barriers: advocating for diet order liberalization as soon as clinically appropriate, requesting speech-language pathology evaluation for dysphagia, ensuring oral care and denture availability, scheduling pain and antiemetic medications appropriately before meals, and providing feeding assistance with patience and encouragement.

**Oral nutritional supplements (ONS)** — commercially prepared liquid or semi-solid supplements providing additional calories, protein, and micronutrients — are a first-line intervention for patients who can eat but are not meeting caloric targets. Nurse administration of ONS as scheduled medications (rather than optional "if desired" offerings) significantly increases patient consumption and caloric adequacy.

> **Safety Alert: Refeeding Syndrome**  
> Refeeding syndrome is a potentially fatal metabolic complication that can occur when nutrition is initiated too rapidly in severely malnourished patients. When malnourished patients begin receiving carbohydrates, the resulting insulin surge drives phosphate, potassium, and magnesium into cells, causing precipitous drops in serum levels. Hypophosphatemia — the hallmark of refeeding syndrome — can cause respiratory failure, cardiac arrhythmias, rhabdomyolysis, hemolytic anemia, and death. At-risk patients include those with anorexia nervosa, chronic alcoholism, prolonged fasting, oncological cachexia, and postoperative NPO states. Nursing responsibilities include identifying at-risk patients, alerting the team before nutrition is initiated, monitoring electrolytes closely during the refeeding period, and reporting early signs (muscle weakness, confusion, arrhythmias) immediately.

## Section 12.4 — Obesity and Eating Disorders: Nursing Considerations

### 12.4.1 Obesity: A Weight-Neutral, Evidence-Based Nursing Approach

**Obesity** — defined as a BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² — affects approximately 42% of U.S. adults and is associated with increased risk of type 2 diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, obstructive sleep apnea, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease, and several cancers (Flegal et al., 2016). However, the relationship between weight and health is complex: BMI is a poor measure of metabolic health, individuals at the same BMI show markedly different cardiometabolic risk profiles, and weight stigma in healthcare settings — demonstrated to worsen health outcomes — is widespread.

Contemporary evidence-based nursing practice in obesity care adopts a **weight-neutral** or **Health at Every Size (HAES)** framework with several core priorities. Assessment focuses on **behavior** — dietary patterns, physical activity, sleep quality, stress, and emotional eating — rather than weight-centric judgment. Nurses use **non-stigmatizing language**, preferring "patient with obesity" over "obese patient" and avoiding terms such as "fat," "heavy," or "overweight" unless the patient uses them about themselves. **Treating every patient with equal dignity** regardless of body size is essential, including ensuring adequate-sized gowns, chairs, blood pressure cuffs, and imaging equipment. Additionally, nurses systematically **address comorbidities** such as hypertension, dyslipidemia, sleep apnea, and joint pain, and facilitate **interprofessional referral** to registered dietitians, exercise physiologists, and behavioral health providers when indicated.

Weight loss interventions — when desired by the patient — are most effective when they combine behavioral support, dietary modification, and physical activity counseling. Pharmacological options (GLP-1 receptor agonists, orlistat) and bariatric surgery are evidence-supported for patients with BMI ≥ 30 with comorbidities or BMI ≥ 40, but are decisions to be made collaboratively with the patient and interprofessional team.

### 12.4.2 Eating Disorders: Recognition and Compassionate Care

**Eating disorders** are serious, potentially life-threatening psychiatric conditions with significant medical consequences. The most common eating disorders encountered in nursing practice are:

**Anorexia nervosa (AN):** Characterized by severe restriction of caloric intake, intense fear of weight gain, and distorted body image. AN has the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric disorder — approximately 5–6% per decade of illness — primarily from cardiac complications (arrhythmias from electrolyte imbalances, sudden cardiac death) and suicide. Physical findings include bradycardia, hypotension, hypothermia, lanugo (fine body hair), amenorrhea, and dry skin. Laboratory findings include leukopenia, anemia, hypoglycemia, hypokalemia, elevated LFTs, and osteopenia.

**Bulimia nervosa (BN):** Characterized by recurrent binge-purge cycles. Purging behaviors — self-induced vomiting, laxative abuse, diuretic misuse — cause electrolyte disturbances (hypokalemia, hyponatremia, metabolic alkalosis) that can be immediately life-threatening. Physical examination findings include erosion of dental enamel (from gastric acid exposure), parotid gland hypertrophy, calluses on the dorsum of the hand (Russell's sign from repeated self-induced vomiting), and peripheral edema.

**Binge eating disorder (BED):** The most prevalent eating disorder in the United States, BED is characterized by recurrent binge episodes without compensatory behavior. BED is strongly associated with obesity, depression, and shame, and responds to treatment with cognitive behavioral therapy and, in some cases, pharmacotherapy (lisdexamfetamine is FDA-approved for BED).

Nurses must approach patients with eating disorders with **compassion, consistency, and the recognition that eating disorders are serious illnesses — not choices, vanity, or moral failings.** The therapeutic nurse-patient relationship is a critical protective factor. Nursing responsibilities include monitoring vital signs and electrolytes, supporting meal completion without judgment, coordinating with eating disorder treatment teams, and maintaining clear boundaries around eating behaviors in the clinical environment.

> **EBP Connection: Weight Stigma in Healthcare Settings**  
> A systematic review by Puhl and Heuer (2009) documented that weight stigma is pervasive among healthcare providers and is experienced by 69% of patients with obesity during medical encounters. Weight stigma is independently associated with avoidance of preventive care, worsened depression and anxiety, and worse clinical outcomes — effects that are observed even controlling for BMI and baseline health status. The Obesity Medicine Association and the American Academy of Nursing have both issued practice statements calling for weight-stigma-free clinical environments. Nurses have both an ethical and an evidence-based obligation to examine and address their own weight-related biases.

## Section 12.5 — Enteral and Parenteral Nutrition: Indications and Nursing Management

### 12.5.1 Enteral Nutrition: "When the Gut Works, Use It"

**Enteral nutrition (EN)** is the delivery of nutrients directly into the gastrointestinal tract via a tube when oral intake is inadequate but GI function is preserved. The guiding clinical principle is: whenever the gut can be used safely, it should be used — enteral nutrition maintains GI mucosal integrity, supports the immune cells of the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), preserves the gut microbiome, and carries lower infection, metabolic, and cost burdens than parenteral nutrition.

Enteral nutrition is indicated when oral intake falls below 60% of estimated needs for more than 7–10 days (or more than 3–5 days in already-malnourished patients), as well as for neurological conditions impairing swallowing (such as stroke, TBI, or ALS), oropharyngeal or esophageal obstruction, severe burns or catabolic illness with markedly increased metabolic demands, and preoperative optimization in malnourished surgical patients.

Several enteral access routes are available depending on clinical circumstances. The **nasogastric (NG) tube** is intended for short-term use (less than 4 weeks) and is the preferred initial route when gastric emptying is intact, delivering nutrients via a tube inserted through the nostril into the stomach. When gastric feeding is not tolerated — due to gastroparesis, high aspiration risk, or acute pancreatitis — a **nasoduodenal (ND) or nasojejunal (NJ) tube** provides postpyloric feeding. For long-term feeding requirements exceeding 4–6 weeks, a **percutaneous endoscopic gastrostomy (PEG)** tube is placed endoscopically into the stomach, while a **percutaneous endoscopic jejunostomy (PEJ)** is used for long-term postpyloric feeding.

**Nursing management of enteral nutrition** encompasses multiple critical responsibilities:

*Before initiation:* Verify tube placement by radiograph (gold standard) before initiating any feeding or medication through a newly placed tube. pH testing and auscultation of air insufflation are not reliable standalone confirmation methods for tube placement verification.

*During feeding:* Elevate the head of the bed to 30–45 degrees continuously during feeding and for 30–60 minutes after intermittent feeding to reduce aspiration risk. Check gastric residual volumes (GRV) per institutional protocol; a GRV > 200–500 mL (depending on protocol) may indicate delayed gastric emptying and should prompt reassessment. Flush the tube with 30 mL of water before and after medication administration and every 4–8 hours during continuous feeding to maintain patency.

*Monitoring:* Monitor for tube feeding intolerance (nausea, vomiting, abdominal distension, diarrhea, constipation), metabolic complications (hyperglycemia, electrolyte abnormalities), and tube displacement. Monitor weight, fluid balance, and serum electrolytes at least three times weekly.

### 12.5.2 Parenteral Nutrition: When the Gut Cannot Be Used

**Parenteral nutrition (PN)** is the delivery of nutrients directly into the bloodstream, bypassing the gastrointestinal tract. PN is reserved for patients in whom the GI tract cannot be used for at least 7–14 days, or who require nutritional support but cannot tolerate enteral feeding.

Parenteral nutrition is indicated for patients with short bowel syndrome (fewer than 100–150 cm of functional small bowel), intestinal failure due to obstruction, ileus, severe malabsorption, or high-output fistula, inability to establish or maintain enteral access, and GI tract pathology — such as ischemia or perforation — that precludes enteral feeding.

PN is administered as **central parenteral nutrition (CPN)** via a central venous catheter (high osmolality requires central administration to avoid peripheral vein sclerosis) or as **peripheral parenteral nutrition (PPN)** via a peripheral vein (lower osmolality, lower caloric density; suitable only for short-term supplemental support).

Nursing management of parenteral nutrition requires strict aseptic technique for all catheter care, as central catheter-associated bloodstream infection (CLABSI) is the most serious infectious complication of PN. PN should be administered through a dedicated catheter lumen, avoiding blood draws or administration of other medications through the PN line when possible. Blood glucose must be monitored every 4–6 hours, as hyperglycemia is the most common metabolic complication of PN; insulin may be added to the PN bag or administered as a separate infusion per protocol. Electrolytes — particularly phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium (given the refeeding syndrome risk) — as well as liver function tests (for PN-associated liver disease) and triglycerides (if lipid emulsion is included) must be monitored regularly. PN should never be abruptly discontinued; instead, it should be tapered over 1–2 hours to prevent rebound hypoglycemia. The patient should be weighed daily with strict intake and output maintained throughout therapy.

## Section 12.6 — Therapeutic Diets for Common Conditions

### 12.6.1 Cardiac and Renal Diets

**Cardiac diet (Heart-Healthy Diet):** For patients with established cardiovascular disease, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, or elevated cardiovascular risk, the most evidence-supported dietary pattern is the **Mediterranean diet** — emphasizing olive oil, nuts, vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains, and fish; moderate poultry and dairy; and minimal red meat and ultra-processed foods. The PREDIMED trial demonstrated that a Mediterranean diet supplemented with olive oil or nuts reduced major cardiovascular events by approximately 30% versus a low-fat control diet (Estruch et al., 2018).

The **Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH)** diet is the most evidence-supported dietary pattern for blood pressure reduction, lowering systolic blood pressure by 8–14 mmHg in clinical trials. Key DASH principles: high in fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy, whole grains, and lean protein; low in sodium (< 2,300 mg/day, ideally < 1,500 mg/day in hypertension), saturated fat, and added sugar. Nurses teaching patients about heart-healthy eating should emphasize practical, culturally relevant substitutions rather than rigid rules, and should collaborate with a registered dietitian for comprehensive diet counseling.

**Chronic kidney disease (CKD) diet:** Dietary management of CKD is among the most complex nutritional interventions in nursing practice because it must simultaneously balance multiple nutrient restrictions that vary by CKD stage and the presence or absence of dialysis. Key principles:

| Nutrient | CKD Stages 3–4 (Non-Dialysis) | Dialysis (Stage 5) |
|----------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Protein | 0.6–0.8 g/kg/day (low-protein to slow progression) | 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day (to replace dialysis losses) |
| Sodium | < 2,000 mg/day | < 2,000 mg/day |
| Potassium | Restriction if hyperkalemic (< 3–4 g/day) | Restriction (< 2–3 g/day) |
| Phosphorus | Restriction (< 800–1,000 mg/day) | Restriction + phosphate binders with meals |
| Fluid | Usually unrestricted; restrict if edema/oliguria | Restriction based on residual urine output |

Nurses must teach patients with CKD to read food labels for sodium and phosphorus content, use the "HALT" mnemonic (High in potAssium, Limit These) for high-potassium foods to avoid, and take phosphate binders as prescribed with meals — not before or after.

### 12.6.2 Diabetic Nutrition Therapy

**Medical nutrition therapy (MNT)** for diabetes is defined by the American Diabetes Association (ADA) as an individualized, evidence-based approach to dietary management developed in collaboration with a registered dietitian and tailored to the patient's type of diabetes, medications, health goals, and personal preferences (ADA, 2023).

No single dietary pattern is recommended for all patients with diabetes. Multiple approaches — Mediterranean diet, DASH, low-carbohydrate, plant-based, and others — can be effective when implemented consistently. Key evidence-based principles that apply across dietary patterns:

Both **carbohydrate quality and quantity** matter: replacing refined carbohydrates with whole grains, legumes, and vegetables reduces postprandial glucose excursions, and counting grams of carbohydrate or using the plate method (half the plate vegetables, one quarter lean protein, one quarter whole grains or starchy vegetables) facilitates portion awareness. **Reducing ultra-processed food intake** is independently associated with improved glycemic control regardless of macronutrient composition. Furthermore, **consistency of carbohydrate intake** is particularly important for patients taking fixed-dose insulin to prevent hypoglycemia.

Nursing responsibilities in diabetes nutrition management include: assessing dietary patterns and food security at every encounter, reinforcing MNT principles established by the dietitian, recognizing when patient understanding or adherence is compromised, and avoiding prescriptive dietary restrictions that have not been individually negotiated with the patient and dietitian.

> **Historical Spotlight: The Glycemic Index — Promise and Complexity**  
> The glycemic index (GI) — developed by David Jenkins at the University of Toronto in 1981 — ranks foods by how rapidly they raise blood glucose relative to a reference food (pure glucose or white bread). High-GI foods (white rice, white bread, potatoes) cause rapid glucose spikes; low-GI foods (legumes, most fruits, whole grains) cause gradual rises. While physiologically sound in principle, GI is difficult to apply in practice: GI values vary with food preparation, ripeness, and food combinations. The ADA acknowledges the GI concept as potentially useful but does not recommend it as a primary diabetes dietary management tool, instead emphasizing overall dietary pattern quality. Nurses should be familiar with the GI concept while helping patients adopt practical, sustainable dietary approaches.

## Section 12.7 — Nutritional Needs Across the Lifespan

### 12.7.1 Nutritional Priorities in Early Life

**Pregnancy and lactation:** Nutritional requirements increase substantially during pregnancy to support fetal growth, placental development, increased maternal blood volume, and the metabolic demands of gestation. Key increases include: total energy (+340 kcal/day in second trimester, +452 kcal/day in third trimester), protein (+25 g/day), folate (600 mcg/day DFE — higher than non-pregnant recommendation to prevent neural tube defects), iron (27 mg/day — increased from 18 mg/day due to expanded maternal blood volume and fetal needs), and calcium (remains 1,000 mg/day in adults; fetal calcium is mobilized from maternal bone if dietary intake is inadequate). Prenatal vitamins are recommended for all pregnant people to ensure folate and iron adequacy. Substances to avoid include alcohol (no safe level during pregnancy), high-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, tilefish, king mackerel), and unpasteurized dairy and deli meats (listeria risk).

**Infants and children:** Breast milk is the gold-standard nutrition for infants through 6 months, providing all required nutrients (except vitamin D, which should be supplemented at 400 IU/day in breastfed infants) and bioactive immunological factors. Complementary solid foods should be introduced at approximately 6 months; early introduction of common allergens (peanuts, eggs) at 4–6 months is now recommended by the AAP to reduce allergic sensitization risk in most infants. Iron-rich foods (pureed meat, iron-fortified cereals) are particularly important as complementary foods because breast milk iron does not meet the needs of infants beyond 6 months.

### 12.7.2 Nutritional Priorities in Later Life

**Older adulthood (65+):** Nutritional needs and challenges are amplified in older adults by multiple intersecting factors: reduced appetite (anorexia of aging, driven by slower gastric emptying, reduced olfactory and gustatory sensitivity, and hormonal changes), reduced absorption efficiency, increased nutrient requirements for muscle maintenance (protein: 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day, higher than younger adults), medication-nutrient interactions, social isolation and food insecurity, dysphagia, cognitive impairment affecting meal preparation and intake, and dental problems affecting chewing.

Protein intake is particularly important in older adults. Increasing evidence supports higher protein intake (1.0–1.5 g/kg/day) for older adults to preserve muscle mass and prevent sarcopenia, in contrast to the 0.8 g/kg/day recommendation for younger adults. Leucine-rich protein sources (dairy, eggs, meat, legumes) stimulate muscle protein synthesis most effectively.

Vitamin D and calcium supplementation is recommended for most older adults because dietary intake is typically insufficient and sun exposure is often limited. Vitamin B12 supplementation is important for older adults because gastric atrophy (common in aging) reduces intrinsic factor production and impairs B12 absorption; crystalline B12 in supplements and fortified foods (not requiring intrinsic factor) is effectively absorbed even in patients with atrophic gastritis.

## Section 12.8 — Food-Drug Interactions of Clinical Importance

### 12.8.1 Mechanisms of Food-Drug Interactions

**Food-drug interactions** occur when a food, nutrient, or dietary component alters the absorption, distribution, metabolism, or excretion of a drug — increasing its efficacy, reducing its efficacy, or causing toxicity. The clinical significance of food-drug interactions ranges from minor (manageable by consistent administration timing) to life-threatening (requiring drug avoidance or substitution).

Several mechanisms underlie food-drug interactions. **Absorption effects** occur when food delays, reduces, or enhances drug absorption; some drugs must be taken on an empty stomach (levothyroxine, bisphosphonates) because food impairs absorption, while others must be taken with food to reduce GI irritation or to enhance the absorption of fat-soluble medications. **Enzyme inhibition or induction** represents another key mechanism: foods can inhibit or induce cytochrome P450 enzymes, altering drug metabolism — grapefruit juice is the classic example, as its furanocoumarins irreversibly inhibit CYP3A4 in the intestinal wall, dramatically increasing the bioavailability of CYP3A4-metabolized drugs and risking toxicity. Finally, **pharmacodynamic effects** arise when foods enhance or antagonize a drug's mechanism of action directly, as when high dietary vitamin K intake antagonizes warfarin's anticoagulant effect, or when tyramine-rich foods trigger hypertensive crisis in patients taking monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs).

### 12.8.2 Clinically Important Food-Drug Interactions

The following food-drug interactions are of greatest clinical importance for nursing practice:

**Warfarin and vitamin K:**  
Warfarin (Coumadin) is a vitamin K antagonist anticoagulant. Dietary vitamin K — found in leafy green vegetables (kale, spinach, collard greens, broccoli) — antagonizes warfarin's effect and reduces anticoagulation. Nurses must teach patients taking warfarin to maintain **consistent** (not eliminate) vitamin K intake rather than avoiding leafy greens entirely. Dramatic day-to-day changes in vitamin K consumption — not the absolute amount — drive INR instability. Reinforce regular INR monitoring and consistent diet pattern.

**Grapefruit and CYP3A4-metabolized drugs:**  
A single 8-ounce glass of grapefruit juice can inhibit intestinal CYP3A4 for 24–72 hours, increasing plasma levels of affected drugs by 200–500%. Affected drug classes include statins (simvastatin, lovastatin — risk of rhabdomyolysis), calcium channel blockers (amlodipine, felodipine), immunosuppressants (cyclosporine, tacrolimus), certain HIV antivirals, and some anxiolytics (triazolam, midazolam). Nurses should ask about grapefruit juice consumption as part of medication reconciliation and teach patients to avoid grapefruit and grapefruit juice if taking affected medications.

**Tyramine and monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs):**  
Patients taking MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine — used for refractory depression; linezolid — an antibiotic with MAOI properties) must avoid tyramine-rich foods. Tyramine is normally inactivated in the gut wall by MAO; when MAO is inhibited, unmetabolized tyramine enters systemic circulation and triggers massive norepinephrine release, causing a potentially fatal **hypertensive crisis** (sudden, severe headache, blood pressure > 180/110 mmHg, flushing, diaphoresis, palpitations). Tyramine-rich foods include aged cheeses, cured meats, fermented products (soy sauce, miso, sauerkraut), tap beer, and some wines. Nurses must include MAOI dietary counseling in every medication education encounter.

**Calcium and tetracyclines/fluoroquinolones:**  
Calcium (in dairy products, antacids, or supplements) chelates tetracycline and fluoroquinolone antibiotics in the GI tract, forming insoluble complexes that are not absorbed. This interaction can reduce antibiotic bioavailability by 30–50%. Patients should be taught to take doxycycline or ciprofloxacin on an empty stomach or at least 2 hours before or 6 hours after calcium-containing products.

**High-fiber foods and levothyroxine:**  
Dietary fiber, soy, walnuts, and calcium — when consumed close to levothyroxine ingestion — can bind the medication and impair absorption. Levothyroxine should be taken on an empty stomach, 30–60 minutes before breakfast (or 3–4 hours after the last meal), consistently every day. Even coffee can impair levothyroxine absorption; patients should take it with water only.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter has established the foundational knowledge and clinical skills necessary for nurses to assess, recognize, and address nutritional problems across the care continuum. Key takeaways include:

1. **Macronutrients** — carbohydrates (4 kcal/g), proteins (4 kcal/g), and fats (9 kcal/g) — provide energy and serve critical structural, enzymatic, and regulatory functions. Protein requirements increase substantially in illness and catabolic states.

2. **Micronutrient deficiencies** that nurses most commonly encounter include vitamin D, vitamin B12, folate, vitamin C, vitamin K, and iron — each with distinct clinical presentations, at-risk populations, and nursing interventions.

3. **Nutritional screening** with validated tools (MUST, MNA, SGA) should be performed on all admitted patients. Nurses perform initial screening and facilitate registered dietitian referral and interprofessional nutrition care plan implementation.

4. **Malnutrition** affects 20–50% of hospitalized patients, causes impaired immunity, wound healing, and organ function, and is associated with increased mortality. Nursing interventions include accurate intake documentation, diet order advocacy, oral nutritional supplement administration, and feeding assistance.

5. **Obesity and eating disorders** require non-stigmatizing, weight-neutral nursing approaches that prioritize patient dignity, behavioral assessment, compassionate care, and interprofessional collaboration.

6. **Enteral nutrition** is preferred over parenteral nutrition whenever the gut functions; nursing management includes tube placement verification, head-of-bed elevation, gastric residual monitoring, and electrolyte surveillance for refeeding syndrome.

7. **Therapeutic diets** — including heart-healthy/DASH for cardiovascular disease, protein/electrolyte-restricted for CKD, and carbohydrate-consistent for diabetes — must be individualized and culturally relevant; nurses reinforce dietitian-led medical nutrition therapy and assess comprehension and adherence.

8. **Clinically important food-drug interactions** — including warfarin/vitamin K, grapefruit/CYP3A4 drugs, tyramine/MAOIs, and calcium/antibiotics — require systematic nursing assessment and structured patient education to prevent adverse outcomes.

   B) Notify the provider and anticipate an order to add insulin to the TPN bag or initiate a continuous insulin infusion

## Answer Key

**1. C** — Rationale: A MUST score of 4 indicates high malnutrition risk and requires immediate registered dietitian consultation and implementation of a high-risk nutrition protocol, including nutritional supplementation and monitoring for refeeding syndrome before surgery. PN is not indicated before attempting enteral supplementation; the decision to delay surgery is the surgeon's, not the nurse's.

**2. B** — Rationale: Before any medication is administered via an enteral feeding tube, placement must be verified and the tube flushed. Medications should be administered in liquid form when available, or crushed and dissolved (for appropriate tablets). Stopping the feeding for 1 hour and clamping are not appropriate for medication administration; the tube feeding may be briefly paused, not clamped.

**3. B** — Rationale: Warfarin-vitamin K interaction: large increases in dietary vitamin K intake (not baseline consistent intake) reduce anticoagulation and can increase the INR in an unpredictable manner. Paradoxically, a sudden dramatic increase in vitamin K may actually have reduced the anticoagulant effect — but the INR of 5.2 suggests a more complex clinical picture that requires provider notification. The key nursing education point is dietary consistency. Eliminating all vitamin K forever is neither necessary nor recommended.

**4. C** — Rationale: Grapefruit furanocoumarins irreversibly inhibit intestinal CYP3A4 for 24–72 hours, so the timing of grapefruit consumption relative to the medication dose does not reduce the interaction risk. A patient taking simvastatin at night should still avoid grapefruit entirely throughout the day. Options A, B, and D reflect correct understanding.

**5. C** — Rationale: MNA-SF score of 9 (range 0–14): 12–14 = normal nutritional status; 8–11 = at risk of malnutrition; 0–7 = malnourished. A score of 9 indicates risk; the next step is to complete the full 18-item MNA and request a registered dietitian consultation. PN is not indicated at this stage.

**6. B** — Rationale: Hypophosphatemia is the hallmark of refeeding syndrome, occurring because insulin released in response to carbohydrate drives phosphate into cells, causing serum phosphorus to drop precipitously. A phosphorus drop from 3.2 to 1.4 mg/dL (normal 2.5–4.5 mg/dL) within days of nutrition initiation in a malnourished patient is the most concerning finding for refeeding syndrome. Mild hyperglycemia is expected with TPN; modest weight gain reflects fluid and nutrient retention; albumin lags as a marker due to its long half-life (20 days).

**7. B, D, E** — Rationale: For non-dialysis CKD Stage 4: protein should be restricted to 0.6–0.8 g/kg/day to slow progression (not increased); sodium restricted to < 2,000 mg/day; potassium restricted if hyperkalemic; phosphate binders are typically indicated but prescribed with meals — not automatically for all Stage 4 patients without confirmed hyperphosphatemia, so C is not universally correct. Once dialysis begins, protein requirements increase substantially.

**8. B** — Rationale: Therapeutic communication with patients with anorexia nervosa requires empathy, consistency, and a non-confrontational approach. Arguing about weight ("you're not fat") is counter-productive and escalates conflict. Using threats ("we'll use a tube") undermines trust and is inappropriate as a first response. Deferring entirely abandons the nursing responsibility to provide nutritional support and therapeutic presence.

**9. C** — Rationale: Linezolid is an antibiotic with MAOI activity. Patients taking MAOIs must avoid tyramine-rich foods to prevent potentially fatal hypertensive crisis. This is the priority dietary teaching for linezolid. Calcium-food avoidance is relevant for fluoroquinolones and tetracyclines; vitamin K consistency and INR monitoring are relevant for warfarin; taking linezolid with food is not required.

**10. B** — Rationale: Persistent significant hyperglycemia (210–240 mg/dL) in a patient on TPN requires provider notification; adding insulin to the TPN formulation or initiating a continuous insulin infusion is the evidence-based response. TPN should not be abruptly discontinued (rebound hypoglycemia risk). Increasing the TPN rate would worsen hyperglycemia. Administering a bolus without notification is outside the scope of nursing practice for this clinical situation.

## Case Study: Nutritional Status Across Settings

### Scene 1: The Surgical Admission

**Background:** Gabriela, a 52-year-old woman with type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and a BMI of 31 kg/m², is admitted for a laparoscopic colectomy for colon cancer. During the nursing admission assessment, Gabriela's MUST score is calculated: BMI score = 0 (BMI > 20), weight loss score = 0 (no weight loss), acute disease score = 2 (she has been NPO for 2 days preoperatively because she was having difficulty eating due to abdominal pain). Total MUST score: 2 (high risk).

Gabriela tells the nurse, "I don't really have an appetite and I've barely eaten this week. I've been trying to manage my blood sugars with my insulin but they've been running higher than normal." Her current morning blood glucose is 218 mg/dL.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. What are Gabriela's two highest nutrition-related nursing priorities on admission?
2. How does Gabriela's diabetes management interact with her current nutritional status, and what nursing assessments are essential in the immediate postoperative period?

### Scene 2: Postoperative Day 3 — Starting Enteral Nutrition

**Background:** Gabriela's surgery is completed without complications. By postoperative day 3, she has not tolerated more than sips of clear liquids, and the surgical team orders a nasogastric tube for enteral nutrition at 50 mL/hour. Before initiating the feeding, the nurse assesses tube placement, confirms the order, and elevates the head of Gabriela's bed to 30 degrees.

At 0600 on postoperative day 4, Gabriela's serum phosphorus returns at 1.2 mg/dL (normal 2.5–4.5 mg/dL), down from 3.0 mg/dL 48 hours earlier. She reports feeling weak and is having difficulty taking deep breaths.

**Discussion Questions:**
3. What complication should the nurse immediately suspect, and what are the nursing priorities?
4. What patient education should Gabriela receive before discharge about continuing oral intake and blood glucose management at home?

### Scene 3: Six Months Post-Surgery — The Outpatient Follow-Up

**Background:** Six months after surgery, Gabriela is seen in the oncology clinic. She has completed adjuvant chemotherapy. She reports fatigue, tingling in her fingers and toes, and "a sore, red tongue." Her recent labs show a hemoglobin of 9.8 g/dL (MCV 108 fL — macrocytic), and her vitamin B12 level is 148 pg/mL (normal 200–900 pg/mL).

**Discussion Questions:**
1. What is the most likely nutritional deficiency, and what is the likely mechanism in this patient?
2. What nursing teaching should accompany the initiation of vitamin B12 supplementation, and what monitoring parameters are appropriate?
