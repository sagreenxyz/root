## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Describe the historical development of the nursing process and explain its significance as the organizing framework for professional nursing practice.
2. Conduct a systematic nursing assessment by collecting and organizing subjective and objective data using a standardized framework.
3. Formulate accurate nursing diagnoses using the NANDA-I taxonomy and the PES (problem–etiology–signs/symptoms) format.
4. Construct measurable, patient-centered goals and expected outcomes that reflect SMART criteria and Maslow's hierarchy of needs.
5. Select and implement independent, dependent, and collaborative nursing interventions using the Nursing Interventions Classification (NIC) framework.
6. Evaluate patient outcomes against established goals and use evaluation findings to revise the plan of care.
7. Apply principles of accurate and legally defensible nursing documentation across the nursing process.
8. Adapt the nursing process to diverse patient populations and clinical settings, including acute care, community health, and home health environments.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| Nursing process | A systematic, evidence-based, five-step problem-solving framework — Assessment, Diagnosis, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation (ADPIE) — that guides individualized nursing care |
| Assessment | The first step of the nursing process; the systematic collection, organization, and documentation of subjective and objective patient data |
| Nursing diagnosis | A clinical judgment about a patient's response to an actual or potential health problem or life process, formulated using standardized NANDA-I language |
| NANDA-I | North American Nursing Diagnosis Association International; the organization that maintains the standardized taxonomy of nursing diagnostic labels |
| PES format | A three-part nursing diagnosis structure: Problem label + Etiology (related to) + Signs and Symptoms (as evidenced by) |
| Planning | The third step of the nursing process; the development of individualized, measurable goals and the selection of nursing interventions to achieve them |
| SMART goals | Goal criteria requiring that outcomes be Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Relevant, and Time-bound |
| Nursing Outcomes Classification (NOC) | A standardized taxonomy of patient outcomes linked to nursing diagnoses, used to measure the effectiveness of nursing care |
| Nursing Interventions Classification (NIC) | A standardized taxonomy of nursing interventions that describes the treatments nurses perform in response to nursing diagnoses |
| Implementation | The fourth step of the nursing process; the execution of the plan of care through independent, dependent, and collaborative nursing interventions |
| Independent intervention | A nursing action that a registered nurse initiates and carries out based on clinical judgment without a physician's order |
| Dependent intervention | A nursing action that requires a physician's or advanced practice provider's order to implement, such as medication administration |
| Collaborative intervention | A nursing action performed jointly with other members of the health care team in response to shared patient problems |
| Evaluation | The fifth step of the nursing process; the systematic reassessment of patient outcomes to determine whether goals have been met and whether the plan requires modification |
| Critical thinking | The deliberate, disciplined process of applying intellectual standards — clarity, accuracy, precision, relevance, depth, and fairness — to clinical reasoning and decision-making |

## Introduction

It is 0630 on a busy medical-surgical floor. A 68-year-old man named Mr. Patel is admitted with a new diagnosis of type 2 diabetes mellitus and a non-healing wound on his right foot. He speaks limited English, lives alone, and expresses considerable anxiety about his future. His blood glucose is 318 mg/dL, his wound shows signs of early cellulitis, and his discharge is anticipated within 48 hours.

How does a nurse know where to begin? Which problem is most urgent? What does success look like? Who needs to be involved? What should be documented, and in what order? Without a systematic framework, a nurse encountering Mr. Patel's complexity might be overwhelmed, reactive, or incomplete — attending to the most visible problem while overlooking the equally important social and educational needs that will determine whether this hospitalization represents a turning point or the first of many.

The **nursing process** — a systematic, evidence-based, five-step framework organized under the acronym **ADPIE** — provides the answer. It transforms the chaos of clinical complexity into an organized, individualized plan of care that is grounded in data, responsive to the whole person, and capable of self-correction. The nursing process is not a rigid checklist; it is a dynamic, cyclical, and deeply professional mode of clinical reasoning that distinguishes nursing from task completion.

The nursing process was first formally described in the 1950s and 1960s and has been refined by decades of nursing scholarship, including the development of standardized nursing languages — NANDA-I, NIC, and NOC — that give the process a common vocabulary. Building on the communication skills of Chapter 4 and the evidence-based practice principles of Chapter 7, this chapter provides both the theoretical foundation and the practical tools to use the nursing process competently in any clinical setting.

The chapter proceeds through each step of ADPIE: Section 8.1 establishes the overview and history; Sections 8.2 through 8.6 examine each step in depth; Section 8.7 addresses documentation; and Section 8.8 explores adaptations across settings and populations.

## Section 8.1 — Overview of the Nursing Process

### 8.1.1 Historical Development

The nursing process did not emerge fully formed from a single theorist's mind. It developed over several decades through the work of nursing leaders responding to the need for a scientific, systematic approach to practice that would define nursing as a profession distinct from medicine.

Lydia Hall first used the phrase "nursing process" in 1955, describing a cyclical approach to nursing care. Ida Jean Orlando's 1961 work *The Dynamic Nurse-Patient Relationship* formalized the interactive dimensions of nursing assessment and response. Dorothy Johnson and Ernestine Wiedenbach extended the process in the early 1960s, and Yura and Walsh published the first comprehensive textbook on the nursing process in 1967, describing the four original steps: Assessment, Planning, Implementation, and Evaluation. The diagnostic step was added formally in the 1970s following the First National Conference on the Classification of Nursing Diagnoses in 1973 — the meeting that would eventually lead to NANDA-I (American Nurses Association [ANA], 2010).

The ANA's *Standards of Clinical Nursing Practice*, first published in 1991 and revised in subsequent editions, formalized the nursing process as the foundational framework for professional nursing and embedded it in the standards of care against which nursing practice is legally and professionally measured. Today, the nursing process is recognized across international nursing bodies, embedded in NCLEX examination blueprints, and taught as the cornerstone of every prelicensure nursing curriculum.

> **Historical Spotlight: From "The Steps" to ADPIE — A Half-Century of Refinement**  
> When Yura and Walsh (1967) described a four-step nursing process, "nursing diagnosis" did not yet exist as a formal construct. Nurses assessed, planned, implemented, and evaluated — but they documented findings in medical language borrowed from physicians. The formation of NANDA in 1973 changed that. Over the next two decades, nursing scholars developed a standardized diagnostic language, followed by the Nursing Interventions Classification (McCloskey & Bulechek, 1992) and the Nursing Outcomes Classification (Johnson & Maas, 1997). The resulting NNN linkages — connecting NANDA-I diagnoses to NIC interventions and NOC outcomes — gave nurses a complete, autonomous clinical language for the first time.

### 8.1.2 The Cyclic, Dynamic Nature of ADPIE

A common misconception is that the nursing process is linear — that a nurse completes assessment, then moves to diagnosis, then planning, then implementation, then evaluation, in a single pass. In reality, the nursing process is **cyclic and dynamic**. Assessment is continuous throughout the patient encounter; new data collected during implementation may prompt revision of the nursing diagnosis; evaluation findings loop back to trigger reassessment and planning modifications.

This dynamic quality is what makes the nursing process responsive to the real-world unpredictability of patient care. A patient whose condition deteriorates during implementation requires immediate reassessment; a patient whose goal is achieved ahead of schedule may require an updated plan with more challenging outcomes. The nurse who understands ADPIE as a cycle — rather than a checklist — is better equipped to respond to the constantly shifting clinical environment.

The nursing process also operates simultaneously at multiple levels of specificity. At the broadest level, it organizes the nurse's entire interaction with a patient across a hospital stay. At a narrower level, each individual nursing intervention — administering a medication, performing a dressing change, conducting discharge teaching — can itself be understood through a mini-ADPIE lens: assess the patient before the intervention, identify the specific problem being addressed, plan the intervention, implement it, and evaluate its effect.

### 8.1.3 Critical Thinking and Clinical Reasoning in ADPIE

The nursing process is, at its core, a structure for **critical thinking** applied to clinical practice. Critical thinking in nursing requires intellectual standards: clarity (what exactly is this patient telling me?), accuracy (is this data correct?), precision (how severe is this pain on a validated scale?), relevance (which of the fifteen problems before me is most urgent?), depth (what underlying pathophysiology explains these findings?), and fairness (am I allowing assumptions about this patient to influence my assessment?).

Paul and Elder's (2008) framework of critical thinking identifies eight elements of reasoning — purpose, question at issue, information, interpretation, concepts, assumptions, implications, and point of view — all of which are engaged during skilled nursing process application. Nursing scholar Alfaro-LeFevre (2019) extended this framework into clinical reasoning, adding the dimension of nursing judgment: the integration of knowledge, experience, and moral agency that transforms data into action.

## Section 8.2 — Assessment: Collecting and Organizing Data

### 8.2.1 Types of Assessment Data

**Assessment** is the systematic, ongoing collection of data about a patient's health status. All assessment data falls into one of two categories. **Subjective data** is information reported by the patient — symptoms, feelings, perceptions, and history that only the patient can supply. "I have a stabbing pain in my chest when I take a deep breath" is subjective data. **Objective data** is observable, measurable information obtained through direct examination, diagnostic testing, or review of records. A respiratory rate of 28 breaths per minute, a temperature of 38.8°C, and a creatinine of 2.4 mg/dL are objective data.

Both types of data are essential. Subjective data provides the patient's perspective and often contains the information most important to understanding the meaning of illness for that individual. Objective data provides measurable, verifiable information against which goals can be measured and outcomes evaluated. Neither type is inherently more valid or more important; the skilled nurse integrates both.

Assessment is also categorized by scope and timing. An **initial comprehensive assessment** — conducted on admission or first contact — provides a baseline picture of the patient's health status across all functional domains. A **focused assessment** targets a specific problem or body system and is performed in response to a change in condition, a new complaint, or before and after an intervention. A **time-lapsed assessment** compares the current status to a previous baseline to identify trends and changes over time.

### 8.2.2 Frameworks for Organizing Assessment Data

Data collected without an organizing framework risks being incomplete or unsystematic. Several frameworks help nurses structure comprehensive assessment.

**Gordon's Functional Health Patterns** (Gordon, 1994) organizes assessment around eleven functional domains: health perception/health management, nutritional/metabolic, elimination, activity/exercise, sleep/rest, cognitive/perceptual, self-perception/self-concept, role/relationship, sexuality/reproductive, coping/stress tolerance, and value/belief. This framework ensures that the nurse's assessment encompasses the whole person — not just the presenting medical complaint — and directly links to nursing (rather than medical) diagnostic categories.

**Head-to-Toe Assessment** organizes the physical examination systematically from the neurological examination through the cardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal, genitourinary, musculoskeletal, and integumentary systems. This approach ensures that no body system is overlooked and is the standard framework for comprehensive physical assessment in acute care settings.

> **Clinical Tip: The OLDCARTS Mnemonic for Symptom Assessment**  
> When assessing a patient's chief complaint or symptom, use OLDCARTS to ensure completeness: **O**nset (when did it start?), **L**ocation (where is it?), **D**uration (how long does it last?), **C**haracter (what does it feel like?), **A**ggravating factors (what makes it worse?), **R**elieving factors (what makes it better?), **T**reatment (what have you tried?), **S**everity (rate it on a scale of 0–10). This structured inquiry prevents the common error of addressing symptoms without understanding their full clinical context.

### 8.2.3 Data Collection Methods and Validation

Assessment data is collected through four primary methods: **interview**, **physical examination**, **observation**, and **record review**. Each method has specific applications and limitations, and the skilled nurse triangulates across methods to achieve an accurate, complete picture.

The nursing health history interview draws on the communication skills detailed in Chapter 4: therapeutic use of open-ended questions, active listening, empathic responses, and culturally sensitive inquiry. The interview is the primary vehicle for collecting subjective data and for establishing the therapeutic relationship that makes all subsequent care possible.

Physical examination uses the techniques of inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation to generate objective data. Observation extends throughout the encounter — the nurse observes gait, affect, skin color, respiratory effort, and the quality of the patient's interaction with family members from the first moment of contact.

**Data validation** — confirming the accuracy of collected data — is a critical but often neglected assessment step. Before drawing conclusions, the nurse asks: Is this data source reliable? Is there a physiological explanation for this finding, or might it represent measurement error? Does the patient's self-report align with objective findings? Discrepancies between subjective and objective data are themselves clinically significant and warrant further exploration rather than dismissal of one data type in favor of the other.

## Section 8.3 — Diagnosis: Identifying Nursing Diagnoses

### 8.3.1 The NANDA-I Taxonomy

**Nursing diagnosis** is the step of the nursing process in which the nurse analyzes collected data to identify patient problems that nursing interventions can address. The **NANDA-I** taxonomy — now in its twelfth edition — provides a standardized language of 267 nursing diagnostic labels organized into 13 domains and 47 classes (Herdman & Kamitsuru, 2018). This standardized language allows nurses across settings, specialties, and countries to communicate precisely about patient problems and to link diagnoses to evidence-based interventions and measurable outcomes.

It is essential to distinguish a nursing diagnosis from a medical diagnosis. A medical diagnosis identifies a pathological condition — community-acquired pneumonia, type 2 diabetes mellitus, acute myocardial infarction. A nursing diagnosis identifies the patient's *response* to that condition — the impaired gas exchange, the deficient knowledge about insulin self-administration, the activity intolerance, the anxiety about the future. Nursing diagnoses address the human response and fall squarely within the independent scope of nursing practice; medical diagnoses are the physician's domain.

> **EBP Connection: NANDA-I Diagnoses and Patient Outcomes**  
> Research consistently demonstrates that the use of standardized nursing diagnostic language improves care quality and patient outcomes. Müller-Staub and colleagues (2007) conducted a randomized controlled trial showing that nurses who used NANDA-I, NIC, and NOC in their practice produced more individualized, more accurate care plans and documented more complete patient responses than nurses using narrative care plans. The study provides Level II evidence supporting the routine use of standardized nursing language in clinical documentation.

### 8.3.2 Types of Nursing Diagnoses

NANDA-I recognizes several types of nursing diagnoses, each with distinct clinical applications.

An **actual nursing diagnosis** describes a patient problem that currently exists, supported by defining characteristics (signs and symptoms) and related factors (etiology). Example: *Impaired skin integrity related to pressure and moisture as evidenced by a 2 cm × 3 cm Stage II pressure injury on the coccyx.*

A **risk nursing diagnosis** describes a vulnerability to a problem that does not yet exist but for which risk factors are present. No signs and symptoms are present because the problem has not yet occurred. Example: *Risk for falls related to lower extremity weakness, altered gait, and multiple antihypertensive medications.* Risk diagnoses require preventive rather than restorative interventions and are equally important as actual diagnoses.

A **health-promotion nursing diagnosis** describes a patient's readiness to adopt behaviors that enhance wellness. Example: *Readiness for enhanced self-care management related to patient's expressed desire to learn insulin administration technique.* Health-promotion diagnoses are especially prevalent in community and primary care settings.

A **syndrome diagnosis** describes a cluster of nursing diagnoses that predictably occur together and respond to similar nursing interventions. Example: *Rape-trauma syndrome* encompasses a predictable cluster of diagnoses — post-trauma response, fear, disturbed personal identity, risk for infection — associated with sexual assault.

### 8.3.3 Writing Nursing Diagnoses Using PES Format

For actual nursing diagnoses, the **PES format** provides a three-part structure that clearly communicates the problem, its cause, and its evidence (Carpenito, 2021; Doenges et al., 2019):

**Problem** (the NANDA-I diagnostic label) + **Etiology** (*related to* [contributing factors]) + **Signs and Symptoms** (*as evidenced by* [defining characteristics from assessment data]).

Example: *Acute pain related to surgical incision and inflammatory response as evidenced by patient rating pain 7/10, guarding behavior, and grimacing on movement.*

Accurate formulation of the etiology is the most clinically important component of the nursing diagnosis because it points directly toward the correct intervention. If the etiology of *Deficient knowledge* is identified as "limited exposure to diabetes self-management information," the intervention is patient education. If the etiology is "low health literacy," the same intervention must be adapted to plain language and teach-back methodology. Two patients with the same diagnostic label may require entirely different interventions depending on their etiology.

## Section 8.4 — Planning: Setting Goals and Outcomes

### 8.4.1 Establishing Patient-Centered Goals

**Planning** is the step in which the nurse — in partnership with the patient, family, and health care team — establishes priorities among nursing diagnoses and develops a plan for addressing them. Planning encompasses two distinct activities: **setting goals and expected outcomes**, and **selecting nursing interventions**.

Patient-centered goal-setting begins with prioritization. Maslow's hierarchy of needs provides one widely used framework: physiological needs (airway, breathing, circulation, nutrition, elimination) take precedence over safety needs, which take precedence over psychosocial needs. An alternate prioritization framework, associated with nursing educator Dorothea Orem, centers self-care deficits. The ANA (2015) and leading nurse theorists agree that patient-identified priorities must be explicitly incorporated — a patient who is more distressed about their inability to care for their children than about their wound dressing may be more motivated to learn wound care as part of returning home than as a clinical outcome per se.

Goals are written at two levels of specificity. **Short-term goals** are achievable within the current care episode (e.g., the hospital stay or the home visit): *By the end of the shift, the patient will rate pain ≤ 4/10 on the numeric rating scale.* **Long-term goals** extend beyond the immediate encounter and guide ongoing care planning: *By discharge, the patient will demonstrate correct insulin injection technique independently on two consecutive observations.*

### 8.4.2 SMART Criteria and NOC Outcomes

Well-constructed goals meet the **SMART** criteria: **S**pecific (describes the behavior to be demonstrated), **M**easurable (includes a verifiable criterion), **A**ttainable (realistic given the patient's condition and resources), **R**elevant (addresses the nursing diagnosis), and **T**ime-bound (specifies a deadline for achievement).

The **Nursing Outcomes Classification (NOC)** provides a standardized taxonomy of over 540 outcomes — each defined by a label, a definition, a set of indicators, and a five-point Likert-type measurement scale — that enables nurses to measure the effectiveness of nursing care with quantitative precision. NOC outcomes are directly linked to NANDA-I diagnoses through validated NNN linkages, allowing the nurse to select outcomes that are evidence-based and clinically appropriate for each specific diagnosis.

> **EBP Connection: SMART Goals and Adherence to Self-Management Behaviors**  
> A systematic review by Shilts and colleagues (2004) found that goal-setting interventions using specific, measurable, proximal goals were significantly more effective than general health education in improving self-management behaviors across multiple chronic disease populations. The application of SMART goal criteria in nursing care planning is therefore not merely a pedagogical convention; it is an evidence-based practice that increases the likelihood of successful patient outcomes.

### 8.4.3 Selecting and Prioritizing Nursing Interventions

Once goals are established, the nurse selects **nursing interventions** — the specific actions that will move the patient toward the desired outcomes. The **Nursing Interventions Classification (NIC)** provides a standardized taxonomy of over 550 interventions, each defined by a label, a definition, and a set of specific nursing activities. NIC interventions span seven domains — physiological basic, physiological complex, behavioral, safety, family, health system, and community — and encompass independent, dependent, and collaborative actions.

Intervention selection is guided by: (1) the etiology of the nursing diagnosis, (2) the characteristics of the patient (age, comorbidities, cultural preferences, health literacy), (3) evidence for intervention effectiveness, (4) available resources, and (5) the patient's own goals and preferences. Multiple interventions are typically selected for each nursing diagnosis and organized in the care plan by priority.

## Section 8.5 — Implementation: Executing the Plan of Care

### 8.5.1 Categories of Nursing Interventions

**Implementation** is the action phase of the nursing process — the performance of the planned nursing interventions. All nursing interventions fall into one of three categories based on the source of authority for the action.

**Independent interventions** are actions within the registered nurse's scope of practice that require no physician's order. They include patient education, therapeutic communication, positioning, ambulation, hygiene assistance, fall prevention, and health promotion counseling. Independent interventions represent the fullest expression of nursing's autonomous professional practice; they are the actions that most clearly distinguish what nurses do from what any other health care discipline does.

**Dependent interventions** require a licensed practitioner's order to perform. Medication administration, IV therapy, and medical procedures are prototypical dependent interventions. The nurse's responsibility in executing dependent interventions extends beyond mere compliance — the nurse must assess whether the order is appropriate for the specific patient, question orders that appear incorrect or unsafe, and monitor the patient's response.

**Collaborative interventions** are performed jointly with other health care team members. Wound care performed in partnership with a wound care specialist, discharge planning conducted with a social worker, or a physical therapy exercise program supervised by both the nurse and the physical therapist are examples of collaborative implementation.

> **Safety Alert: The "Five Rights" Are Not Enough**  
> Traditional medication administration education taught the Five Rights: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time. Contemporary patient safety science has extended this list to include the right documentation, right reason, right response, and right to refuse. The Joint Commission's National Patient Safety Goals consistently identify medication errors as a leading cause of preventable patient harm, and research demonstrates that errors frequently occur not during drug preparation but during the cognitive step of assessment — when the nurse fails to assess for contraindications, relevant allergies, or abnormal laboratory values before administration (The Joint Commission, 2023).

### 8.5.2 Delegation and Supervision

A significant component of implementation in team nursing environments is the appropriate **delegation** of nursing interventions to unlicensed assistive personnel (UAP), including nursing assistants, patient care technicians, and orderlies. The ANA and the National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN) jointly specify that delegation requires careful application of the Five Rights of Delegation: right task, right circumstance, right person, right direction and communication, and right supervision and evaluation (ANA & NCSBN, 2019).

Delegation does not transfer professional accountability. When a registered nurse delegates a task to a UAP, the nurse retains accountability for the outcome of that task and must provide appropriate supervision, clearly communicate expectations, and follow up to ensure the task was performed correctly. Tasks requiring nursing assessment, clinical judgment, or the application of the nursing process — including the formulation of nursing diagnoses and the evaluation of patient responses — cannot be delegated.

### 8.5.3 Reassessment During Implementation

Implementation is not execution-only; it requires continuous **reassessment**. Before initiating any intervention, the nurse conducts a pre-intervention assessment to confirm that the patient's condition still warrants the planned action and that no contraindications have emerged. During the intervention, the nurse observes the patient's response. After the intervention, the nurse reassesses to evaluate its immediate effect.

This continuous reassessment loop is what makes implementation more than task completion. A nurse who administers a scheduled opioid analgesic, reassesses the patient's respiratory rate at 14 breaths per minute and pain score at 8/10, then documents both pre- and post-administration findings is practicing the nursing process at the level of individual intervention execution. A nurse who administers the medication and immediately moves to the next patient without reassessment is executing a task — and missing the evaluation that makes the nursing process a complete cycle.

## Section 8.6 — Evaluation: Measuring Outcomes

### 8.6.1 The Structure of Evaluation

**Evaluation** is the step of the nursing process in which the nurse systematically compares the patient's current status to the goals established during planning and determines the extent to which each goal has been achieved. This comparison requires the same systematic data collection skills used during assessment — physical examination, interview, record review — applied now with the explicit question: Has the desired outcome been met?

Three possible conclusions emerge from evaluation. A goal may be **met**: the patient's pain is now 3/10 after opioid administration, meeting the goal of ≤ 4/10. A goal may be **partially met**: the patient can correctly name three of five warning signs of hypoglycemia but not all five. A goal may be **not met**: the patient remains unable to demonstrate correct insulin injection technique after three education sessions.

Only the "met" conclusion permits discontinuation of the related nursing diagnosis and its interventions. "Partially met" and "not met" conclusions require the nurse to return to earlier steps of the nursing process to determine why the goal was not achieved and what modification is indicated.

> **Clinical Tip: Asking "Why Was the Goal Not Met?" Before Revising the Plan**  
> When a goal is not met, the reflexive impulse is often to repeat the same intervention more frequently or at a higher intensity. Before doing so, the nurse should systematically ask: Was the nursing diagnosis correct? Was the goal realistic and SMART? Was the selected intervention appropriate for this patient's etiology, literacy, and motivation level? Was there an organizational or systems barrier? Careful evaluation analysis often reveals that the problem lies not in the intensity of implementation but in the accuracy of the diagnosis or the fit of the intervention to the patient's specific circumstances.

### 8.6.2 Modifying the Plan of Care

When evaluation reveals that goals are not being met, the nurse initiates **care plan modification**. Modification may involve revising nursing diagnoses (adding new ones, deleting resolved ones, or updating the etiology of existing ones), revising goals (adjusting the time frame or the level of achievement), selecting new or additional interventions, or escalating to collaborative action (consulting with a physician, pharmacist, social worker, or specialist).

The NOC measurement scale is particularly useful during evaluation because it provides a quantified baseline (the patient's score on admission) and a quantified target (the desired score at discharge or at a specified time point), allowing the nurse to track movement toward the goal even when it has not yet been fully achieved.

### 8.6.3 Documenting Evaluation Findings

Evaluation documentation closes the loop of the nursing process and provides the legal and professional record of care delivered and patient response. Every patient goal in the care plan should have a corresponding evaluation entry — not simply "continued" or "no change," but a specific, data-based statement of the patient's current status relative to the goal.

## Section 8.7 — Documentation and the Nursing Process

### 8.7.1 Principles of Nursing Documentation

**Documentation** is simultaneously the nurse's professional record, the legal record of care, the communication tool that bridges across shifts and disciplines, and the data source for quality improvement initiatives. As such, documentation must be accurate, timely, complete, concise, legible (or legibly entered into the electronic health record), and legally defensible.

Accurate documentation means recording what was observed, done, and communicated — not what the nurse intended to do, what was customary to do, or what a standing protocol called for. Timely documentation means recording findings and actions as close to the time of occurrence as possible to minimize memory error and to ensure that the record reflects the patient's condition when clinical decisions were made. Complete documentation captures all five steps of the nursing process: the assessment findings that informed the diagnosis, the goals established during planning, the interventions performed during implementation, and the evaluation findings that close each care cycle.

### 8.7.2 SBAR and Structured Communication

When nursing documentation serves the purpose of handoff communication — shift-to-shift report, transfer reports, telephone communications with physicians — the **SBAR** framework (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) provides a structured template that reduces the risk of critical information loss (Leonard et al., 2004). The SBAR framework maps directly onto the nursing process: Situation and Background draw from assessment; Assessment reflects diagnosis and ongoing evaluation; Recommendation reflects planning and implementation.

The integration of SBAR into nursing education and practice is supported by patient safety literature documenting that unstructured handoffs are associated with preventable adverse events, including medication errors, missed critical findings, and delayed treatment (The Joint Commission, 2023). Nurses who use SBAR as both a communication tool and a mental framework for organizing their nursing process thinking are more likely to communicate relevant information completely and in a clinically useful sequence.

> **Clinical Tip: The Electronic Health Record Is Not the Nursing Process**  
> A common pitfall in contemporary nursing practice is the conflation of electronic health record (EHR) charting with nursing process application. Completing required EHR documentation fields — vital signs, intake and output, medication administration records — is necessary but not sufficient. The nursing process requires synthesis: connecting assessment findings to nursing diagnoses, linking diagnoses to evidence-based interventions, and evaluating outcomes against established goals. This synthesis must be evident in nursing notes, care plan documentation, and shift assessments — not merely in discrete data fields. The EHR is the vehicle for nursing process documentation, not a substitute for nursing process thinking.

### 8.7.3 Legal and Ethical Dimensions of Documentation

Nursing documentation is a legal document. In a malpractice case, the standard of proof is whether the nurse's practice met the standard of care for a reasonably prudent nurse in the same circumstances. Documentation is the primary evidence through which that standard is either established or refuted. The legal principle that "if it wasn't charted, it wasn't done" reflects the reality that verbal testimony about undocumented care is far weaker evidence than contemporaneous written records.

Ethical dimensions of documentation include the obligation of honesty (documentation must reflect what actually occurred, never falsified or retrospectively altered), confidentiality (documentation must comply with HIPAA requirements for protected health information), and accountability (the nurse who documents care takes professional responsibility for the accuracy of that record). Late entries, addenda, and corrections must follow institutional policy and must never obscure the original entry.

## Section 8.8 — The Nursing Process Across Settings and Populations

### 8.8.1 Acute Care Settings

In acute care environments — hospitals, emergency departments, critical care units — the nursing process operates under conditions of high acuity, rapid change, and compressed time. The initial comprehensive assessment may be conducted over minutes rather than hours; nursing diagnoses must be prioritized quickly and care plans activated before all data has been collected. The nursing process in acute care is characterized by its responsiveness: the skilled acute care nurse continually reassesses, reprioritizes, and revises in real time.

High-priority nursing diagnoses in acute care frequently involve the physiological domain: impaired gas exchange, decreased cardiac output, acute pain, risk for infection, impaired physical mobility. However, the nurse who attends only to physiological diagnoses in acute care misses equally important psychosocial problems — anxiety, fear, disturbed body image, interrupted family processes — that profoundly affect recovery and long-term outcomes. The nursing process demands a holistic lens even in the most physically acute environments.

### 8.8.2 Community and Primary Care Settings

In community and primary care settings, the nursing process is applied over longer time horizons with greater emphasis on health promotion, chronic disease management, and self-care support. Assessment in these settings draws heavily on health history, functional status, social determinants of health, and patient-reported outcomes. Nursing diagnoses in primary care frequently address readiness for enhanced self-care management, deficient community health, impaired health maintenance, and risk diagnoses related to chronic disease complications.

Goal-setting in community settings must be realistic across much longer time frames and must account for the patient's life context — work schedule, family obligations, financial resources, transportation, and health literacy — in ways that acute care planning often does not. Implementation in community nursing relies heavily on independent interventions: health education, motivational interviewing, care coordination, community resource referral, and advocacy.

> **Clinical Reasoning: Applying the Nursing Process to a Community Health Visit**  
> A public health nurse visiting a 72-year-old woman with hypertension and diabetes who has missed her last three clinic appointments applies ADPIE as follows: **Assessment** reveals uncontrolled blood pressure (158/96 mmHg), medication non-adherence, and a home environment with no food in the refrigerator. **Diagnosis** identifies *Ineffective health management related to financial barriers, transportation limitations, and competing caregiver responsibilities* and *Imbalanced nutrition: less than body requirements related to food insecurity*. **Planning** sets short-term goals for same-day connection to food assistance programs and next-week clinic appointment. **Implementation** includes motivational interviewing, community resource referral, and coordination with the clinic for a telehealth visit. **Evaluation** at the next home visit confirms the patient has received emergency food assistance and attended a telehealth appointment. This sequence illustrates that the nursing process is not limited to the hospital — it is the organizing framework for all professional nursing practice.

### 8.8.3 Lifespan Adaptations

The nursing process is equally applicable across all age groups, but its content — the specific nursing diagnoses that are most prevalent, the appropriate goals and interventions, the communication strategies required — must be adapted to the developmental stage of the patient.

In **pediatric** nursing, developmental norms provide the baseline against which assessment findings are compared. Growth and developmental screening, family assessment, and parental education are central components of the assessment and implementation steps. Many nursing diagnoses in pediatric care address caregiver knowledge and family function in addition to the child's individual health status.

In **older adult** nursing, comprehensive geriatric assessment incorporates functional status measures (Activities of Daily Living, Instrumental Activities of Daily Living), cognitive screening (Mini-Cog, Montreal Cognitive Assessment), fall risk assessment (Morse Fall Scale, Hendrich II), and social support evaluation alongside the standard nursing assessment. Common nursing diagnoses in older adults include risk for falls, impaired physical mobility, chronic pain, social isolation, and caregiver role strain. Goal-setting must account for the complex interplay of multimorbidity, polypharmacy, and the patient's own priorities — which for many older adults center on maintaining function, independence, and quality of life rather than cure or longevity.

## Chapter Summary

The nursing process — Assessment, Diagnosis, Planning, Implementation, Evaluation (ADPIE) — is the foundational framework of professional nursing practice. It transforms clinical complexity into organized, individualized, evidence-based care that is patient-centered, scientifically grounded, and capable of self-correction.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. **History and purpose:** The nursing process was formalized between the 1950s and 1970s and embedded in ANA standards of practice; it defines nursing as a profession distinct from medicine and as a systematic clinical science.
2. **Documentation:** Accurate, timely, and complete documentation across all five steps of the nursing process constitutes the professional and legal record of care — reflected in nursing notes, care plans, and shift assessments, not merely in discrete electronic health record data fields.
3. **Assessment:** Comprehensive nursing assessment collects both subjective and objective data using frameworks such as Gordon's Functional Health Patterns and validates findings before drawing conclusions.
4. **Diagnosis:** Nursing diagnoses using NANDA-I language describe patient responses — not medical conditions — using PES format that links problem, etiology, and defining characteristics to guide individualized intervention selection.
5. **Planning:** SMART goals and NOC outcomes provide measurable targets; NIC interventions provide evidence-based actions; Maslow's hierarchy guides prioritization across competing diagnoses.
6. **Implementation:** Interventions are categorized as independent, dependent, or collaborative; delegation requires application of the Five Rights of Delegation; continuous reassessment during implementation integrates evaluation into every action.
7. **Evaluation:** Goal-attainment comparison drives plan modification; "not met" conclusions require systematic analysis of diagnosis accuracy, goal realism, and intervention fit before plan revision.
8. **Adaptation:** The nursing process applies across all clinical settings and patient populations, with adaptations in assessment content, diagnostic priorities, goal time frames, and intervention strategies based on setting, acuity, and patient developmental stage.

## Case Study: Starting From the Beginning

### Scene 1: Admission Assessment

Marcus Webb is a 52-year-old construction supervisor admitted to the medical-surgical unit at 2200 following an outpatient colonoscopy that identified a 3 cm polyp requiring surgical excision. He is scheduled for laparoscopic colon resection in the morning. Marcus has a history of hypertension and hyperlipidemia. He is 5'11", weighs 230 pounds (BMI 32.1), smokes one pack of cigarettes per day for 30 years, and drinks three to four beers nightly. He is anxious and asks repeatedly, "Is this cancer? Am I going to be okay?" His vital signs: blood pressure 158/96 mmHg, heart rate 90 bpm, SpO₂ 97% on room air, respiratory rate 16, temperature 37.1°C.

**Scene 1 questions:**

1. Using Gordon's Functional Health Patterns, identify at least four functional domains that require focused assessment based on the information provided. For each domain, specify the type of data (subjective or objective) and at least one specific assessment question or finding.

2. Which nursing diagnosis would receive the highest priority based on the assessment data? State the full PES-format diagnosis and explain the rationale for prioritization.

### Scene 2: The Night Before Surgery

It is now 2330. Marcus's blood pressure remains elevated at 162/94 mmHg despite his evening antihypertensive dose. He tells the night nurse, "I can't sleep. I keep thinking about my wife and kids. What if something goes wrong?" He discloses that he has not told his family about the colonoscopy results because "I didn't want to scare them." He also admits he had "a couple drinks before the procedure today."

**Scene 2 questions:**

3. Two new nursing diagnoses emerge from the night assessment: *Anxiety related to surgical outcome uncertainty and undisclosed diagnosis as evidenced by patient verbalization, inability to sleep, and elevated vital signs* and *Risk for alcohol withdrawal related to daily alcohol consumption and NPO status.* For each diagnosis, write one SMART short-term goal and select two nursing interventions.

4. How should the nurse approach Marcus's disclosure about alcohol use? Identify the relevant nursing assessment tool, the clinical significance of the finding, and the collaborative interventions that should be initiated before surgery.

### Scene 3: Postoperative Day 1 Evaluation

Marcus has returned from surgery and is 18 hours post-laparoscopic colon resection. He is alert and oriented. His pain is 5/10 with the current analgesic regimen. He has ambulated to the chair once with assistance. His surgical incision site is clean and intact. His vital signs are stable. The nursing goal set preoperatively for anxiety — "patient will verbalize at least one effective coping strategy and state reduced anxiety before surgery" — was met. The pain management goal — "patient will rate pain ≤ 3/10 with ambulation by postoperative day 1" — has not been met.

**Discussion questions:**

- How should the nurse document the evaluation of both goals in the medical record?
- For the unmet pain management goal, walk through the steps of care plan modification: reassessment, diagnosis revision, goal revision, and intervention adjustment.
- Which new nursing diagnoses should be added to Marcus's postoperative care plan, and how should they be prioritized?
- Marcus tells the nurse he wants to be discharged in 24 hours to "get back to his family." How does patient-identified priority influence the planning and implementation steps for the remainder of his hospitalization?
