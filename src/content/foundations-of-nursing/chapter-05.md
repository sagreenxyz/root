## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Distinguish between cultural competence and cultural humility, and explain why cultural humility represents a more robust framework for inclusive nursing practice.
2. Describe the relationships among culture, health beliefs, and health disparities in the United States.
3. Assess patient health literacy using validated tools and adapt teaching strategies to match literacy levels.
4. Demonstrate culturally responsive communication techniques that honor diverse patient populations.
5. Integrate knowledge of religion and spirituality into patient-centered care planning.
6. Apply LGBTQ+-affirming practices to create safe and inclusive health care environments.
7. Identify the unique communication and care needs of patients with physical, sensory, or cognitive disabilities.
8. Analyze systemic factors that perpetuate health inequities and describe the nurse's role in policy advocacy and equitable systems design.

## Introduction

Imagine two patients admitted to the same medical-surgical unit on the same day with the same diagnosis — community-acquired pneumonia. The first patient, a 55-year-old White male college professor with private insurance, receives detailed discharge instructions, has a follow-up appointment arranged, and leaves with a prescription and a handout in plain language. The second patient, a 55-year-old Somali immigrant woman with limited English proficiency, a seventh-grade reading level, and Medicaid coverage, receives the same printed discharge instructions — in English — nods when asked if she understands, and returns to the emergency department four days later with worsening symptoms. Same diagnosis. Profoundly different outcome.

This scenario, replicated millions of times each year in health care systems across the United States, illustrates why culturally responsive and literacy-informed nursing is not a specialty skill or a cultural sensitivity elective — it is a fundamental component of safe, effective, patient-centered care. The failure to deliver care that accounts for language, culture, health literacy, and social context is not neutral. It produces preventable harm.

**Cultural humility** — the ongoing practice of recognizing the limitations of one's cultural knowledge, centering the patient's own understanding, and committing to continuous learning — is the framework through which this chapter approaches inclusive care. It is distinguished from earlier models of **cultural competence** in important ways that this chapter will explore. Building on the communication skills established in Chapter 4, this chapter examines how those skills must be adapted for patients whose cultural backgrounds, health beliefs, literacy levels, religious identities, sexual orientations, gender identities, and physical abilities differ from the mainstream assumptions embedded in many health care systems.

The chapter proceeds from theoretical foundations through practical application. Section 5.1 establishes the conceptual framework. Sections 5.2 and 5.3 address health beliefs, disparities, and literacy. Sections 5.4 through 5.7 explore specific populations and contexts. Section 5.8 connects individual nursing practice to the systemic policy advocacy necessary to create genuinely equitable health care.

## Section 5.1 — Foundations of Cultural Competence and Cultural Humility

### 5.1.1 The Evolution from Competence to Humility

The concept of **cultural competence** emerged in health care in the 1980s as a response to documented disparities in care for racial and ethnic minority populations. The Cross Continuum of Cultural Competence (Cross et al., 1989) described cultural competence as a developmental spectrum from cultural destructiveness through cultural blindness to cultural proficiency, and identified knowledge, skills, and attitudes as the three pillars of culturally competent practice. This framework drove decades of diversity training in nursing education and health care organizations.

Despite its influence, the cultural competence model has been criticized on several grounds. Chief among them is the implication that culture can be "mastered" — that a nurse who learns the dietary restrictions of a particular religious group or the communication preferences of a specific ethnic community has become competent to care for all members of that group. This assumption leads to stereotyping: reducing individuals to their presumed cultural characteristics and ignoring the immense diversity within any cultural, ethnic, or religious group. Cultural competence training that teaches nurses "Latinos value family involvement in decision-making" may actually diminish individualized care by creating rigid assumptions.

**Cultural humility**, a concept introduced by Tervalon and Murray-García (1998), offers a corrective framework. Cultural humility is not a destination but a practice — a lifelong commitment to:

1. **Self-reflection and critique:** Examining one's own cultural assumptions, biases, and privileges, recognizing that every nurse enters the clinical encounter with a cultural lens that shapes perception.
2. **Centering the patient's perspective:** Approaching each patient as the expert on their own cultural identity, health beliefs, and preferences, rather than applying external assumptions.
3. **Attending to power imbalances:** Acknowledging the structural power differential between health care providers and patients, and actively working to create partnerships rather than hierarchies.
4. **Lifelong learning:** Understanding that cultural knowledge is never complete and that genuine inclusivity requires continuous engagement, reflection, and revision.

> **Historical Spotlight: The Tuskegee Syphilis Study and Institutional Mistrust**  
> From 1932 to 1972, the U.S. Public Health Service conducted a study in Macon County, Alabama, in which 399 Black men with syphilis were observed without receiving treatment — even after penicillin became the standard of care in 1947. Participants were deceived about the nature of the study and were actively prevented from receiving treatment. The study's exposure in 1972 triggered federal reforms including the creation of Institutional Review Boards, but its legacy — profound and historically grounded mistrust of the medical establishment among African American communities — persists to this day. Nurses who understand this history are better equipped to respond to vaccine hesitancy, clinical trial participation concerns, and reluctance to seek care as rational responses to real events, not irrational superstition (Reverby, 2009).

### 5.1.2 Cultural Humility in Nursing Practice

Translating cultural humility from a philosophical stance into clinical behavior requires specific practices. Central among these is **asking rather than assuming**: instead of inferring a patient's dietary preferences from their religious identity, the nurse asks, "Are there any foods or beverages that are important for us to consider in your care?" A complementary tool is the **LEARN model** (Berlin & Fowkes, 1983), which guides the nurse to Listen to the patient's perspective, Explain their own understanding, Acknowledge differences and similarities, Recommend a plan, and Negotiate an agreement. **Eliciting the explanatory model** is equally essential: Arthur Kleinman's (1988) questions — "What do you call this problem? What do you think caused it? What do you fear most about it? What kind of treatment do you think you should receive?" — invite patients to share their illness frameworks, surfacing cultural beliefs that may affect adherence and shared decision-making. Finally, **reflecting on personal biases** through tools such as the Implicit Association Test (IAT) and structured self-reflection exercises helps clinicians identify unconscious associations that may affect clinical judgment; research consistently demonstrates that implicit racial bias influences pain assessment, treatment recommendations, and diagnostic accuracy (FitzGerald & Hurst, 2017).

## Section 5.2 — Culture, Health Beliefs, and Health Disparities

### 5.2.1 Culture and the Experience of Illness

Culture profoundly shapes how individuals experience, interpret, and respond to illness. It influences whether a person perceives a symptom as significant, where they seek care (physician, traditional healer, religious leader, pharmacist), how they express pain, which family members are involved in decision-making, and what treatments are acceptable. Nurses who are unaware of these dynamics may misinterpret culturally shaped behaviors as non-compliance, psychiatric disturbance, or intellectual limitation.

**Acculturation** — the degree to which individuals from one cultural background have adopted the values and behaviors of a dominant culture — adds further complexity. First-generation immigrants may hold traditional health beliefs more strongly than their children; highly acculturated individuals may have adopted mainstream health behaviors while retaining specific cultural practices in times of illness. Acculturation is not a linear or uniform process, and nurses should not assume that fluency in English indicates alignment with Western biomedical models of illness.

**Explanatory models** vary significantly across cultural groups. In many Asian, Latin American, and Middle Eastern cultures, illness is understood through frameworks that emphasize imbalance — between hot and cold (traditional Chinese medicine, Ayurvedic medicine, Latino folk medicine), between spiritual harmony and disharmony (many Indigenous healing traditions), or between divine will and human failing (some Christian and Islamic frameworks). These frameworks are not simply superstitions to be corrected; they are sophisticated systems of meaning that influence patient behavior and that, when respected and incorporated, can improve therapeutic alliance and adherence.

### 5.2.2 Health Disparities: Scope and Causes

**Health disparities** — preventable differences in the burden of disease, injury, and death experienced by disadvantaged populations — represent one of the most serious ethical failures of the American health care system. The evidence is extensive and consistent. Black Americans die from cardiovascular disease, diabetes, and preventable infections at rates significantly higher than White Americans (Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality [AHRQ], 2021). American Indian and Alaska Native populations face the highest rates of infant mortality and unintentional injury death among all racial/ethnic groups. Hispanic and Latino Americans are disproportionately uninsured, limiting access to preventive care and early treatment. Furthermore, LGBTQ+ individuals experience higher rates of depression, anxiety, substance use, and avoidance of health care compared to cisgender heterosexual peers. Rural populations across all racial/ethnic groups face shortages of primary care providers, specialist access, and emergency services.

The causes of health disparities are complex and interconnected. **Social determinants of health** — the conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work, and age — are responsible for an estimated 30–55% of variation in health outcomes (World Health Organization, 2008). These determinants include income and wealth, educational attainment, housing quality and stability, neighborhood safety, access to nutritious food, and exposure to environmental toxins. Biological factors explain relatively little of the disparity burden; structural racism, historical disinvestment, and inequitable policy decisions explain considerably more.

> **EBP Connection: The Social Determinants Screening Imperative**  
> The American Academy of Pediatrics, the American College of Physicians, and multiple nursing organizations now recommend that clinical encounters routinely screen for social determinants of health using validated tools such as the PRAPARE (Protocol for Responding to and Assessing Patients' Assets, Risks, and Experiences) or the SEEK (Safe Environment for Every Kid) screening tools. Studies demonstrate that screening and subsequent referral to community resources reduce emergency department utilization and improve chronic disease management. Nurses in all settings can implement SDOH screening as a standard component of admission assessment.

### 5.2.3 Structural Racism and Health Inequity

**Health equity** — the attainment of the highest possible level of health for every person — requires acknowledging that disparities are not the result of individual choices or inherent biological differences but are produced and maintained by structural inequities: unequal access to quality education and employment, discriminatory housing policies (including historical redlining), mass incarceration, and differential treatment within health care systems themselves.

Research demonstrates that Black patients receive less pain medication than White patients with equivalent conditions (Hoffman et al., 2016), that implicit bias affects the diagnostic accuracy of clinical examinations, and that Black women are significantly more likely to die from pregnancy-related complications than White women, regardless of education and income level (Petersen et al., 2019). These disparities are not incidental — they are embedded in clinical algorithms, institutional policies, and provider behaviors.

The nurse's role in addressing structural racism includes delivering individualized, bias-aware care at the bedside; advocating for equitable institutional policies; participating in quality improvement initiatives that stratify outcomes data by race and ethnicity; and supporting community health initiatives that address the upstream determinants of health.

## Section 5.3 — Health Literacy: Assessment and Impact

### 5.3.1 Defining and Measuring Health Literacy

**Health literacy** is defined by the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (2010) as the degree to which individuals can obtain, process, and understand basic health information and services needed to make appropriate health decisions. The concept encompasses not only reading ability but also numeracy (understanding medication dosing, blood glucose levels, insurance co-pays), oral communication, and the capacity to navigate health care systems.

The scope of low health literacy is striking: the 2003 National Assessment of Adult Literacy (NAAL) found that 36% of U.S. adults had "basic" or "below basic" health literacy — meaning they could not reliably understand prescription labels, understand a hospital consent form, or interpret a medication dosing chart (Kutner et al., 2006). Low health literacy is disproportionately prevalent among older adults, individuals with limited formal education, non-native English speakers, and people living in poverty — but it occurs across all demographic groups and cannot be reliably detected by educational level, race, or appearance.

Validated tools for screening health literacy include several widely used instruments. The **REALM (Rapid Estimate of Adult Literacy in Medicine)** is a word recognition test in which patients read aloud a list of medical words, scored by the number of words correctly pronounced. The **TOHFLA (Test of Functional Health Literacy in Adults)** assesses reading comprehension and numeracy using actual health care materials. The **Newest Vital Sign (NVS)** uses a nutrition label to assess literacy and numeracy and takes approximately three minutes to administer. Additionally, **single-item screeners** — such as "How often do you have someone help you read hospital materials?" — predict limited literacy with moderate accuracy and can be used for rapid screening.

### 5.3.2 The Impact of Low Health Literacy on Outcomes

Low health literacy is one of the most powerful predictors of poor health outcomes, independent of other factors. Patients with low health literacy are less likely to take medications correctly, less likely to receive preventive services such as cancer screening, and less likely to effectively manage chronic conditions. They are more likely to be hospitalized, more likely to delay seeking care until conditions become severe, and more likely to misunderstand discharge instructions (Berkman et al., 2011).

The health literacy environment — the complexity of health information materials, the design of health care settings, and the communication practices of providers — is as important as the individual's literacy skills. A health care system designed with the assumption that patients have college-level reading ability will produce poor outcomes for a substantial portion of the population regardless of how much effort those patients invest.

> **Clinical Tip: The "Universal Precautions" Approach to Health Literacy**  
> Just as infection control applies universal precautions regardless of whether a patient is known to carry a pathogen, health literacy experts recommend applying universal health literacy precautions to every patient interaction. This means: using plain language in all communication; limiting information to three to five key points; always using teach-back; providing written materials at a 5th–6th grade reading level; using visual aids and models; and creating a welcoming environment where patients feel safe to say "I don't understand." This approach removes the stigma of identifying individual patients as having low literacy and improves outcomes for all patients.

### 5.3.3 Plain Language and Written Health Education Materials

Plain language is defined by the Plain Writing Act of 2010 as communication that "the intended audience can understand and act on the first time they read or hear it." Key principles include using common words instead of medical jargon — for example, "heart attack" instead of "myocardial infarction," or "once a day" instead of "once daily" — and writing in short, active sentences of ideally 15–20 words or fewer. Effective materials also employ a clear visual hierarchy with headers and white space, incorporate pictures, diagrams, or icons to supplement text, and target a 5th–6th grade reading level using validated readability formulas such as Flesch-Kincaid or the SMOG index.

Nurses are frequently the last provider to review discharge instructions with patients before they leave the health care setting. This conversation — often rushed, often following a stressful hospitalization — represents the last opportunity to ensure the patient can safely manage their condition at home. Slowing down, sitting at eye level, using teach-back, and asking "What questions do you have?" (rather than "Do you have any questions?") are evidence-based practices that meaningfully improve comprehension and safe self-management.

## Section 5.4 — Culturally Responsive Communication

### 5.4.1 Language Access and Professional Interpreter Services

For patients with **limited English proficiency (LEP)**, language barriers in health care are associated with longer hospital stays, higher rates of adverse events, reduced patient satisfaction, and worse clinical outcomes (Jacobs et al., 2006). Federal law — specifically Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 — requires health care organizations receiving federal funding (which includes virtually all hospitals) to provide language access services at no cost to the patient.

Professional interpreter services — in person, telephone, or video — must be used for all clinical encounters involving diagnosis, treatment, informed consent, and discharge planning. Family members, friends, and untrained bilingual staff should not be used as interpreters in clinical contexts. Family members may filter, minimize, or editorialize information out of protectiveness, shame, or conflict of interest; furthermore, minor children should never be placed in the role of translating sensitive or distressing medical information for parents. Untrained bilingual staff members lack the medical vocabulary and interpreter ethics training of professional interpreters, and using any informal interpreter may violate the patient's right to confidentiality regarding sensitive health information.

Nurses must be proactive in arranging professional interpreter services, not reactive. Before beginning a clinical encounter with an LEP patient, identify the patient's preferred language, arrange the appropriate interpreter modality, and document interpreter use in the medical record.

### 5.4.2 Nonverbal Communication Across Cultures

As Chapter 4 established, communication is substantially nonverbal. Cultural variation in the interpretation of nonverbal cues is significant and can lead to misunderstanding in clinical encounters. **Eye contact**, for instance, is valued as a sign of engagement and honesty in many Western cultures but may be considered disrespectful, aggressive, or inappropriate with authority figures in many East Asian, Southeast Asian, Native American, and Middle Eastern cultures. **Personal space** preferences vary similarly; what feels professionally appropriate in one cultural context may feel invasive or cold in another. **Touch** during assessment and care should be approached with sensitivity to cultural and religious practices, and nurses should proactively ask about preferences for same-gender care providers for intimate assessments. **Silence**, too, carries cultural meaning — in many cultures it is an expression of respect, consideration, or disagreement rather than a communication gap to be filled. Finally, **head nodding** does not universally indicate agreement or comprehension; in some cultures, nodding indicates only that the listener is attending, not that they understand or consent.

### 5.4.3 Cross-Cultural Pain Assessment

Pain assessment presents particular cultural challenges. The numeric pain scale (0–10) is a Western biomedical instrument that assumes a linear, individually experienced, verbally reported phenomenon. Research demonstrates that many cultures — including many South Asian, East Asian, and some Latin American cultures — encourage stoicism in pain expression, while others — including some Mediterranean and Middle Eastern cultures — express pain more vocally and physically. Neither pattern is inherently more or less accurate; both require the nurse to go beyond the numeric rating and assess functional impact, behavioral cues, and the patient's own meaning-making about their pain.

The Wong-Baker FACES Pain Scale, which uses facial expressions, and behavioral observation scales (e.g., FLACC, PAINAD) offer alternatives for patients who cannot reliably use numeric self-report. Regardless of tool, the nurse's cultural humility requires the willingness to ask: "How would you describe the pain? What does this pain mean to you? What makes it better or worse?"

## Section 5.5 — Religion, Spirituality, and Health Care

### 5.5.1 Distinguishing Religion, Spirituality, and Religiosity

**Religion** refers to organized systems of beliefs, practices, and rituals associated with a community of faith. **Spirituality** is a broader concept referring to an individual's search for meaning, purpose, and connection — which may be expressed through religious practice, or through secular activities such as relationships, nature, art, or philosophical reflection. Nursing assessments address both.

Research consistently demonstrates that spirituality and religion are significant resources for patients facing serious illness, disability, and end-of-life. Spiritual well-being is associated with lower rates of depression, higher quality of life, better coping, and — in some populations — a preference for less aggressive end-of-life care (Phelps et al., 2009). Ignoring the spiritual dimension of the patient's experience is not neutral; it leaves a significant resource unmobilized and communicates that the nurse is interested in only a portion of the person.

### 5.5.2 Spiritual Assessment in Nursing Practice

The FICA Spiritual History Tool (Puchalski & Romer, 2000) is one of the most widely used frameworks for nursing spiritual assessment. The tool guides the nurse through four domains. **F — Faith and Belief** asks, "Do you consider yourself spiritual or religious? Do you have spiritual beliefs that help you cope with stress?" **I — Importance** explores, "How important are these beliefs to you? Do they influence how you make decisions about your health care?" **C — Community** inquires, "Are you part of a spiritual or religious community? Is this community a source of support?" Finally, **A — Address in Care** asks, "How would you like me to address these issues in your care?"

The FICA tool is a starting point, not a comprehensive spiritual evaluation — chaplaincy referral is appropriate when significant spiritual distress, conflict between spiritual beliefs and care preferences, or requests for religious rites are identified.

> **Clinical Tip: Common Religious Practices Affecting Clinical Care**  
> Nurses should be familiar with the health care implications of common religious practices. Jehovah's Witnesses may decline blood products — ensure refusal of transfusion documentation is in the chart. Muslim patients observe fasting (Ramadan) and specific gender preferences in care; coordinate medication timing and request same-gender care providers when possible. Jewish patients may observe Sabbath restrictions that affect willingness to engage with electronics or certain procedures from Friday sundown to Saturday sundown. Christian Scientists may decline conventional medical care — involve the care team in respectful, honest conversations about treatment options while honoring the patient's autonomy. This knowledge should inform, not replace, individualized patient inquiry.

### 5.5.3 Spiritual Distress: Recognizing and Responding

Spiritual distress is defined by NANDA-International as an impaired ability to experience and integrate meaning and purpose in life through connections with self, others, art, music, literature, nature, or a power greater than oneself. Clinical indicators include expressions of hopelessness, loss of meaning, feeling abandoned by a higher power, anger directed at God or religious leaders, questions about the meaning of suffering, and withdrawal from spiritual practices that previously provided comfort.

When spiritual distress is identified, the nurse's role encompasses several important responses. First and foremost, the nurse acknowledges and validates the distress without attempting to resolve or rationalize the patient's experience. Sitting quietly with the patient is often more therapeutic than words; presence alone communicates that the nurse views the whole person, not merely the medical condition. The nurse also facilitates chaplain or spiritual care referral and supports the patient's access to religious practices, rites, and community to the extent clinically feasible. All spiritual assessment findings and referrals should be documented in the care plan.

## Section 5.6 — LGBTQ+ Inclusive Nursing Care

### 5.6.1 LGBTQ+ Health Disparities and Clinical Context

Lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer, and other gender-diverse (LGBTQ+) individuals experience substantial and well-documented health disparities relative to their cisgender heterosexual peers. These disparities are not the result of inherent differences in biology or behavior but reflect the consequences of minority stress — chronic exposure to discrimination, stigma, violence, and social exclusion — and structural barriers to affirming health care.

Key disparities are documented across subgroups. Transgender and gender-nonconforming individuals report high rates of discrimination in health care settings; a 2020 survey found that 33% of transgender adults avoided needed health care due to fear of discrimination (James et al., 2016). LGBTQ+ youth have disproportionately high rates of depression, anxiety, suicidality, and homelessness relative to cisgender heterosexual peers. Bisexual individuals, often overlooked in both clinical and research contexts, have higher rates of depression, anxiety, and intimate partner violence than either heterosexual or gay/lesbian individuals. Additionally, LGBTQ+ older adults face unique challenges including elevated rates of social isolation, fewer legally recognized family relationships, and historical-era trauma from the criminalization of same-sex relationships and psychiatric pathologization.

### 5.6.2 Creating Affirming Health Care Environments

**Affirming care** for LGBTQ+ patients begins before the clinical encounter. The physical environment of the health care setting communicates whether diverse identities are welcomed. Evidence-based environmental practices include displaying rainbow symbols, Pride flags, or Safe Zone stickers in patient care areas and registration desks, and ensuring gender-neutral restrooms are accessible and visibly marked. In addition, intake forms should be updated to collect sexual orientation and gender identity (SOGI) data using inclusive terminology, and all staff — not only clinical staff — should receive training in LGBTQ+-affirming communication.

Within the clinical encounter, affirming practices begin with **using correct pronouns and names**: prior to any clinical interaction, the nurse asks, "What is your preferred name? What pronouns do you use?", documents this information in the patient's chart, and communicates it to the care team. Using a person's deadname or incorrect pronouns is harmful and communicates disrespect. **Avoiding assumptions** is equally essential — rather than assuming a patient's relationship or family structure matches heterosexual norms, asking "Do you have a partner or significant other?" is more inclusive than "Are you married?" Nurses should also **ensure privacy during sensitive assessments**, as transgender patients may experience significant distress during physical examinations involving body parts that do not align with their gender identity; explaining each step, allowing the patient to control the pace, and minimizing unnecessary exposure are important practices. Furthermore, **cervical cancer screening guidance must be adapted**: transgender men who still have a cervix require screening per standard guidelines but may avoid care due to gender dysphoria triggered by gynecological encounters, and nurses can support these patients by using gender-neutral language and offering trauma-informed approaches.

> **EBP Connection: LGBTQ+ Inclusive Documentation in EHRs**  
> The Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology (ONC) now recommends that EHR systems collect sexual orientation and gender identity data using two-step methodology: first asking about current gender identity (man, woman, non-binary, etc.) and second asking about sex assigned at birth. Research demonstrates that collecting SOGI data enables health systems to identify and address disparities, improve care coordination for transgender patients on hormone therapy, and ensure all screening recommendations are applied based on anatomy rather than gender identity (Cahill et al., 2017).

### 5.6.3 Caring for Transgender and Gender-Diverse Patients

Transgender and gender-diverse patients may be at various stages of gender transition: social transition (name and pronoun change), medical transition (hormone therapy), and/or surgical transition. Nurses should understand several key clinical implications. Regarding **hormone therapy**, transgender women on estrogen therapy have altered cardiovascular risk profiles, while transgender men on testosterone therapy experience changes in hematocrit, lipid profiles, and bone density; medication reconciliation should include hormone therapies, which patients may not volunteer without being asked. **Screening recommendations** must be applied based on anatomy rather than gender marker: a transgender man with a uterus requires cervical cancer screening, whereas a transgender woman who has not had vaginoplasty does not require cervical screening but may require prostate cancer screening. Finally, **mental health support** is an essential component of care; gender dysphoria — distress resulting from incongruence between gender identity and assigned sex — is not a mental illness, but transgender individuals have elevated rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidality, making screening and referral indispensable.

## Section 5.7 — Caring for Patients with Disabilities

### 5.7.1 Disability: Models and Perspectives

**Disability** encompasses a broad range of physical, sensory, cognitive, and psychiatric impairments. The conceptual model through which disability is understood has profound implications for nursing practice. The **biomedical model** frames disability as a deficit residing within the individual — a problem to be fixed, cured, or minimized. The **social model of disability**, developed by the disability rights movement, frames disability as the product of the interaction between individual impairment and societal barriers — physical barriers, attitudinal barriers, and systemic barriers that prevent full participation.

For nursing, the social model demands that nurses ask not only "What is wrong with this patient?" but "What barriers does this environment create for this patient, and what can I do to reduce them?" This reframing shifts the locus of the problem from the individual to the system — a shift consistent with cultural humility and with the Healthy People 2030 goals for disability and health.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990) requires health care organizations to provide reasonable accommodations for individuals with disabilities, including accessible examination tables, accessible health information in alternative formats, and sign language interpreters for deaf patients. Nurses play a role in identifying accommodation needs and ensuring the clinical environment fulfills its legal obligations.

### 5.7.2 Patients with Sensory Impairments

**Deaf and hard-of-hearing patients** require particular attention to communication access. American Sign Language (ASL) is a distinct language with its own grammar and structure; many Deaf individuals identify as members of a cultural and linguistic minority community rather than as persons with a disability. Written communication may not be adequate for clinical encounters involving complex information — a professional ASL interpreter or video remote interpreting (VRI) service must be provided.

Key practices for deaf and hard-of-hearing patients include facing the patient when speaking and ensuring adequate lighting on the nurse's face. The nurse should speak clearly at a natural pace without exaggerating mouth movements, and should not shout, as shouting may distort sound for patients who use hearing aids or cochlear implants. Hearing aids should be confirmed in place and functioning before clinical conversations begin. Visual communication tools such as written notes, whiteboards, and tablets serve as supplements to — not replacements for — professional interpreting services.

**Patients with visual impairments** require verbal descriptions of the clinical environment ("The call light is on your right, approximately one foot from your hand"), preparation before physical contact ("I'm going to take your blood pressure now"), and health information provided in alternative formats (large print, audio recordings, or accessible electronic formats compatible with screen readers).

### 5.7.3 Patients with Cognitive and Communication Disabilities

Patients with intellectual disabilities, acquired brain injury, autism spectrum disorder, or communication disorders retain the right to participate in their own care to the maximum extent of their ability. Supported decision-making — providing information in accessible formats, involving trusted support persons, allowing extra time, and using visual supports — can significantly expand participation.

Nurses should avoid the assumption that a patient with a communication disability also has a cognitive disability. Many patients with cerebral palsy, for example, use augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices and have typical cognitive abilities. Speaking to the caregiver rather than the patient in such circumstances is disrespectful and constitutes a denial of the patient's right to participate in their own care.

## Section 5.8 — Building Equitable Systems: Policy and Advocacy

### 5.8.1 The Nurse's Role in Health Equity Advocacy

Individual clinical encounters — however skillfully and humbly conducted — cannot by themselves eliminate health disparities produced by structural inequity. The ANA Code of Ethics (2015b) and the Essentials of Baccalaureate Nursing Education (AACN, 2021) both explicitly call on nurses to engage in **health equity advocacy** — actions directed at changing the policies, institutions, and systems that produce and maintain disparities.

The Quad Council Coalition Competencies for Public Health Nursing (2018) describe three tiers of advocacy: working with individuals and families to address immediate needs; working with communities and organizations to address service gaps and barriers; and working at the policy level to address the laws, regulations, and resource allocations that shape health outcomes for entire populations.

Nurses are the largest segment of the health workforce and interact with patients across every care setting and community. This scope positions nursing uniquely to both identify inequities and advocate for change. The challenge is translating clinical observation into policy action — a skill that requires deliberate development.

### 5.8.2 Data, Quality Improvement, and Disparities Identification

A fundamental tool of health equity advocacy is data. Health care systems that do not collect race, ethnicity, language, and socioeconomic data cannot identify disparities in their patient populations, cannot design targeted interventions, and cannot measure progress. Nurses can advocate for the standardized collection of race, ethnicity, language, and SOGI data at registration, as well as the stratification of quality metrics by race, ethnicity, and socioeconomic indicators. In addition, nurses can support the integration of SDOH screening into the electronic health record and the inclusion of community representatives and patients with lived experience on quality improvement committees.

### 5.8.3 Community Partnerships and Social Prescribing

**Social prescribing** — the practice of connecting patients to non-clinical community resources to address social determinants of health — is an emerging, evidence-supported model that positions nurses as bridges between clinical care and community support. Referrals to food banks, housing assistance programs, community health workers, domestic violence shelters, and transportation services address root causes of poor health outcomes in ways that pharmacological interventions cannot.

Community health workers (CHWs) — laypeople from the communities they serve who are trained to provide health education, case management, and social support — have demonstrated effectiveness in reducing disparities in diabetes management, cardiovascular disease, maternal health, and pediatric asthma (Viswanathan et al., 2009). Nurses working in community settings frequently collaborate with CHWs and should understand their role, scope, and contribution.

> **EBP Connection: The Effectiveness of Community Health Workers**  
> A systematic review by Viswanathan et al. (2009) found strong evidence that community health worker interventions improve outcomes for patients with diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and asthma in underserved communities. Outcomes improved across multiple domains: medication adherence, blood pressure control, blood glucose management, and preventive service utilization. The effectiveness of CHW programs derives from their cultural and linguistic congruence with the populations they serve — a living illustration of the principle that care delivered by those with shared cultural identity and lived experience has distinctive impact.

### 5.8.4 Advocacy in Action: From Bedside to Policy

The path from individual clinical observation to policy change is not direct, but nurses have historically traveled it with great effect. Florence Nightingale used mortality data to advocate for sanitation reform in British hospitals. Lillian Wald founded settlement houses to address the social determinants of health in immigrant communities. Contemporary nurses serve on hospital boards, state legislatures, federal advisory panels, and international health organizations.

Practical entry points for health equity advocacy are numerous and accessible at every career stage. **Joining a professional nursing organization** that advocates for health equity at the state and federal levels — such as the National Black Nurses Association, the National Association of Hispanic Nurses, or the National LGBTQ Task Force — connects nurses to collective advocacy infrastructure. **Participating in unit-level quality improvement** by identifying patterns of unequal care in documentation, patient experience data, or outcome metrics transforms clinical observation into institutional action. **Engaging in community-based activities** — volunteering, participating in community advisory boards, or contributing to local public health planning processes — extends nursing influence beyond the hospital walls. Furthermore, **contacting elected officials** regarding legislation affecting health care access, Medicaid coverage, or social safety net programs ensures that nurses' clinical expertise informs policy decisions. Finally, **using professional platforms** — presentations, publications, and social media — to raise awareness of health inequities and evidence-based solutions amplifies advocacy beyond individual institutions.

The nurse who begins with cultural humility at the bedside and extends that humility to systemic inquiry — asking not only "How do I serve this patient well?" but "Why do patients like this one have worse outcomes, and what can I do about that?" — is positioned to contribute meaningfully to the transformation of a health care system that has too long failed those it most needs to reach.

## Case Study: "She Just Doesn't Follow Instructions"

**Setting:** Outpatient primary care clinic, urban community health center

**Scenario:**

**Scene 1:** Nurse practitioner Cora asks staff nurse Deron to follow up with Ms. Rivera, a 48-year-old Puerto Rican woman with type 2 diabetes whose hemoglobin A1c has risen from 7.8% to 10.4% over the past year despite multiple medication adjustments. A note in the chart from a prior visit reads: "Discussed dietary compliance — patient non-compliant. Continue current regimen." Deron reviews Ms. Rivera's chart and notes: primary language Spanish (interpreter rarely documented); lives in a neighborhood food desert; works two jobs; three children at home; no transportation; insurance status: Medicaid. He schedules a 30-minute dedicated appointment for Ms. Rivera and arranges a professional telephone interpreter in advance.

**Scene 2:** At the appointment, Deron uses the interpreter to conduct a comprehensive interview. He begins with the explanatory model questions: "What do you think is causing your diabetes to be harder to control lately?" Ms. Rivera explains that she has been fasting during certain religious observances and that her family's traditional foods are different from what the dietitian recommended. She says her previous written dietary instructions were in English and she could not read them. She describes her job as physically demanding but that she cannot afford the gym membership the dietitian recommended. She adds, with visible embarrassment: "Last time the doctor said I wasn't following instructions — I've been trying. I just don't know how." Deron thanks her for sharing this, validates her efforts, and begins to build a care plan collaboratively.

**Scene 3:** Deron connects Ms. Rivera with the clinic's bilingual community health worker, who conducts a PRAPARE social determinants screening and identifies her as eligible for a mobile farmers' market program and a medical transportation benefit through her Medicaid plan that she was unaware of. He arranges a referral to a dietitian who speaks Spanish and specializes in Latin American food traditions. Ms. Rivera's educational materials are reprinted in Spanish at a 6th-grade reading level with visual diagrams. At her three-month follow-up, her A1c has decreased to 8.9%. Deron documents the case in a de-identified format for the clinic's quality improvement committee as an example of how SDOH screening can identify remediable barriers to glycemic control.

**Discussion Questions:**

1. The original chart note described Ms. Rivera as "non-compliant." Using the concepts from Sections 5.2 and 5.3, critique this characterization. What does the evidence suggest about why Ms. Rivera's A1c increased, and how does the language of "non-compliance" reflect a failure of cultural humility?
2. Deron used explanatory model questions and the FICA framework at his appointment with Ms. Rivera. Identify at least two specific pieces of information he gathered through these approaches that would not have been captured by standard clinical questioning, and explain how each affected the care plan.
3. Several social determinants of health contributed to Ms. Rivera's clinical trajectory. Identify three specific SDOH factors documented in the case and describe one evidence-based intervention Deron implemented for each.
4. In Scene 3, Deron documented the case for the clinic's quality improvement committee. Explain the connection between individual patient advocacy and systemic equity advocacy illustrated by this action, drawing on Section 5.8. Why is this documentation step as important as the individual clinical encounter?

## Chapter Summary

This chapter established cultural humility as the governing framework for inclusive nursing practice, distinguishing it from the earlier cultural competence model by emphasizing ongoing self-reflection, patient-centered inquiry, attention to power dynamics, and commitment to lifelong learning. The chapter examined the complex relationships among culture, health beliefs, and health disparities, identifying structural racism and social determinants of health as the primary drivers of inequitable outcomes. Health literacy was analyzed as both an individual characteristic and an environmental product, with the universal precautions approach and plain language principles as evidence-based responses. Culturally responsive communication — including professional interpreter services, cross-cultural nonverbal communication awareness, and culturally informed pain assessment — was examined as the practical application of cultural humility in clinical encounters. Religion and spirituality were addressed as dimensions of the whole person that nursing care must integrate through spiritual assessment and chaplaincy collaboration. LGBTQ+-affirming practices — from inclusive documentation to anatomy-based screening — were reviewed as essential components of equitable care. The chapter examined disability through the social model framework, emphasizing accommodation, communication access, and the dismantling of attitudinal barriers. Finally, the chapter connected individual clinical advocacy to systemic health equity advocacy, positioning nurses as agents of change from the bedside to the policy arena.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. Cultural humility — characterized by self-reflection, patient-centering, power awareness, and lifelong learning — is a more robust framework for inclusive care than cultural competence models that imply mastery or expertise.
2. Health disparities are produced primarily by structural inequities — including racism, economic disadvantage, and discriminatory policy — not by individual behavior or biology.
3. Low health literacy affects more than one-third of U.S. adults and is a powerful predictor of poor health outcomes; universal precautions and plain language principles are evidence-based responses that improve outcomes for all patients.
4. Language access through professional interpreter services is a legal requirement and a patient safety imperative; family members and untrained staff should not serve as interpreters in clinical encounters.
5. Spiritual and religious beliefs are significant health care resources for many patients; spiritual assessment using the FICA tool and chaplaincy referral are standard components of culturally responsive nursing care.
6. LGBTQ+ individuals experience significant health disparities attributable to minority stress and discrimination; affirming care begins with correct pronoun use and extends to anatomy-based screening, inclusive documentation, and trauma-informed practice.
7. Patients with disabilities retain full rights to participate in their own care; the social model of disability directs nurses to address environmental and attitudinal barriers rather than focusing exclusively on the patient's impairment.
8. Individual clinical advocacy for culturally diverse patients is necessary but insufficient; systemic equity requires data collection, quality improvement, community partnership, and policy engagement at the institutional and societal levels.
