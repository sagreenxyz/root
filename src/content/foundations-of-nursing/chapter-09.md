2. Describe the structure and purpose of the NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (CJMM) and explain its relevance to the Next Generation NCLEX (NGN).
3. Analyze how the client context — individual factors, nursing factors, and environmental factors — influences clinical judgment.
4. Apply the cognitive operations of recognizing and analyzing cues to a clinical scenario using the NCSBN CJMM framework.

6. Evaluate patient outcomes following nursing action and describe the iterative feedback loop between evaluation and subsequent clinical judgment.
7. Identify the item types used in Next Generation NCLEX (NGN) questions and explain how each measures clinical judgment.
8. Apply priority-setting frameworks — ABCs, Maslow's hierarchy of needs, and SBAR — to clinical scenarios requiring triage and communication.

## Key Terms

| Term | Brief Definition |
|------|-----------------|
| Clinical judgment | The observed outcome of critical thinking and decision-making; a complex process that includes noticing and interpreting patient cues, responding, and reflecting on outcomes |
| Clinical reasoning | A thinking process that enables nurses to collect and process information, generate and evaluate solutions, and apply evidence to individual patient situations |
| Critical thinking | The disciplined, intellectually driven process of applying specific standards of thought — clarity, accuracy, precision, relevance, depth, breadth, and fairness — to clinical problems |
| NCSBN CJMM | The National Council of State Boards of Nursing Clinical Judgment Measurement Model; a layered framework describing the factors that influence and the cognitive operations that constitute clinical judgment in nursing |
| Recognizing cues | The cognitive operation of identifying which assessment data are relevant or of concern; the first internal cognitive operation in the NCSBN CJMM |
| Analyzing cues | The cognitive operation of linking recognized cues to the patient's clinical condition, forming an initial clinical picture |
| Prioritizing hypotheses | The cognitive operation of ranking possible explanations for a patient's condition from most to least likely or most to least urgent |
| Generating solutions | The cognitive operation of identifying nursing actions or interventions that will address prioritized hypotheses |
| Taking action | The cognitive operation of implementing the selected interventions, including direct care, communication, and documentation |
| Evaluating outcomes | The cognitive operation of reassessing the patient after action to determine whether the intended outcomes were achieved |
| Next Generation NCLEX (NGN) | The revised NCLEX examination launched in 2023, incorporating clinical judgment measurement items including unfolding case studies and six new item types designed to assess the cognitive operations in the NCSBN CJMM |
| Unfolding case study | An NGN item type presenting a clinical scenario in stages, with questions at each stage that assess recognition, analysis, hypothesis generation, solution generation, action-taking, and evaluation |
| ABCs framework | A clinical priority-setting framework in which airway, breathing, and circulation concerns are addressed before all other patient needs |
| Maslow's hierarchy of needs | A motivational theory organized into a five-level pyramid — physiological → safety → love/belonging → esteem → self-actualization — used in nursing to prioritize patient needs from most to least fundamental |
| SBAR | A structured communication tool — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — used to convey critical patient information between healthcare providers in a concise, standardized format |

## Introduction

It is 1430 on a busy medical-surgical unit. Kayla, a registered nurse two months out of orientation, enters the room of Mr. Torres, a 72-year-old man who was admitted yesterday following elective hip replacement surgery. His vital signs at 1400 were unremarkable — blood pressure 118/74 mmHg, heart rate 82 beats per minute, respiratory rate 16 breaths per minute, oxygen saturation 97% on room air. But as Kayla performs her afternoon assessment, something stops her. Mr. Torres is quieter than usual. His skin, which was warm and pink this morning, looks slightly mottled around his knees. He answers her questions with one-word responses and seems to be working harder to breathe, though the monitor still reads 95%. His last hemoglobin, drawn at 0600, was 9.2 g/dL.

Nothing has triggered an alarm. No critical value has appeared in the electronic health record. And yet Kayla feels, with growing certainty, that something is wrong.

This moment — noticing a subtle shift, interpreting its significance, deciding what to do, and acting in time — is the essence of **clinical judgment**. It is not guesswork, and it is not purely intuition. It is a learned cognitive skill that integrates knowledge, pattern recognition, contextual awareness, and deliberate reasoning. It is what separates a nurse who catches a developing hemorrhage from one who charts "patient resting comfortably" and moves on.

The **NCSBN Clinical Judgment Measurement Model (CJMM)** provides the formal architecture for this skill, describing the layers of context and the sequence of cognitive operations that underlie safe, effective nursing decisions. It is also the framework that drives the **Next Generation NCLEX (NGN)**, the revised licensure examination that assesses whether new nurses are prepared to exercise clinical judgment in complex, real-world situations.

Building on the communication skills developed in Chapter 4, the evidence-based practice principles introduced in Chapter 7, and the nursing process framework of Chapter 8, this chapter examines clinical judgment from multiple angles. Section 9.1 distinguishes clinical reasoning from critical thinking. Section 9.2 provides an overview of the NCSBN CJMM. Sections 9.3 through 9.6 examine each layer of the model in depth. Section 9.7 maps the model to Next Generation NCLEX item types. Section 9.8 introduces the priority-setting frameworks — ABCs, Maslow, and SBAR — that operationalize clinical judgment in practice.

## Section 9.1 — Clinical Reasoning vs. Critical Thinking: A Distinction

### 9.1.1 Defining Critical Thinking in Nursing

The terms **critical thinking** and **clinical reasoning** are often used interchangeably in nursing education, but they refer to related yet distinguishable cognitive processes. Understanding the distinction between them is essential for developing the metacognitive awareness that underlies expert clinical judgment.

Critical thinking, as applied to nursing, is the disciplined, standards-governed process of evaluating evidence, questioning assumptions, and drawing reasoned conclusions. Paul and Elder (2008) define it as thinking that is "self-directed, self-disciplined, self-monitored, and self-corrective" (p. 4). The intellectual standards of critical thinking — clarity, accuracy, precision, relevance, depth, breadth, logic, significance, and fairness — serve as evaluative benchmarks against which any claim or conclusion can be measured. In a clinical context, critical thinking asks: Is this assessment data accurate? Is my interpretation of the data logical? Have I considered alternative explanations? Am I allowing bias to influence my conclusions?

Critical thinking is domain-general — it applies with equal force to analyzing a research study, evaluating a medication order, or questioning a colleague's care plan. It is the foundational cognitive habit that nurses must bring to every encounter, every shift, and every decision. Nursing educators have long recognized that critical thinking cannot be taught as a list of steps but must be cultivated through deliberate practice, reflective questioning, and exposure to complex clinical scenarios (Alfaro-LeFevre, 2019).

Crucially, critical thinking alone does not produce clinical decisions. A nurse may think with perfect logical rigor about an abstract problem and still fail to connect that reasoning to the specific patient, specific context, and specific moment at hand. That connection is the work of clinical reasoning.

> **Historical Spotlight: Critical Thinking in Nursing Education — A Decades-Long Conversation**  
> The American Nurses Association (ANA) first formally identified critical thinking as a core nursing competency in its 1995 *Nursing's Social Policy Statement*. By the early 2000s, accreditation bodies including the National League for Nursing Accrediting Commission (NLNAC) and the Commission on Collegiate Nursing Education (CCNE) required nursing programs to document how their curricula developed critical thinking skills. The challenge was measurement: critical thinking is a disposition and a process, not a checklist item. This measurement problem ultimately drove the development of the NCSBN CJMM — a model that breaks clinical judgment into discrete, observable, and testable cognitive operations (National Council of State Boards of Nursing [NCSBN], 2019).

### 9.1.2 Clinical Reasoning as Applied Thinking

**Clinical reasoning** narrows the broad capacity of critical thinking to a specific task: processing patient information and making nursing decisions. Simmons (2010) defines clinical reasoning as "a complex cognitive process that uses formal and informal thinking strategies to gather and analyze patient information, evaluate the significance of this information, and weigh alternative actions" (p. 1151). Where critical thinking is about the quality of thought in general, clinical reasoning is about the application of that quality to a specific patient at a specific moment.

Tanner's (2006) seminal research on clinical judgment identified four phases of clinical reasoning that nurses move through during patient encounters: *noticing* (perceiving relevant cues), *interpreting* (making sense of what is noticed), *responding* (deciding and acting), and *reflecting* (evaluating the outcome and learning from it). Tanner emphasized that clinical reasoning is never purely cognitive — it is embedded in the nurse's background knowledge, relationship with the patient, and expectations based on prior experience. The expert nurse who walks into a room and immediately senses something is wrong is drawing on thousands of prior patient encounters organized into pattern recognition schemas that operate below conscious awareness.

This distinction has important implications for nursing education. Students cannot become skilled clinical reasoners by memorizing facts alone; they must practice reasoning in context — through simulation, clinical experiences, case studies, and structured debriefing. The NCSBN CJMM formalizes this insight by organizing clinical judgment into a teachable, assessable framework that makes the invisible cognitive process visible.

## Section 9.2 — The NCSBN CJMM: Overview

### 9.2.1 Origins and Purpose of the Model

The **NCSBN CJMM** was developed by the National Council of State Boards of Nursing in response to a fundamental challenge: the traditional NCLEX examination was measuring knowledge recall rather than the clinical judgment competencies that determine whether a new nurse will practice safely. Research conducted by the NCSBN over more than a decade — including surveys of nurse preceptors, employers, and educators — consistently identified clinical judgment as the most critical, and most frequently deficient, competency among newly licensed nurses (NCSBN, 2019).

The CJMM is not a theory of nursing or a model of nursing care; it is a measurement framework — a map of the cognitive territory that nursing clinical judgment occupies. Its design goal was precision: to define clinical judgment specifically enough that it could be assessed by an examination, observed in clinical practice, and taught in a curriculum. The model was developed through an iterative process that included review of the existing literature on clinical reasoning (Tanner, 2006; Benner, 1984; Cappelletti et al., 2014), cognitive psychology (Klein, 2008), and educational measurement theory.

The CJMM consists of two major elements: an outer layer describing the **contextual factors** that influence clinical judgment, and an inner layer describing the **cognitive operations** through which clinical judgment is exercised. Understanding these two elements — and the relationship between them — is essential for both passing the NGN and practicing safely.

> **EBP Connection: Research Underpinnings of the NCSBN CJMM**  
> The cognitive operations in the NCSBN CJMM are grounded in decades of nursing and cognitive science research. Tanner's (2006) model of clinical judgment — noticing, interpreting, responding, reflecting — provided the foundational phenomenological description. Klein's (2008) Recognition-Primed Decision model from cognitive psychology contributed the understanding of how experts recognize patterns under uncertainty. Benner's (1984) novice-to-expert framework informed the model's treatment of how clinical judgment develops over time and experience. The NCSBN synthesized these streams into a model specifically designed to be operationalized as a measurement instrument for entry-level nurses.

### 9.2.2 The Architecture of the CJMM

The CJMM is depicted as a series of concentric layers, each layer influencing the one inside it. Reading from the outside in:

**Layer 1 — The Client Context** is the outermost layer, encompassing the three categories of factors that shape every clinical judgment encounter: individual factors (the patient's characteristics, history, and needs), nursing factors (the nurse's own background, experience, and knowledge), and environmental factors (the healthcare setting, available resources, and system-level influences).

**Layer 2 — The Cognitive Operations** is the inner layer, describing the six sequential mental processes through which clinical judgment occurs: recognizing cues, analyzing cues, prioritizing hypotheses, generating solutions, taking action, and evaluating outcomes. These operations are the observable, teachable, and assessable components of clinical judgment — the processes that the NGN is specifically designed to measure.

The CJMM makes clear that clinical judgment is never exercised in a vacuum. Every decision a nurse makes is shaped by who the patient is, who the nurse is, and where the encounter takes place. This ecological understanding of judgment distinguishes the CJMM from earlier models that treated clinical reasoning as a purely cognitive, context-free process (NCSBN, 2019).

## Section 9.3 — Layer 1: The Client Context

### 9.3.1 Individual and Nursing Factors

The **client context** — the outermost layer of the NCSBN CJMM — encompasses the three categories of background factors that shape how clinical judgment unfolds in any given encounter. These factors do not directly produce judgments; rather, they set the stage within which the cognitive operations occur, influencing what cues a nurse notices, how cues are interpreted, and what actions are selected.

**Individual factors** are characteristics of the patient that the nurse must know and consider. They include the patient's age, biological sex, and gender identity; cultural background, health literacy, and preferred language; medical history, comorbidities, current diagnoses, and medications; psychological and emotional state; social support network; and personal health beliefs and values. These factors influence how disease presents, how patients communicate symptoms, and what interventions are appropriate, acceptable, and feasible. A 78-year-old patient with cognitive impairment will not report pain the same way a 35-year-old will; a patient from a culture that values stoicism may minimize symptoms that a nurse would otherwise recognize as significant. Attention to individual factors is not optional — it is the foundation of individualized, patient-centered care.

**Nursing factors** are characteristics of the nurse that shape clinical judgment. They include educational preparation, years of clinical experience, familiarity with the clinical setting and patient population, personal knowledge gaps and biases, emotional state at the time of the encounter, and degree of situational awareness. Benner's (1984) novice-to-expert framework illuminates why nursing factors matter: the novice nurse relies on explicit rules and procedures, while the expert nurse draws on an extensive schema of pattern recognition that allows rapid, intuitive recognition of clinical deterioration. Neither novice nor expert judgment is infallible — the novice may miss subtle cues, while the expert may over-rely on pattern recognition and dismiss data that does not fit an expected pattern.

> **Clinical Tip: Combating Anchoring Bias**  
> **Anchoring bias** occurs when a clinician fixes on an initial interpretation of data and fails to revise that interpretation as new information arrives. It is one of the most common cognitive errors in clinical reasoning (Croskerry, 2002). A nurse who learns at handoff that a patient is "anxious and a poor historian" may attribute all subsequent complaints to anxiety, missing early signs of sepsis or pulmonary embolism. To combat anchoring bias, practice deliberately asking: "What else could explain this finding?" and "What data would change my mind?" This habit of intellectual humility is foundational to safe clinical judgment.

### 9.3.2 Environmental Factors

**Environmental factors** are the characteristics of the healthcare setting and system that influence clinical judgment. They encompass the physical environment (unit layout, available equipment, staffing ratios), organizational culture (norms around speaking up, escalation pathways, support for novice nurses), available resources (access to rapid response teams, diagnostic equipment, clinical pharmacists, social workers), time pressure, and system factors such as electronic health record design and handoff quality.

Environmental factors can facilitate or impair clinical judgment in powerful ways. A unit with a strong safety culture — where nurses feel empowered to escalate concerns without fear of dismissal — produces better clinical judgment outcomes than one where hierarchy discourages speaking up (Institute of Medicine [IOM], 2011). Time pressure, a pervasive feature of modern nursing practice, degrades the quality of all six cognitive operations: nurses recognize fewer cues, analyze them less thoroughly, generate fewer solution options, and evaluate outcomes less systematically when they feel rushed.

The physical design of the patient's room and unit matters as well. Isolation precautions that require donning and doffing personal protective equipment before entry create a friction cost that nurses must consciously manage — research consistently shows that nurses make fewer assessments on patients in contact isolation, with consequences for the early recognition of clinical deterioration (Croft et al., 2015).

Understanding environmental factors is not an invitation to excuse unsafe practice; it is an invitation to design systems and cultures that support the highest possible quality of clinical judgment. The IOM's (2011) landmark report *The Future of Nursing* explicitly identifies organizational environment as a determinant of nursing judgment quality and calls for nursing leadership, interprofessional collaboration, and adequate staffing as structural prerequisites for safe care.

## Section 9.4 — Layer 2: The Nurse's Cognitive Operations — Recognizing and Analyzing Cues

### 9.4.1 Recognizing Cues

The six cognitive operations in the inner layer of the NCSBN CJMM describe the sequential mental processes through which clinical judgment is exercised. The first two operations — **recognizing cues** and **analyzing cues** — constitute the assessment phase of clinical judgment and correspond broadly to the Assessment step of the nursing process (ADPIE) explored in Chapter 8.

**Recognizing cues** is the cognitive operation of identifying which patient data are relevant and noteworthy — that is, which data points represent a departure from expected norms, a change from the patient's baseline, or an unexpected pattern. Recognizing cues requires a foundation of clinical knowledge: a nurse cannot recognize that a serum lactate of 4.2 mmol/L is significant without knowing the normal range (< 2 mmol/L) and understanding its association with tissue hypoperfusion and sepsis. It also requires contextual knowledge: a heart rate of 110 beats per minute in a patient who has just walked to the bathroom is expected; the same heart rate in a patient who has been at rest for four hours requires recognition as a cue.

Cues are not limited to abnormal laboratory values and vital signs. Behavioral cues — increased restlessness, decreased engagement, change in facial expression — are often the earliest signals of clinical deterioration, particularly in patients who cannot self-report symptoms. Tanner (2006) describes a concept she calls "the nursing gaze" — the trained perceptual awareness that allows an experienced nurse to notice subtle shifts in a patient's presentation that precede measurable physiological change. The nursing gaze is not mystical; it is the product of pattern recognition schemas built through repeated clinical exposure.

In the NGN framework, recognizing cues corresponds to specific item types that present a patient scenario and ask the examinee to identify which findings require immediate attention or represent significant changes from baseline. The examinee must discriminate between relevant and irrelevant data — a skill that requires both clinical knowledge and contextual judgment.

> **Safety Alert: The Danger of Alarm Fatigue**  
> Modern clinical environments generate thousands of monitor alarms per patient per day. Research shows that the vast majority of these alarms are false positives or clinically insignificant — and that nurses, overwhelmed by alarm volume, begin to habituate to and ignore them (Joint Commission, 2013). This phenomenon, known as **alarm fatigue**, directly impairs the cognitive operation of recognizing cues: when all alarms are treated as noise, genuine signals are missed. The Joint Commission has identified alarm fatigue as a leading cause of preventable patient deaths and has issued National Patient Safety Goals requiring healthcare organizations to develop alarm management programs. As a new nurse, cultivate the discipline of evaluating every alarm on its individual clinical merits rather than allowing habituation to override judgment.

### 9.4.2 Analyzing Cues

Once cues have been recognized, the nurse moves to the second cognitive operation: **analyzing cues**. Where recognizing cues is about identifying what is noteworthy, analyzing cues is about interpreting what it means — linking the recognized data to the patient's clinical condition and forming an initial clinical picture.

Analyzing cues requires the integration of multiple types of knowledge: pathophysiological knowledge (what biological processes might explain this finding?), pharmacological knowledge (could a medication be contributing to this presentation?), epidemiological knowledge (what conditions are common in this patient population?), and knowledge of the patient's individual context (does this change represent a new problem or a worsening of an existing one?). The process is inherently inferential: the nurse moves from observations to interpretations, from "what I see" to "what it might mean."

Cappelletti et al.'s (2014) systematic review of clinical reasoning in nursing found that expert nurses analyze cues through a process of hypothesis activation — generating tentative explanations for observed data and then testing those explanations against additional findings. This process is largely automatic in experienced nurses but must be made explicit and deliberate in novice nurses who have not yet developed robust pattern recognition schemas. Structured frameworks — such as asking "What do I expect to see? What am I actually seeing? What might explain the difference?" — can scaffold this deliberate analysis for nursing students and new graduates.

Analyzing cues also involves recognizing the limits of one's own interpretation. When a nurse's analysis produces an interpretation that she is uncertain about, or when the cue pattern does not fit a recognized schema, the appropriate response is not to default to a familiar explanation but to escalate — to seek additional data, consult a more experienced colleague, or invoke the rapid response system. The willingness to say "I don't know what this means, but I know something is wrong" is itself a form of advanced clinical reasoning.

## Section 9.5 — Layer 3: Prioritizing Hypotheses and Generating Solutions

### 9.5.1 Prioritizing Hypotheses

With cues recognized and analyzed, the nurse advances to the third cognitive operation: **prioritizing hypotheses**. A hypothesis, in the clinical judgment context, is a tentative explanation for the patient's current status — a working interpretation of what is happening and why. In most complex clinical situations, more than one hypothesis is plausible simultaneously. The nurse's task is not to select a single correct hypothesis and discard all others but to rank the competing hypotheses by their relative likelihood and urgency.

Prioritizing hypotheses is explicitly a probability exercise, not a certainty exercise. The nurse encountering a post-surgical patient with tachycardia, restlessness, and falling blood pressure must entertain multiple hypotheses: hypovolemia from surgical blood loss, pulmonary embolism, sepsis from wound infection, pain, or anxiety. Each hypothesis carries a different probability, a different urgency, and a different required response. The nurse who fixates on the least dangerous hypothesis (anxiety) and delays investigation of the most dangerous (pulmonary embolism) has failed the cognitive operation of prioritizing — with potentially fatal consequences.

The concept of **hypothesis refinement** is central to this operation. As new data is gathered — either through ongoing assessment or through the results of interventions — hypotheses are revised upward or downward in priority. A nurse who orders a chest X-ray and sees a new infiltrate will elevate the pneumonia hypothesis and deprioritize the anxiety hypothesis. This continuous updating process mirrors the Bayesian reasoning framework from statistics: each new piece of evidence alters the probability distribution across competing explanations.

> **EBP Connection: Situation Awareness and Clinical Deterioration**  
> Situation awareness — the perception of environmental elements, comprehension of their meaning, and projection of their future status — is a concept borrowed from aviation and adapted to healthcare by researchers studying clinical deterioration. Endsley's (1995) model of situation awareness maps closely to the hypothesis-prioritization operation in the NCSBN CJMM: Level 1 (perception) corresponds to recognizing cues; Level 2 (comprehension) corresponds to analyzing cues and prioritizing hypotheses; Level 3 (projection) corresponds to generating solutions by anticipating how the patient's condition will evolve if untreated. Studies of rapid response team activations consistently show that nurses who activate the team earliest have higher situation awareness scores — and that targeted training in situation awareness reduces preventable in-hospital deterioration (Jones et al., 2011).

### 9.5.2 Generating Solutions

Once hypotheses are prioritized, the nurse moves to the fourth cognitive operation: **generating solutions**. This is the deliberate identification of nursing actions — assessments, interventions, communications, or escalations — that will address the highest-priority hypotheses.

Generating solutions is not the same as selecting interventions from a care plan template. It is an active, context-sensitive process that asks: What specific actions will give me more information about this patient's condition? What interventions will address the most urgent hypothesis? What can I do independently, and what requires a physician or advanced practice provider order? What must be communicated, to whom, and how?

The scope of solutions available to a nurse is broad: direct assessment (obtain vital signs, perform focused physical examination), data gathering (review trend data in the EHR, call for laboratory results), communication (notify the primary provider, activate the rapid response team, contact the charge nurse), implementation of standing orders, and patient education. Effective solution generation requires the nurse to draw on clinical knowledge, scope-of-practice awareness, and understanding of the healthcare team's roles and capabilities.

A critical dimension of generating solutions is **prioritization among interventions**. When multiple solutions are simultaneously feasible, the nurse must sequence them by the relative urgency of the hypotheses they address and the relative speed with which they can be implemented. In a patient with suspected hemorrhage, calling the provider is not an alternative to obtaining vital signs and applying pressure to a bleeding wound — all three actions may need to occur nearly simultaneously, delegated across available team members.

## Section 9.6 — Layer 4: Taking Action and Evaluating Outcomes

### 9.6.1 Taking Action

The fifth cognitive operation — **taking action** — is the implementation phase of clinical judgment. It is the visible manifestation of the reasoning that has preceded it: the nurse who has recognized cues, analyzed them, prioritized hypotheses, and generated solutions now acts. Taking action includes performing direct nursing interventions (repositioning, administering a prescribed medication, applying oxygen), communicating (escalating to a provider, updating the charge nurse, informing the patient), and documenting (creating an accurate, legally defensible record of assessment findings and interventions).

Taking action is not merely execution. Even during implementation, the nurse continues to gather data, reassess the patient, and update the clinical picture. A nurse who hangs a bolus of intravenous normal saline in response to hypotension is simultaneously observing how the patient's blood pressure responds, how the patient's mental status changes, and whether new cues emerge. This concurrent assessment is what allows the action phase to be responsive rather than mechanical.

The decision to *not* act is also a form of taking action and requires deliberate justification. When a nurse observes a patient-reported complaint of mild nausea and decides not to notify the provider, she is making a clinical judgment: this finding does not rise to the threshold of urgency that requires escalation. This decision can be correct or incorrect — and it must be based on an explicit analysis of the cue, not on task burden, time pressure, or reluctance to appear as if one is overreacting. The IOM (2011) documented that nurses' hesitancy to escalate concerns — driven by hierarchical culture and fear of negative response from physicians — is a major contributor to preventable patient harm.

> **Clinical Tip: Using SBAR to Structure Communication During Taking Action**  
> When a nurse decides to escalate a concern during the taking-action phase, the quality of the communication can be as important as the decision to communicate. Providers receiving a poorly structured, disorganized report may dismiss legitimate concerns or fail to grasp urgency. The **SBAR** tool — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — provides a standardized structure that efficiently conveys the essential information a provider needs to make a decision. Using SBAR does not require extensive preparation time; a 30-second mental rehearsal before picking up the phone ensures that the nurse's clinical judgment is communicated with the precision and authority it deserves. Chapter 4 introduced SBAR in the context of therapeutic communication; in the NCSBN CJMM, it is the vehicle through which the Taking Action operation makes contact with the broader healthcare team.

### 9.6.2 Evaluating Outcomes

The sixth and final cognitive operation in the NCSBN CJMM is **evaluating outcomes** — the systematic reassessment of the patient after action to determine whether the intended outcomes have been achieved, whether the situation has improved, stabilized, or worsened, and whether the clinical judgment that drove the action was correct.

Evaluating outcomes is the operation most directly aligned with the Evaluation step of the nursing process (ADPIE). It asks: Did the intervention produce the expected result? If not, why not? Does the patient's response support, refute, or complicate the hypotheses that were prioritized? What does the new information mean for the patient's plan of care?

The most important characteristic of outcome evaluation is its iterative relationship with all preceding cognitive operations. A patient whose blood pressure does not respond to a 500 mL normal saline bolus as expected requires the nurse to loop back to analyzing cues: the failure of the expected response is itself a new cue, suggesting that the original hypothesis (simple hypovolemia) may be inadequate or incomplete. This looping process — recognize new cues, re-analyze, revise hypotheses, generate new solutions, take new actions, evaluate again — is what the NCSBN CJMM depicts through its feedback arrows and what Tanner (2006) describes as the reflecting phase of clinical reasoning.

Outcome evaluation also has a retrospective, learning dimension. After an acute clinical situation has resolved, the reflective nurse asks: What did I notice first? What did I miss? How could I have recognized the deterioration sooner? What would I do differently next time? This deliberate reflection — formalized in simulation debriefing and clinical post-conference — is the mechanism through which clinical experience is converted into the pattern recognition schemas that characterize expert nursing judgment (Benner, 1984).

## Section 9.7 — Next Generation NCLEX (NGN) and Clinical Judgment Item Types

### 9.7.1 The NGN Transformation

The **Next Generation NCLEX (NGN)** represents the most significant transformation of the licensure examination in its history. Launched in April 2023, the NGN was designed specifically to address the gap between what the traditional NCLEX measured (primarily knowledge recall through multiple-choice questions) and what newly licensed nurses actually need to demonstrate to practice safely (clinical judgment, as defined by the NCSBN CJMM).

The transition was driven by the same workforce research that produced the CJMM. Surveys of nurse preceptors and nurse employers conducted by the NCSBN between 2012 and 2019 consistently identified clinical judgment deficits as the most significant practice gap in newly licensed nurses. The traditional NCLEX could identify nurses who knew that a serum potassium of 6.8 mEq/L is critically elevated; it could not reliably identify nurses who would recognize the symptom pattern of hyperkalemia in a patient, link it to the laboratory finding, prioritize the risk of fatal arrhythmia, generate the appropriate interventions, and communicate urgently to the provider. The NGN attempts to measure exactly that sequence (NCSBN, 2023).

The NGN does not replace the content-based knowledge framework of the traditional NCLEX; rather, it adds clinical judgment measurement on top of it. Examinees who take the NGN will encounter both traditional multiple-choice questions (which continue to assess clinical knowledge) and the six new item types designed to measure the cognitive operations of the CJMM. Passing the NGN requires both content knowledge and clinical judgment competency.

> **NGN Connection: Preparing for Next Generation NCLEX Item Types**  
> Students preparing for the NGN should understand that it tests not just what you know but how you think. The six new item types are designed to resist "testwiseness" strategies — you cannot eliminate wrong answers by reading the question carefully without clinical reasoning. The most effective preparation strategy is not memorizing item-type formats but developing genuine clinical reasoning skills through case studies, simulation, clinical post-conference, and deliberate reflection. Students who internalize the NCSBN CJMM as a cognitive framework — who habitually ask "What cues am I recognizing? What do they mean? Which hypothesis is most urgent?" — will be better prepared for NGN than those who simply practice item types in isolation.

### 9.7.2 The Six NGN Item Types

The NGN incorporates six new item types, each targeting specific cognitive operations in the NCSBN CJMM. Understanding the design logic of each item type enables students to approach them strategically and helps clinical instructors align their teaching to the measurement framework.

**1. Extended Multiple Response** — Expands the traditional "select all that apply" format to items with more than five options and a requirement to select a specific number of correct answers. Targets recognizing cues and analyzing cues by presenting clinical data and requiring examinees to identify significant findings.

**2. Extended Drag-and-Drop** — Requires examinees to organize or sequence information (e.g., rank nursing actions in order of priority, or place items into categories). Targets prioritizing hypotheses and generating solutions.

**3. Cloze (Drop-Down)** — Presents a sentence or documentation note with blank fields that the examinee must complete by selecting from a drop-down menu. Targets analyzing cues and generating solutions by requiring precise, context-appropriate completion of clinical language.

**4. Enhanced Hot Spot (Highlighting)** — Presents a clinical document (nurse's note, laboratory report, medication administration record) and requires the examinee to highlight specific words or values that represent relevant or concerning findings. Directly targets recognizing cues.

**5. Matrix/Grid** — Presents rows of clinical information and columns of assessment categories or intervention types; the examinee marks the appropriate cell for each row. Targets multiple cognitive operations simultaneously by requiring categorization, prioritization, and analysis across a data matrix.

**6. Unfolding Case Study** — The most complex NGN item type. Presents a multi-scene clinical scenario in which the patient's situation evolves over time. Each scene presents new clinical data and is followed by one or more questions targeting specific CJMM cognitive operations. The unfolding case study assesses the full sequence of clinical judgment — from initial recognition through outcome evaluation — in a realistic, contextually rich format (NCSBN, 2023).

The **unfolding case study** is particularly significant because it mirrors the temporal structure of actual patient care: the nurse encounters a patient at a moment in time, makes judgments with incomplete information, takes action, receives new information, and revises. This longitudinal design captures the dynamic, iterative nature of clinical judgment in a way that individual stand-alone questions cannot.

## Section 9.8 — Priority-Setting Frameworks: ABCs, Maslow, and SBAR

### 9.8.1 ABCs and Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

No matter how sophisticated the clinical judgment framework, nurses must ultimately decide: which patient need do I address first? Two foundational priority-setting frameworks — the **ABCs framework** and **Maslow's hierarchy of needs** — provide the organizing logic for this decision in both bedside practice and NCLEX examination questions.

The **ABCs framework** derives from emergency and critical care medicine and reflects a universal physiological truth: without a patent airway, adequate breathing, and sufficient circulation, no other intervention matters. In clinical priority setting, airway concerns always supersede breathing concerns, which supersede circulation concerns, which supersede all other needs. A patient whose airway is obstructed cannot benefit from wound care, pain management, or discharge teaching. A patient in cardiac arrest requires immediate circulatory intervention before any secondary assessment. The ABCs framework operationalizes the first principle of emergency medicine — prevent the most immediately life-threatening physiological failure — and applies it to triage decisions in any clinical setting.

The ABCs framework is not limited to emergency situations. On a general medical-surgical floor, a nurse managing five patients must decide which assessment to perform first. The patient with a reported respiratory rate of 8 and altered mental status (airway/breathing concern) is assessed before the patient requesting pain medication for a reported 5/10 headache (comfort need). This everyday application of ABCs — not dramatic enough to feel like emergency triage but critical enough to affect patient safety — is where most real-world priority-setting decisions are made.

**Maslow's hierarchy of needs** offers a complementary framework for priority setting that integrates biological, psychological, and social needs into a coherent hierarchy. Maslow (1954) proposed that human needs can be organized into five levels, with more fundamental levels requiring satisfaction before higher-level needs become relevant:

1. **Physiological needs** — oxygen, water, nutrition, thermoregulation, elimination, sleep
2. **Safety needs** — physical safety, security, freedom from harm
3. **Love and belonging needs** — social connection, belonging, intimate relationships
4. **Esteem needs** — self-respect, achievement, recognition
5. **Self-actualization needs** — personal growth, realizing one's full potential

In nursing, Maslow's hierarchy is applied as a decision rule: physiological needs take priority over safety needs, which take priority over psychological and social needs. A patient who is hypoxic, hypoglycemic, or in acute pain cannot engage meaningfully with discharge teaching (an esteem or self-actualization concern) or process a social worker's conversation about home support (a love and belonging concern). Addressing the physiological need first is both the ethically correct priority and the practically necessary precondition for everything else.

> **Clinical Tip: When ABCs and Maslow Conflict**  
> In most clinical situations, ABCs and Maslow's hierarchy yield the same priority. However, they occasionally suggest different responses. A patient requesting a urinal (physiological — elimination) while a second patient is reporting chest pain and diaphoresis (physiological — circulation) presents a Maslow-level tie broken by the ABCs: circulatory threats supersede elimination needs. Similarly, a patient in emotional crisis following a cancer diagnosis may present a genuine safety need (risk of self-harm) that supersedes a purely physiological need in another patient. The skilled nurse applies both frameworks fluidly, recognizing that priority setting is a judgment exercise, not an algorithm.

### 9.8.2 SBAR as a Clinical Judgment Communication Tool

**SBAR** — Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation — was originally developed by the U.S. Navy for use in nuclear submarine operations and adapted for healthcare in the early 2000s at Kaiser Permanente (Leonard et al., 2004). It has since become the most widely adopted structured communication tool in acute care nursing and is embedded in patient safety standards worldwide.

In the context of the NCSBN CJMM, SBAR is most directly relevant to the Taking Action cognitive operation: when the nurse's clinical judgment identifies an urgent concern that requires provider notification, SBAR structures the communication so that the provider receives, in a standardized sequence, the information needed to take action.

Each component of SBAR serves a specific function:

- **Situation** — A one- to two-sentence statement of what is happening right now: "Mr. Torres in Room 412 has developed new-onset tachycardia and hypotension with a blood pressure of 84/52." This orients the provider immediately to the urgency and nature of the concern.

- **Background** — Relevant clinical context: the patient's diagnosis, surgery, pertinent history, current medications, and recent laboratory values. This allows the provider to situate the current finding within the patient's overall clinical picture without reading the chart.

- **Assessment** — The nurse's clinical judgment: "I am concerned that Mr. Torres may be experiencing a postoperative hemorrhage. His hemoglobin at 0600 was 9.2 g/dL and he has had 300 mL of bloody output from his drain since midnight." This is the most important component of SBAR for demonstrating clinical judgment — it communicates not only what is happening but what the nurse believes it means.

- **Recommendation** — What the nurse believes needs to happen: "I am requesting that you come to assess the patient immediately and consider ordering a STAT hemoglobin and cross-match." This closes the communication loop by making a specific, actionable request rather than leaving the provider to infer what the nurse wants.

Research consistently demonstrates that SBAR use improves the accuracy and completeness of nurse-to-provider communication, reduces the frequency of adverse events related to communication failures, and increases providers' satisfaction with the quality of nursing reports (Beckett & Kipnis, 2009). For the nursing student and new graduate, SBAR also serves a psychological function: it transforms what might feel like an intrusive or alarmist phone call into a structured, professional clinical communication — one that is grounded in assessed data and supported by a reasoned clinical judgment.

## Chapter Summary

Clinical judgment is the apex competency of professional nursing — the point at which knowledge, reasoning, contextual awareness, and professional responsibility converge into action. It is neither a fixed trait nor a mysterious gift; it is a learnable, practicable, and assessable skill whose components the NCSBN has codified in the Clinical Judgment Measurement Model.

This chapter traced the architecture of clinical judgment from its conceptual foundations to its practical applications. We distinguished critical thinking — the domain-general discipline of standards-governed reasoning — from clinical reasoning, which applies that discipline to specific patient encounters. We examined the NCSBN CJMM's outer layer of contextual factors (individual, nursing, and environmental) and its inner layer of six cognitive operations. We mapped those operations to the NGN's item types and to the priority-setting frameworks — ABCs, Maslow, and SBAR — that operationalize clinical judgment at the bedside.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. **Critical thinking** provides the intellectual standards — clarity, accuracy, precision, relevance, depth, breadth, and fairness — that govern all reasoning, while **clinical reasoning** applies those standards to specific patient situations, producing clinical judgments through the processes of noticing, interpreting, responding, and reflecting.

2. The **NCSBN CJMM** is a layered measurement framework describing the contextual factors (individual, nursing, and environmental) and cognitive operations (recognizing cues, analyzing cues, prioritizing hypotheses, generating solutions, taking action, and evaluating outcomes) that constitute clinical judgment in nursing.

3. The **client context** — encompassing individual patient factors, nursing factors, and environmental factors — shapes every clinical judgment encounter and determines what cues are noticed, how they are interpreted, and what actions are feasible and appropriate.

4. **Recognizing cues** involves identifying which assessment data are relevant or of concern, while **analyzing cues** involves interpreting what those cues mean by linking them to the patient's clinical condition and generating working hypotheses.

5. **Prioritizing hypotheses** requires ranking competing explanations by likelihood and urgency — not certainty — and continuously revising those rankings as new data arrives; **generating solutions** produces the specific nursing actions that will address the highest-priority hypotheses.

6. **Taking action** is the implementation of selected interventions, including direct care, communication, and documentation, and must remain concurrent with ongoing assessment; **evaluating outcomes** closes the judgment loop by reassessing the patient and using the results to confirm, revise, or abandon prior hypotheses and actions.

7. The **Next Generation NCLEX (NGN)** uses six new item types — extended multiple response, extended drag-and-drop, cloze, enhanced hot spot, matrix/grid, and unfolding case study — to assess the cognitive operations of the NCSBN CJMM directly, measuring clinical judgment rather than knowledge recall alone.

8. The **ABCs framework** and **Maslow's hierarchy of needs** provide the organizing logic for prioritizing competing patient needs; **SBAR** structures the communication of clinical judgments to other providers, ensuring that assessments, interpretations, and recommendations are conveyed with precision and urgency.

## Case Study: "The New Graduate"

**Setting:** 38-bed medical-surgical/telemetry unit, academic medical center, 1900 hours on a Friday evening.

**Background:** Destiny, a registered nurse with four months of independent practice following completion of a six-month orientation, is completing her second-to-last assessment of the shift. She is caring for five patients. Her most complex patient is Mr. Abramowitz, a 67-year-old man admitted 18 hours ago with an acute exacerbation of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD). He responded well to bronchodilators and systemic corticosteroids during the day shift and was downgraded from the step-down unit to the general medical floor at 1500.

---

### Scene 1 — 1900 Assessment

Destiny enters Mr. Abramowitz's room for her 1900 assessment. He is sitting up in bed at a 45-degree angle. His vital signs are: blood pressure 132/78 mmHg, heart rate 96 bpm, respiratory rate 20 breaths per minute, temperature 37.2°C, and oxygen saturation 93% on 2 L/min nasal cannula. At 1500 — four hours ago — his saturation was 96% on 2 L/min. He is using accessory muscles minimally when he breathes. When Destiny asks how he is feeling, he replies, "A little more tired than I was earlier. The breathing is a bit harder." His lung sounds reveal diminished air entry bilaterally with expiratory wheezing in both lower lobes. He denies chest pain, fever, or new cough.

Destiny notes that Mr. Abramowitz's oxygen saturation has dropped 3% in four hours, that his respiratory rate has increased from 16 at 1500 to 20 now, and that he is reporting increased dyspnea. She recalls that the day nurse mentioned his last ABG (arterial blood gas) at 1200 showed a PaCO₂ of 52 mmHg, consistent with his chronic hypercapnia baseline.

**Discussion Questions:**

1. Using the NCSBN CJMM framework, identify the cues Destiny should recognize as significant in this scene. Which findings represent changes from earlier in the shift, and why does that pattern of change matter more than any single value in isolation?

2. Destiny notices that Mr. Abramowitz is "a little more tired" and is using accessory muscles. How does the analyzing-cues operation help Destiny interpret these subtle behavioral and physiological findings in the context of his COPD exacerbation history? What hypotheses should she generate, and how should she prioritize them?

---

### Scene 2 — 1945 Assessment

Forty-five minutes later, Destiny returns to check Mr. Abramowitz. His oxygen saturation has dropped to 89% on 2 L/min nasal cannula. She has increased his oxygen to 4 L/min per the unit's standing order for oxygen titration, but his saturation has only improved to 90%. His respiratory rate is now 24, and he is using accessory muscles prominently. He is no longer conversing — he answers in monosyllables and closes his eyes between responses. Destiny checks his level of consciousness: he is oriented to person but is confused about the date and his location, which was not present at 1900. His last respiratory treatment was at 1800.

Destiny's pulse is quickening. She knows that in COPD, worsening hypercapnia can produce altered mental status before oxygenation falls dramatically. She considers whether she is seeing early respiratory failure.

3. Destiny must decide in the next two to three minutes whether to call the rapid response team (RRT) or simply notify the primary provider. Using the Taking Action cognitive operation and the SBAR framework, outline the communication she should make. What specific information must her Assessment component convey, and what should her Recommendation include?

---

### Scene 3 — 2015: After the Rapid Response

Destiny activated the rapid response team at 1952. The RRT arrived within three minutes. The team assessed Mr. Abramowitz, confirmed a rising PaCO₂ (now 72 mmHg, up from 52 mmHg at 1200) and a pH of 7.28, indicating acute-on-chronic hypercapnic respiratory failure. He was placed on bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP) at 10/5 cmH₂O, 60% FiO₂, and transferred back to the step-down unit. By 2015, his oxygen saturation has improved to 94%, his mental status is clearing, and he is acknowledging staff appropriately.

Destiny completes her documentation and then sits briefly with the charge nurse for a structured debrief. The charge nurse asks: "When did you first notice something was different?"

Destiny reflects. She thinks back to 1900, when she noted the drop from 96% to 93% saturation and the slight increase in work of breathing. She says, "I noticed it at 1900. I'm not sure I acted on it quickly enough — I waited 45 minutes before going back. I wonder if I should have called sooner."

The charge nurse responds: "What would have helped you feel more confident to escalate earlier?"

4. Reflecting on the entire case, which cognitive operation of the NCSBN CJMM was Destiny demonstrating during the debrief with the charge nurse? What individual, nursing, and environmental factors contributed to the 45-minute gap between her initial recognition of the cues and her decision to activate the RRT? What specific strategies — for both Destiny as an individual and for the unit as a system — could support earlier, more confident escalation in future situations?
