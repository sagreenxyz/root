## Introduction

Maria is 74 years old and has been admitted for the fourth time in six months with decompensated heart failure. Her ejection fraction is 15%. Her cardiologist has documented that she is not a candidate for further intervention. On morning rounds, a medical student asks her, briskly, "Are you a full code?" Maria looks at the ceiling. "I don't really know what that means," she says quietly. "I just don't want to suffer."

That single sentence — *I just don't want to suffer* — contains everything a nurse needs to begin a genuine end-of-life conversation. It is a statement of values, a statement of fear, and an invitation. Yet in busy clinical environments, it is too often left unanswered, passed over in the rush to document vital signs and hang another diuretic. The consequences of that silence are measured not in missed charting entries but in deaths that do not honor the people who lived them.

**Palliative care** and end-of-life nursing represent one of the profession's most demanding and most profoundly human responsibilities. They require the full integration of the communication skills developed in Chapter 4, the ethical frameworks of Chapter 2, and the cultural humility principles of Chapter 5. They also require specialized knowledge: an understanding of symptom physiology, prognostic awareness, legal instruments, and the particular emotional landscape of dying — for patients, for families, and for the nurses who accompany them.

This chapter begins by establishing the conceptual and historical foundations of palliative and hospice care (Section 6.1), then examines evidence-based communication frameworks for serious illness conversations (Section 6.2). Sections 6.3 and 6.4 address the physical and psychological dimensions of the dying experience. Section 6.5 covers advance care planning instruments, and Section 6.6 explores the cultural and spiritual dimensions of death. Section 6.7 describes the nurse's role in the active dying phase and the immediate postmortem period. The chapter concludes in Section 6.8 with an analysis of the ethical challenges — from withholding treatment to moral distress — that arise at the intersection of professional responsibility and human mortality.

## Section 6.1 — Foundations of Palliative Care and Hospice

### 6.1.1 Defining Palliative Care

**Palliative care** is often misunderstood — by patients, by families, and sometimes by clinicians — as synonymous with dying, with giving up, or with stopping treatment. This misunderstanding causes profound harm. In fact, palliative care is a philosophy and a specialty of care whose defining feature is the relief of suffering across the full trajectory of serious illness, from diagnosis through death and into bereavement.

The World Health Organization (2020) defines palliative care as "an approach that improves the quality of life of patients and their families facing the problems associated with life-threatening illness, through the prevention and relief of suffering by means of early identification and impeccable assessment and treatment of pain and other problems, physical, psychosocial, and spiritual." Several elements of this definition deserve emphasis. First, palliative care begins at diagnosis — not at a six-month prognosis. A patient with a new diagnosis of stage III lung cancer may benefit from palliative care alongside aggressive chemotherapy. Second, palliative care addresses the full human person: physical symptoms, psychological distress, social and relational needs, and spiritual dimensions of suffering. Third, palliative care explicitly includes the family as a unit of care, recognizing that serious illness is a family experience.

The landmark SUPPORT study (Connors et al., 1995) documented that half of seriously ill hospitalized patients experienced moderate-to-severe pain, half did not have their do-not-resuscitate preferences documented, and there was poor communication between physicians and patients about prognosis and goals of care. This study galvanized the palliative care movement in the United States and led directly to the development of palliative care consultation services in major academic medical centers.

> **Historical Spotlight: Dame Cicely Saunders and the Modern Hospice Movement**  
> The modern hospice movement was born in London in 1967, when Dame Cicely Saunders — a nurse, social worker, and physician — founded St. Christopher's Hospice. Saunders introduced the concept of "total pain," arguing that the suffering of dying patients has physical, emotional, social, and spiritual dimensions, all of which require active and compassionate attention. Her work demonstrated that dying patients could be kept comfortable and that their final weeks could be characterized by dignity, connection, and meaning rather than fear and isolation. Her influence shaped the WHO's palliative care guidelines and the hospice benefit enacted in the United States in 1982 (Clark, 2007).

### 6.1.2 Hospice Care: Philosophy and Structure

**Hospice care** represents palliative care's most focused expression: a program of care for patients whose illness is expected to result in death within six months if the disease follows its natural course. In the United States, the Medicare Hospice Benefit (established 1982) funds hospice care for Medicare beneficiaries who meet this criterion and agree to forgo curative treatment in favor of comfort-focused care. Hospice may be provided in the patient's home, in a skilled nursing facility, in a residential hospice facility, or in an inpatient unit for symptom crises.

Hospice care is delivered by an interdisciplinary team that typically includes a physician, registered nurse, social worker, chaplain, home health aide, and volunteer. The nurse is often the team member with the most frequent patient contact and plays a central role in symptom management, family education, and care coordination. Hospice nurses make home visits, conduct phone triage, and are available twenty-four hours a day to guide families through the dying process.

The hospice philosophy holds that death is a natural process that need not be hastened or artificially prolonged. The goal is neither to cure nor to accelerate death, but to ensure that the patient's remaining time is characterized by maximum comfort, dignity, and meaningful engagement with loved ones. This philosophy challenges the cure-oriented paradigm dominant in acute care nursing education and requires nurses to reconceptualize success not as survival but as a death consistent with the patient's values and wishes.

### 6.1.3 The Trajectory Model of Serious Illness

Not all patients die along the same illness trajectory, and the nurse's anticipatory guidance must be tailored accordingly. Murray and colleagues (2005) described three prototypical dying trajectories:

1. **Cancer trajectory:** A period of relatively stable function followed by a rapid decline in the final weeks and months. Patients and families often have a clear prognostic window and time to prepare.
2. **Organ failure trajectory:** Characterized by slow overall decline punctuated by acute exacerbations (e.g., heart failure hospitalizations), with uncertain timing of death. Patients may not identify as "dying" because recovery from each acute event creates hope for ongoing survival.
3. **Frailty and dementia trajectory:** Prolonged, gradual functional decline over years, often with no clear terminal event. Caregivers may experience extended anticipatory grief and caregiver burnout.

Understanding these trajectories helps nurses engage in timely goals-of-care conversations and anticipate the specific needs — and the specific prognostic uncertainty — of each patient. A patient with end-stage COPD on the organ failure trajectory may benefit from early advance care planning precisely because predicting the timing of death is so difficult.

## Section 6.2 — End-of-Life Communication Frameworks

### 6.2.1 The SPIKES Protocol

Delivering serious news — a terminal prognosis, a recommendation to transition to hospice, the news that treatment is no longer working — is one of the most challenging communication tasks in clinical practice. The **SPIKES protocol**, developed by Baile and colleagues (2000), provides a structured six-step framework that has been adopted across nursing and medical education globally.

The protocol begins with **Setting**: arrange a private, quiet space, ensure the patient has support persons present if desired, sit at eye level, and silence pagers when possible. The second step, **Perception**, asks the nurse to assess what the patient already understands before delivering information — "What have the doctors told you so far about your illness?" — so that the conversation begins where the patient is, not where the clinician assumes they are. **Invitation** follows: the nurse asks how much information the patient wants and in what form, respecting that some patients want every detail while others prefer information be shared first with a family member. Only then does the nurse move to **Knowledge**, delivering information in plain language, in small increments, with deliberate pauses and no euphemisms — "I'm sorry to tell you that the tests show the cancer has spread and the chemotherapy is no longer controlling it." The fifth step, **Empathy**, requires the nurse to acknowledge and respond to the patient's emotional reaction before providing further information, resisting the impulse to fix or problem-solve and using silence as a therapeutic tool. The protocol concludes with **Strategy and Summary**: once the emotional response has been acknowledged, the nurse discusses the path forward — including palliative or hospice care options — and summarizes what has been discussed.

Nurses play a critical role in this process even when a physician delivers the primary prognosis. The nurse is often present during the conversation, frequently conducts follow-up conversations after the physician leaves, and must be prepared to use the SPIKES framework in situations where they are the primary communicator — for example, when a family asks "Is he going to die?" in the absence of the physician.

### 6.2.2 The NURSE Mnemonic for Responding to Emotion

While SPIKES guides the overall structure of a serious news conversation, the **NURSE mnemonic** — developed by Back and colleagues (2005) — provides specific language for responding to a patient's emotional response in the moment. It is applicable in any clinical encounter, not only in terminal diagnosis conversations.

**Naming** the emotion is the first move: acknowledging it directly — "I can see this is devastating news" — signals that the nurse has witnessed what the patient is feeling. **Understanding** communicates that the reaction is legitimate and expected — "Of course you're frightened — this is not what anyone expects to hear." **Respect** acknowledges the patient's strength or the difficulty of what they are managing: "You've been through so much already." **Support** expresses the nurse's commitment to continued presence and care — "I want you to know that we will not abandon you, whatever you decide" — countering the fear of abandonment that many seriously ill patients experience. Finally, **Explore** invites the patient to say more: "Can you tell me more about what's going through your mind right now?" This open invitation creates space for the patient to express fears, wishes, or concerns that may otherwise remain unspoken.

> **Clinical Tip: Silence Is a Therapeutic Tool**  
> New nurses often feel compelled to fill silence after delivering serious news. This impulse — though well-intentioned — can prevent patients from processing what they have heard. After delivering a difficult message or after a patient begins to cry, allow a full fifteen to thirty seconds of silence before speaking. Place a hand on the patient's arm if culturally appropriate. Presence without words is often the most powerful nursing intervention in an end-of-life conversation.

### 6.2.3 Goals-of-Care Conversations

A **goals-of-care conversation** is a structured dialogue between the clinical team, the patient, and often the family to align medical treatment with what the patient values most. These conversations are distinct from informed consent discussions and from code status documentation, though they inform both. The Institute for Healthcare Improvement (2015) framework for goals-of-care conversations includes four core questions:

1. **What do you understand about your illness?** (Establishes shared understanding of prognosis)
2. **What are your fears and worries?** (Surfaces underlying concerns)
3. **What are your hopes?** (Identifies what the patient is living for)
4. **What is most important to you?** (Anchors decision-making in values)

Nurses can initiate and facilitate goals-of-care conversations in their own right — particularly in contexts such as home health, hospice, and palliative care specialty nursing. In acute care settings, nurses often identify that a goals-of-care conversation is needed and advocate to the medical team that it be scheduled. Nursing research consistently demonstrates that nurses who are trained in goals-of-care communication are more likely to document patient preferences accurately and are less likely to report moral distress related to aggressive end-of-life treatment (Weissman & Meier, 2011).

## Section 6.3 — Symptom Management at End of Life

### 6.3.1 Pain Management

Pain is the symptom most feared by dying patients and their families, and inadequate pain management at end of life remains a persistent problem in U.S. health care. The nurse plays the central role in pain assessment, pharmacological management, and family education about the appropriate use of opioids.

The principles of end-of-life pain management differ from those of acute pain management in several important respects. First, the goal is not to titrate to the lowest effective dose — it is to achieve comfort, which may require doses that would be inappropriate in a non-palliative context. Second, the nurse must overcome personal and institutional barriers to adequate opioid administration, including opiophobia (the irrational fear that appropriate opioid doses will hasten death or cause addiction in a dying patient). The principle of double effect, recognized in both nursing ethics and law, holds that the administration of analgesics intended to relieve suffering does not constitute euthanasia even if the medications may, as an unintended secondary effect, hasten death.

Opioid analgesics — most commonly morphine sulfate and hydromorphone — are the cornerstone of end-of-life pain management. They are titrated to effect based on the patient's reported pain level and behavioral cues (for patients unable to self-report). Continuous infusion with patient-controlled analgesia bolus capability is common in inpatient settings. Oral or sublingual routes are preferred when available.

> **Safety Alert: Recognizing Inadequate Symptom Control**  
> A dying patient who is grimacing, guarding, moaning, or restless even without verbal communication is demonstrating behavioral evidence of pain or discomfort. The Behavioral Pain Scale (BPS) and the Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia Scale (PAINAD) are validated tools for assessing pain in patients who cannot self-report. Do not assume that unresponsiveness indicates comfort. Document all behavioral assessments and advocate promptly for medication adjustments when indicated.

### 6.3.2 Dyspnea, Nausea, and Other Symptoms

**Dyspnea** — the subjective sensation of breathlessness — is reported by up to 70% of patients in the final weeks of life and is among the most distressing symptoms at end of life (Solano et al., 2006). Importantly, dyspnea is a subjective experience and does not always correlate with measurable oxygen saturation. A patient with a SpO₂ of 85% who reports no breathlessness is not dyspneic; a patient with a SpO₂ of 94% who reports feeling unable to breathe is dyspneic and requires intervention. Supplemental oxygen should be reserved for patients who are hypoxic and who benefit symptomatically; it is not indicated for all dying patients. Low-dose opioids — particularly morphine — are the most effective pharmacological intervention for refractory dyspnea at end of life.

**Nausea and vomiting** occur in approximately 30–40% of dying patients and may be caused by opioids, constipation, bowel obstruction, hypercalcemia, or increased intracranial pressure. Management includes antiemetics (ondansetron, prochlorperazine, haloperidol), bowel regimen optimization, and, when appropriate, reduction or rotation of opioid medications.

**Terminal secretions** — the rattling or gurgling respiratory sounds caused by accumulation of secretions in the upper airway of a comatose or semiconscious patient — are often more distressing to families than to the patient, who is generally no longer conscious enough to experience distress. The nurse's role includes patient repositioning (lateral or semi-prone), judicious use of anticholinergic agents (hyoscine, glycopyrrolate), and extensive family education: "The sound you're hearing is caused by the throat relaxing, not by your mother choking or suffering."

### 6.3.3 Palliative Sedation

**Palliative sedation** — the intentional reduction of consciousness through sedating medications to relieve refractory suffering in imminently dying patients — is a legitimate, ethically supported intervention when all other symptom management strategies have been exhausted. It is governed by the principle of double effect and is distinguished from euthanasia by intent (relief of suffering, not induction of death) and by the fact that death results from the underlying disease.

Palliative sedation requires physician orders, documented informed consent from the patient or surrogate decision-maker, and close interdisciplinary communication. The nurse administering palliative sedation medications must document the indication, the patient's or surrogate's consent, the specific medications and doses, and the patient's comfort response. Family members require particular support during this period, as seeing their loved one sedated may feel like a loss even before death has occurred.

## Section 6.4 — Psychological and Existential Dimensions of Dying

### 6.4.1 Grief Theories and Kübler-Ross

The psychological literature on dying and grief has been shaped profoundly by the work of Elisabeth Kübler-Ross, whose 1969 book *On Death and Dying* (Kübler-Ross, 1969) introduced the five-stage model based on her interviews with terminally ill patients. The **Kübler-Ross stages** — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance — are deeply embedded in clinical culture and remain widely taught. However, their application requires important caveats.

The stages are not linear, sequential, or universal. A patient may experience anger and acceptance simultaneously, may revisit denial after a period of apparent acceptance, or may never progress through all five stages. The model was descriptive, not prescriptive — Kübler-Ross identified patterns in her patients' experience; she did not propose a pathway that all patients should follow. Applying the stages rigidly — "she's still in denial" — risks invalidating the patient's actual experience.

More recent grief models offer additional frameworks. Stroebe and Schut's (1999) Dual Process Model describes coping with loss as an oscillation between loss-orientation (confronting grief) and restoration-orientation (attending to life changes and secondary losses). This model is particularly applicable to bereaved family members. Worden's (2018) Tasks of Mourning frames grief as active work: accepting the reality of loss, processing the pain, adjusting to the world without the deceased, and finding a way to maintain connection while embarking on a new life.

### 6.4.2 Anticipatory Grief

**Anticipatory grief** is the grief experienced by patients and their families in advance of an expected death. It can begin at the moment of a serious diagnosis, intensify with each disease progression, and encompass the losses that have already occurred (loss of function, role, independence) as well as the losses that are anticipated. Anticipatory grief is not the same as completed bereavement and does not shorten or replace the grief experienced after death — in fact, some research suggests that anticipatory grief does not significantly reduce post-death grief intensity (Lindemann, 1944/2001; Rando, 1986).

Nurses can support patients and families experiencing anticipatory grief through active listening, normalization ("It's natural to grieve even while your husband is still alive — you're already experiencing many losses"), referral to social work and chaplaincy, and facilitation of legacy work such as life review, letter writing, and recording of oral histories.

### 6.4.3 Spiritual Distress and Existential Suffering

**Spiritual distress** — a disruption in the sense of meaning, purpose, and connection that sustains human wellbeing — is common in dying patients and is not limited to those with religious belief. Patients may express spiritual distress through statements such as "I don't know why this is happening to me," "I feel like I'm being punished," or "What was the point of my life?" These expressions require a nursing response that acknowledges the depth of the suffering without offering premature reassurance or religious platitudes.

The FICA Spiritual History Tool (Puchalski & Romer, 2000) provides a structured framework for spiritual assessment: Faith (what gives meaning); Importance (how central spirituality is to coping); Community (whether the patient has a faith community for support); Address in care (how the nurse can address spiritual needs). Chaplains are the primary spiritual care specialists in health care settings and should be consulted whenever spiritual distress is identified. However, every nurse has a role in providing spiritual presence — attentive, non-judgmental, compassionate accompaniment in the face of existential suffering.

> **Patient Voice**  
> "The worst part wasn't the pain. The pain they managed pretty well. The worst part was lying there in the dark wondering if any of it meant anything — if I had done anything that mattered. The night nurse, she just sat with me. She didn't say anything religious or try to fix it. She just stayed, and it helped. I don't know why, but it helped." — Patient in a palliative care unit, as recorded by Ferris et al. (2002).

## Section 6.5 — Advance Care Planning and Advance Directives

### 6.5.1 Types of Advance Directives

**Advance directives** are legal documents that allow individuals to express their health care preferences and designate decision-makers for situations in which they have lost decision-making capacity. The two principal types are the **living will** and the **durable power of attorney for health care** (DPAHC), also called a health care proxy.

A **living will** is an instructional directive — it specifies which medical treatments the patient does or does not wish to receive under defined circumstances. Common provisions include statements about cardiopulmonary resuscitation, mechanical ventilation, artificial nutrition and hydration, and hospitalization versus comfort care in the home. Living wills are highly specific in their instructions but can fail to anticipate every clinical situation that arises.

A **durable power of attorney for health care** designates an individual — the health care agent or proxy — to make medical decisions on behalf of the patient when the patient lacks capacity. The agent is expected to apply substituted judgment: making decisions based on what the patient would want, not on what the agent would personally choose. A DPAHC is often more clinically useful than a living will precisely because it designates a person who can respond to unanticipated situations. Nurses should encourage patients to discuss their values and preferences explicitly with their designated agent so that the agent can make genuinely informed substituted judgments.

### 6.5.2 POLST and DNR Orders

The **Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment (POLST)** — known in some states as MOLST (Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) or MOST (Medical Orders for Scope of Treatment) — is a portable medical order document designed for patients with serious illness or frailty. Unlike advance directives, which are patient-authored legal documents, POLST is a physician's or authorized provider's order set that translates the patient's preferences into immediately actionable medical orders. POLST travels with the patient across care settings, including emergency services.

A **do-not-resuscitate (DNR) order** instructs health care providers not to perform cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) if the patient experiences cardiac or respiratory arrest. DNR orders require informed consent from the patient or surrogate decision-maker and must be documented in the medical record and on portable order forms (such as POLST) to be honored by emergency medical services outside hospital settings. Nurses bear a critical responsibility in ensuring that DNR orders are accurately documented, communicated to all care team members, and honored.

### 6.5.3 The Nurse's Role in Advance Care Planning

Nurses are ideally positioned to initiate and facilitate advance care planning conversations. Research consistently demonstrates that patients who are asked about their preferences by nurses are more likely to complete advance directives and are more likely to have their preferences honored (Hammes et al., 2010). However, many nurses report discomfort initiating these conversations, citing concerns about causing distress, uncertainty about prognosis, and inadequate training.

Effective advance care planning involves more than distributing a form. It requires a genuine conversation about values: What does quality of life mean to this patient? What would make life not worth living? Who does the patient trust to make decisions? These conversations should occur before a crisis, when the patient has capacity and time to reflect. They are appropriate at discharge from a hospitalization for serious illness, at the first visit with a new primary care provider, and as part of annual wellness visits for older adults and patients with chronic conditions.

> **EBP Connection: The Respecting Choices® Model**  
> Developed at Gundersen Health System in La Crosse, Wisconsin, the Respecting Choices® program trained nurses and social workers as advance care planning facilitators and created a community-wide system for documenting and honoring patient preferences. A landmark study found that 85% of La Crosse residents who died had advance directives on file — compared to a national average of approximately 30% — and that those directives were consistently honored (Hammes & Rooney, 1998). The model has since been replicated internationally and demonstrates that nurse-led advance care planning programs produce measurable, sustained improvements in documentation and preference-honoring.

## Section 6.6 — Cultural and Spiritual Considerations in Dying

### 6.6.1 Cross-Cultural Variation in Death and Dying

Every culture has norms — explicit or implicit — about how death should be experienced, communicated about, mourned, and ritualized. These norms shape whether patients want to know their prognosis, how much family involvement in decision-making is expected, what happens to the body after death, how grief should be expressed, and which rituals are essential for a spiritually appropriate death.

Building on the cultural humility framework introduced in Chapter 5, nurses must approach dying patients and their families with curiosity rather than assumption. A patient of East Asian heritage may come from a cultural tradition in which disclosure of a terminal prognosis directly to the patient is considered harmful — with family members expected to absorb and mediate this information. A Navajo patient may hold cultural beliefs about the power of spoken words that make explicit discussion of death or dying feel spiritually dangerous. An Orthodox Jewish family may have religious obligations about the presence of family members at the moment of death and specific requirements for the handling of the body. A Muslim patient may want to die facing Mecca and may request that certain prayers be said.

The nurse's role is not to possess expert knowledge of every cultural and religious tradition but to ask, with genuine respect: "Are there any customs, beliefs, or practices that are important to you or your family as you go through this? Is there anything we can do to honor those?" This question opens a space for patients and families to articulate what they need, rather than requiring the nurse to guess.

### 6.6.2 Spiritual Care at End of Life

**Spiritual distress** at end of life intersects with but is not equivalent to religiosity. Studies consistently demonstrate that existential and spiritual concerns — about meaning, legacy, connection, forgiveness — are among the most significant sources of suffering for dying patients and cannot be adequately addressed through pharmacological or social interventions alone (Steinhauser et al., 2000).

The nurse's role in spiritual care begins with assessment. Using the FICA tool or simply asking, "Is there anything spiritual or religious that's important to you as you go through this?" provides an opening. When spiritual distress is identified, the appropriate response includes active listening, presence, and prompt chaplaincy referral. Nurses should not attempt to impose their own spiritual or religious beliefs on patients, offer unsolicited religious perspectives, or dismiss expressions of spiritual distress as psychiatric symptoms.

### 6.6.3 Family Presence and Cultural Bereavement

The experience of being present at a loved one's death is shaped by cultural norms, personal preference, and the quality of clinical support provided. Some families maintain a continuous vigil; others follow religious traditions requiring all family members to be present for the final breath; others prefer to be called after death has occurred.

Nurses should ask families directly about their preferences and support them in being present to the degree they wish. When a death is expected, nurses can help families know what to expect: the changes in breathing, the mottled skin, the periods of apnea that may precede death. This anticipatory guidance transforms what might otherwise feel chaotic and terrifying into an experience the family can navigate with presence and intention.

**Bereavement** support does not end at the time of death. Hospice programs are required to provide bereavement support to families for at least thirteen months following the patient's death. In inpatient settings, nurses can facilitate bereavement by expressing condolences verbally and in writing, by ensuring that the care of the body after death reflects the family's cultural and religious preferences, and by connecting families with appropriate grief counseling resources.

## Section 6.7 — The Nurse's Role in the Final Hours

### 6.7.1 Recognizing Active Dying

**Active dying** refers to the final phase of the dying process — typically lasting hours to a few days — during which specific physiological changes indicate that death is imminent. Recognizing these changes allows nurses to prepare families, optimize comfort, and ensure that the patient does not die alone or in uncontrolled pain.

Clinical signs of active dying include:

- **Mottling** — irregular, bluish-purple discoloration of the skin, beginning in the extremities, caused by peripheral vascular shutdown
- **Cheyne-Stokes respirations** — irregular breathing with alternating periods of apnea and hyperpnea, caused by decreased sensitivity of the respiratory center to carbon dioxide
- **Terminal secretions** (the "**death rattle**") — gurgling respiratory sounds caused by pooling of secretions in the relaxed upper airway
- **Cooling and cyanosis** of the extremities — peripheral circulation ceases as the body prioritizes central circulation
- **Decreased urine output or anuria** — the kidneys cease filtering as perfusion declines
- **Unresponsiveness** — the patient no longer responds to voice or touch

Oral intake typically ceases in the active dying phase. Families often request that intravenous fluids be initiated or continued, fearing that their loved one is dying of thirst. The nurse must provide sensitive education: the absence of thirst is a normal part of the dying process; the body is shutting down, not starving; and fluid administration in this phase may actually increase respiratory secretions, edema, and discomfort.

### 6.7.2 Comfort Care in the Final Hours

In the active dying phase, all nursing interventions are oriented toward comfort. Non-essential medications and monitoring are discontinued. Blood pressure monitoring, pulse oximetry, and glucose checks are no longer appropriate when the patient is actively dying and not being treated for acute illness. Oral care — moisture swabs, lip balm — provides comfort even when the patient cannot swallow. Repositioning every two to four hours prevents pressure injury and maintains dignity. Environmental management — soft lighting, reduced noise, the presence of meaningful music or spiritual materials — creates conditions for a peaceful death (National Consensus Project for Quality Palliative Care, 2018).

Family members may need guidance on how to interact with an unresponsive dying patient. Nurses can offer: "Your mother may still be able to hear you. This is a good time to say anything you want her to know. Many families find it helpful to say 'I love you' or 'It's okay to go.'" This guidance can be transformative for families who feel helpless and do not know what their role is in the final hours.

### 6.7.3 Postmortem Care and Death Pronouncement

Following death, the nurse's responsibilities include postmortem care, notification of required parties, and support for the bereaved family. Postmortem care — bathing the body, positioning the hands, closing the eyes, removing tubes and catheters per policy — should be performed with the same respect and dignity afforded to living patients. Cultural and religious practices may govern which family members should participate in postmortem care, what should be done with the body before it is moved, and whether specific prayers or rituals should be performed.

The pronouncement of death — formally confirming that death has occurred — is typically a physician or advanced practice provider responsibility in inpatient settings, though scope-of-practice laws in some states authorize registered nurses to pronounce death in specific circumstances (e.g., home hospice). The nurse documents the time at which death was observed and the time of pronouncement, notifies the physician, and completes required notifications per institutional policy.

## Section 6.8 — Ethical Challenges in End-of-Life Care

### 6.8.1 Withholding Versus Withdrawing Treatment

One of the most common ethical questions in end-of-life care involves the moral and legal distinction — or lack thereof — between withholding and withdrawing life-sustaining treatment. From a bioethical standpoint, these two acts are considered morally equivalent: a patient's right to refuse treatment includes the right to request that a treatment already in progress be stopped. From a psychological standpoint, however, many nurses and physicians find withdrawal of treatment (e.g., discontinuing a ventilator) more emotionally difficult than withholding (e.g., not initiating CPR).

This psychological asymmetry has clinical consequences: research has documented that clinicians are more likely to offer fewer treatment options, or to present withdrawal as a more morally complex action, when in fact both actions represent the legitimate exercise of patient autonomy (Sprung et al., 2003). Nurses who understand the moral equivalence of withholding and withdrawing are better equipped to advocate for patients who request discontinuation of burdensome treatments.

> **EBP Connection: Family Meetings and ICU End-of-Life Decision-Making**  
> A Cochrane Review by Lautrette and colleagues (2007) found that a structured family meeting combined with a printed bereavement brochure in the ICU significantly reduced symptoms of anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress in bereaved family members compared to standard communication. The intervention required nurse and physician co-facilitation, active listening, and explicit acknowledgment of family emotions. This study supports the integration of structured communication training into ICU nursing competencies.

### 6.8.2 Aid in Dying

Medical aid in dying — the practice of prescribing lethal medications to terminally ill, mentally competent adults at their explicit request — is legal in ten U.S. states and the District of Columbia as of 2024, under statutes typically called "Death with Dignity" laws. Nurses practicing in jurisdictions where aid in dying is legal must be aware of the relevant scope-of-practice laws governing nursing's role in the process.

Nursing's position on aid in dying is governed by the American Nurses Association (ANA) Code of Ethics and individual state nurse practice acts. The ANA (2019) position statement holds that nurses should not be required to participate in aid in dying against their conscience but must provide patients with accurate information and appropriate referrals regardless of their personal beliefs. A nurse who objects to participating on conscientious grounds must still ensure that the patient has access to care through another provider.

### 6.8.3 Moral Distress in End-of-Life Nursing

**Moral distress** — the psychological suffering experienced when a nurse knows the ethically correct action but is prevented from taking it by institutional, hierarchical, or procedural constraints — is endemic in end-of-life care settings. Common sources of moral distress include:

- Perceived futile treatment being continued due to family pressure or physician reluctance to have goals-of-care conversations
- Inadequate symptom management due to prescribing practices or nursing hesitancy
- Patients dying without their preferences honored because advance directives were not documented or communicated
- Institutional cultures that discourage nurses from raising ethical concerns

Addressing moral distress requires both individual resilience strategies (reflective practice, peer support, debriefing) and systemic interventions (ethics consultation, unit-level ethical climate assessments, nursing leadership advocacy). The AACN's Moral Distress Thermometer is a validated tool for assessing moral distress in clinical nurses and can guide unit-level interventions (Epstein & Hamric, 2009).

> **Clinical Tip: Using the Ethics Consultation Service**  
> Every accredited hospital in the United States is required to have a mechanism for addressing ethical conflicts in care. The ethics consultation service — typically an interdisciplinary committee or team — can be requested by any member of the care team, including nurses. If you are experiencing moral distress because you believe a patient's end-of-life wishes are not being honored, or because you believe treatment is causing suffering without benefit, requesting an ethics consultation is an appropriate and professionally supported action. Document your concerns, consult your charge nurse or manager, and know that raising ethical concerns is an expression of professional integrity, not insubordination.

## Chapter Summary

End-of-life nursing encompasses one of the most profound dimensions of professional practice: the sustained commitment to accompany patients and families through dying with competence, compassion, and respect for human dignity. This chapter has traced the arc from the philosophical foundations of palliative care — its origins in Dame Cicely Saunders's concept of total pain — through the evidence-based communication frameworks that structure difficult conversations, the pharmacological and non-pharmacological strategies for managing end-of-life symptoms, and the psychological and spiritual dimensions of the dying experience. Advance care planning instruments — living wills, durable powers of attorney for health care, and POLST orders — provide the legal architecture for honoring patient preferences, but their effectiveness depends entirely on nurses who are willing and able to initiate conversations about values before crisis makes them impossible. Cultural and spiritual humility require nurses to approach each death as unique, governed by the patient's own frameworks of meaning rather than the clinician's assumptions. The final hours of life — and the care of the body and family after death — call for nursing presence of the highest order. And the ethical challenges of end-of-life care — from withholding versus withdrawing treatment to moral distress — demand nurses who can reason clearly under pressure and advocate courageously for their patients.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. Palliative care is appropriate from the point of diagnosis for any serious illness, not only at the end of life; it addresses physical, psychological, social, and spiritual suffering and includes the family as a unit of care.
2. The SPIKES protocol and the NURSE mnemonic provide evidence-based frameworks for delivering serious news and responding to emotional reactions in goals-of-care conversations.
3. Common end-of-life symptoms — pain, dyspnea, nausea, and terminal secretions — require proactive nursing assessment and management; opioids are the cornerstone of both pain and dyspnea management in actively dying patients.
4. Grief is not a linear progression through fixed stages; nurses should use multiple theoretical models to understand the varied experiences of dying patients and their bereaved families, including anticipatory grief.
5. Advance directives (living wills, DPAHC) and POLST orders translate patient values into documented preferences and portable medical orders; nurse-facilitated advance care planning increases completion rates and preference-honoring.
6. Cultural humility requires nurses to ask rather than assume about death rituals, disclosure preferences, and spiritual needs; every cultural tradition brings unique norms that shape the experience of dying.
7. Signs of active dying — mottling, Cheyne-Stokes respirations, terminal secretions, cooling extremities — signal that death is hours to days away; family education and comfort-focused nursing care are the clinical priorities.
8. Withholding and withdrawing life-sustaining treatment are morally equivalent acts; moral distress in end-of-life nursing can be addressed through reflective practice, peer support, and formal ethics consultation.

## Case Study: A Good Death

**Setting:** A 68-year-old retired teacher named Evelyn has been admitted to the palliative care unit with metastatic ovarian cancer. She has declined further chemotherapy. Her husband, Robert, and adult daughter, Sarah, are present. Evelyn is oriented but fatigued, and her pain is partially controlled on a morphine infusion.

---

**Scene 1:**

On admission, the palliative care nurse, James, performs an initial assessment. Evelyn tells him, "I've made peace with dying. I just don't want to hurt." Robert interrupts: "She can't give up. There must be something else they can do." Sarah is crying quietly in the corner. James recognizes that he is caring not just for Evelyn but for three people with different emotional states and different needs.

James uses the NURSE mnemonic to respond to Robert: "I can hear how much you love her and how hard this is to accept. That makes complete sense." He then creates a brief space for Robert to feel heard before returning to Evelyn's assessment. He ensures that Evelyn's pain is assessed using the numeric pain scale, documents her morphine requirement, and reviews her POLST form with the team.

---

**Scene 2:**

Three days later, Evelyn is showing signs of active dying: mottled lower extremities, periods of apnea, and decreased responsiveness. Her morphine infusion has been uptitrated twice. Robert is sitting at her bedside and approaches James: "Her breathing sounds terrible. Is she in pain? Are those fluids running — can you increase them to give her more strength?" James explains the signs of active dying, normalizes the sound of terminal secretions, and gently explains why increased fluids would not improve Evelyn's comfort and might worsen her respiratory symptoms. He encourages Robert to speak to Evelyn, noting that hearing is often the last sense to fade.

---

**Scene 3:**

Evelyn dies peacefully four hours later, with Robert and Sarah holding her hands. James enters the room, expresses his condolences, and asks about any cultural or religious practices the family wishes to observe before the body is moved. Sarah, who was raised Catholic, asks whether a chaplain can come to say a prayer. James contacts the chaplain immediately. He performs postmortem care with the assistance of a colleague, ensuring that Evelyn is positioned with dignity and that all lines are removed. He provides Sarah and Robert with information about bereavement support services and leaves a written condolence note in the bedside folder.

---

**Discussion Questions:**

1. How did James apply the NURSE mnemonic to respond therapeutically to Robert's emotional state without dismissing Evelyn's stated wishes? What communication skills were essential in this moment?
2. In Scene 2, Robert requested increased IV fluids during the active dying phase. How would you explain the rationale for not increasing fluids in a way that is compassionate, honest, and supportive of the family's understanding?
3. What signs of active dying were present in Scene 2, and what nursing assessments would you use to determine whether Evelyn's comfort was adequately maintained?
4. In Scene 3, James asked about cultural and religious practices before moving the body. How does this action reflect the principles of cultural humility introduced in Chapter 5, and why does it matter?
