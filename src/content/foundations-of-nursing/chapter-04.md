## Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the student will be able to:

1. Describe the components of the communication process and explain how each element influences healthcare interactions.
2. Distinguish between verbal and nonverbal communication and apply strategies to ensure message congruence in clinical settings.
3. Identify and apply the three phases of the therapeutic nurse-patient relationship (orientation, working, and termination).
4. Demonstrate therapeutic communication techniques (e.g., active listening, open-ended questions, reflection, silence) in clinical scenarios.
5. Recognize and avoid non-therapeutic communication patterns that impede trust and information exchange.
6. Apply motivational interviewing principles using the OARS technique to support behavior change in patients.
7. Adapt communication strategies across the lifespan and in high-stress or crisis situations.
8. Use SBAR and I-PASS frameworks to communicate critical patient information accurately to interdisciplinary team members.
9. Apply documentation standards, including legal considerations, to electronic health record (EHR) entries.

## Introduction

Consider this scenario: A 68-year-old man named Robert is admitted to a medical-surgical unit following a myocardial infarction. His nurse, Elise, enters his room to complete the admission assessment. Robert sits rigidly in the bed, arms crossed, gaze fixed on the window. "I feel fine," he says flatly when asked how he is doing. Elise pauses, pulls a chair close, sits at eye level, and says, "You've been through something significant today. I'd like to understand how you're really feeling." A long silence follows. Robert's shoulders drop slightly, and he begins to speak — about his fear of dying, about a brother who had a fatal heart attack at 65, about how he has not told his wife how worried he is.

In fewer than thirty seconds, through deliberate positioning, an open-ended invitation, and the therapeutic use of silence, Elise transformed a perfunctory intake interaction into a meaningful clinical encounter. Robert left that conversation with a nurse who understood his fears; Elise left it with information that would shape his education plan, discharge instructions, and referral for cardiac rehabilitation. No medication, no procedure, and no technology achieved that outcome — communication did.

**Therapeutic communication** is the most foundational clinical skill a nurse possesses. It is simultaneously the tool through which assessment data are gathered, the medium through which health education is delivered, and the mechanism through which trust is built and healing is supported. Yet communication is also among the most frequently cited root causes of preventable adverse events. The Joint Commission (2023) has consistently ranked communication failures among the top contributors to sentinel events, underscoring that the way nurses communicate is not merely a matter of style — it is a patient safety imperative.

This chapter provides a comprehensive framework for therapeutic communication in nursing practice. It begins with the foundational theory of how communication works (Section 4.1), then examines the unique dimensions of verbal and nonverbal messaging (Section 4.2), the structured phases of the therapeutic nurse-patient relationship (Section 4.3), specific evidence-based communication techniques (Section 4.4), and the barriers created by non-therapeutic patterns (Section 4.5). The chapter then addresses motivational interviewing for behavior change (Section 4.6), communication adaptations across the lifespan and in crisis (Section 4.7), interdisciplinary communication tools for team safety (Section 4.8), and documentation standards that preserve the communicative record legally and clinically (Section 4.9).

## Section 4.1 — The Communication Process in Healthcare

### 4.1.1 Components of the Communication Model

Every clinical interaction — from a brief vital-sign check to a lengthy family meeting at end of life — can be analyzed through the lens of communication theory. Understanding the structural components of communication allows nurses to diagnose breakdowns systematically and intervene purposefully rather than relying on intuition alone.

The classical transactional model of communication identifies six essential elements. The **sender** is the originator of a message — a nurse explaining a medication, a patient describing pain, or a physician dictating orders. The **message** is the information or meaning the sender intends to convey; it may be expressed verbally (through words), nonverbally (through body language, facial expressions, and tone), or in writing. The **channel** is the medium through which the message travels: a face-to-face conversation, a telephone call, an electronic health record note, or a text page. The **receiver** is the individual for whom the message is intended, who must decode and interpret what has been communicated. The **feedback loop** closes the transaction — the receiver's verbal or behavioral response confirms whether the message was received accurately. Finally, **noise** is any factor that distorts, interrupts, or degrades the message in transit (Kozier et al., 2021; Potter et al., 2023).

These elements do not operate in linear sequence. Communication is a dynamic, simultaneous, and bidirectional process: while a nurse is speaking, she is also receiving feedback through a patient's facial expression and posture, and that feedback shapes how she continues. Encoding refers to the process by which the sender translates an internal thought or intention into words, gestures, or symbols. Decoding refers to how the receiver interprets those signals and reconstructs meaning. Encoding and decoding are both filtered through each participant's unique experience, culture, emotional state, cognitive ability, and language proficiency — which is why two people can hear the same words and derive entirely different meanings from them.

### 4.1.2 Communication Channels in Clinical Practice

The choice of channel profoundly affects communication quality. Richness — the information-carrying capacity of a channel — varies substantially across available media. Face-to-face communication is the highest-richness channel because it conveys verbal content, nonverbal cues, paralanguage, and allows immediate feedback. For this reason, conversations about new diagnoses, informed consent, treatment options, and serious psychosocial concerns should occur in person whenever possible (Northouse & Northouse, 2020).

Telephone communication eliminates visual cues and reduces richness considerably, increasing the risk of misinterpretation. Safety protocols compensate for this limitation. The Joint Commission's National Patient Safety Goals require a "read-back" procedure for all verbal and telephone orders: the recipient repeats the order back to the prescriber, who confirms or corrects it before the recipient acts (The Joint Commission, 2023). Nurses must document verbal orders promptly and according to institutional policy.

Written communication — nursing notes, care plans, discharge instructions — provides a permanent record and allows for reflection before transmission, but it lacks the immediacy of feedback. Electronic messaging through secure hospital platforms offers speed and a written record, but the brevity of text-based messages risks oversimplification and loss of nuance. The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) strictly governs the use of electronic communications containing protected health information, prohibiting the use of personal email, consumer text messaging applications, and social media platforms for clinical communication (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2022).

> **Safety Alert: Verbal Orders and Read-Back Requirements**
> The Joint Commission requires healthcare personnel to verify all verbal and telephone orders using a complete read-back process: write down the order, read it back in full, and receive explicit confirmation from the ordering provider. Do not act on a verbal order until this confirmation is documented. Failure to complete read-back is a cited source of medication errors and wrong-procedure events. Always record the ordering provider's name, your name, the date and time, and the verbatim content of the order.

### 4.1.3 Factors That Influence Communication

No two clinical communication encounters are identical because each is shaped by an intersecting web of individual, relational, and environmental factors. Identifying these factors before initiating a clinical conversation allows the nurse to anticipate obstacles and modify the approach proactively.

*Environmental factors* include noise level, privacy, physical comfort, and lighting. A patient who is trying to discuss a sensitive diagnosis while sharing a room with another patient, separated only by a thin curtain, faces a barrier to authentic disclosure. Whenever possible, nurses should close room doors, draw curtains, and position themselves so that they are not standing over the patient or facing away. A noisy unit corridor is not an appropriate location for a medication teaching session.

*Timing and pacing* affect receptivity. A patient who is acutely distressed, experiencing uncontrolled pain, or waiting for an urgent procedure is physiologically and psychologically unavailable for education or detailed discussion. Effective nurses assess readiness before initiating communication and return when conditions are more favorable.

*Cultural and linguistic background* shape both the content of messages and the norms by which they are exchanged. Eye contact, personal space, the role of silence, the appropriateness of discussing illness directly, and decision-making authority all vary across cultures (Campinha-Bacote, 2019). Nurses who practice cultural humility — an ongoing process of self-reflection and openness rather than rote knowledge of cultural "rules" — communicate more effectively with patients from backgrounds different from their own.

*Emotional state and stress* impair both sending and receiving. A patient in acute grief may hear only fragments of what a nurse says; a nurse who is overwhelmed by a high patient census may speak efficiently but fail to attend fully to patient cues. Self-awareness — recognizing one's own emotional state and its potential impact on communication — is a core professional competency.

*Physical factors* such as pain, hearing impairment, intubation, altered consciousness, aphasia, and cognitive impairment create specific communication challenges requiring targeted adaptations. These are addressed in detail in Section 4.7.

## Section 4.2 — Verbal and Nonverbal Communication

### 4.2.1 Characteristics of Effective Verbal Communication

Words are the most consciously constructed element of communication, yet they account for a minority of the meaning received in a face-to-face encounter. Research consistently estimates that nonverbal channels carry 60–80% of interpersonal meaning, leaving verbal content responsible for the remaining fraction (Mehrabian, 1971; updated synthesis in Burgoon et al., 2016). Nevertheless, the precision and selection of words matter enormously in clinical contexts, where misunderstanding can result in medication errors, treatment refusal, or psychological harm.

Effective verbal communication in nursing is guided by four principles. *Clarity* means using language that is unambiguous and specific: "Take this pill once every morning with food" is clearer than "Take this as needed." *Simplicity* means avoiding jargon when communicating with patients and families. Medical terminology — hypertension, myocardial infarction, dyspnea, NPO — is efficient within the healthcare team but creates barriers when used with patients who lack clinical training. The rule is to use plain language with patients ("high blood pressure," "heart attack," "trouble breathing," "nothing to eat or drink") and reserve clinical terminology for professional contexts. *Brevity* means prioritizing essential information; information overload during patient teaching is a well-documented barrier to retention. *Timing* means delivering messages when the receiver is ready — assessing engagement before launching into an explanation and pausing to check for understanding (Kozier et al., 2021).

Beyond word selection, the *paralanguage* of verbal communication — the vocal qualities that accompany spoken words — powerfully shapes interpretation. Tone of voice can communicate warmth, impatience, condescension, or urgency regardless of the literal words chosen. Pace affects comprehension and emotional tone: slow, measured speech conveys calm and seriousness; rapid speech may signal anxiety or dismissiveness. Volume must be calibrated to the environment and the patient's hearing; speaking too softly in a noisy unit creates misunderstanding, while shouting strips dignity. Inflection — the rise and fall of pitch within a sentence — carries emotional meaning and emphasis.

### 4.2.2 Nonverbal Communication Domains

The nonverbal dimensions of communication operate continuously, often below conscious awareness, and they carry emotional and relational meaning with particular force in high-stakes clinical interactions. Four primary domains are relevant to nursing practice.

**Kinesics** encompasses body movement as a communication system, including facial expressions, eye contact, gestures, posture, and head movements. The face is especially expressive: the universally recognized facial expressions of happiness, sadness, fear, disgust, surprise, and anger are reliably interpreted across cultures (Ekman, 1992). In clinical settings, a nurse whose facial expression registers shock, disgust, or impatience at a patient's disclosure communicates a powerful negative message that verbal words cannot undo. Deliberate facial management — maintaining a calm, open, receptive expression — is a learnable professional skill. Eye contact signals attention, respect, and engagement; its appropriate duration and meaning vary by culture, but in Western clinical contexts, sustained, soft eye contact communicates that the nurse is present and listening (Arnold & Boggs, 2020).

**Proxemics** is the study of personal space and how the physical distance between communicants affects the interaction. Hall's (1966) classic spatial zones remain clinically relevant: the *intimate zone* (0–18 inches) is reserved for physical care and examination; the *personal zone* (1.5–4 feet) is the appropriate distance for most therapeutic conversations; the *social zone* (4–12 feet) characterizes more formal, less personal interactions; the *public zone* (12+ feet) is for group settings. Violating a patient's expected personal space without explanation — as occurs during physical examination — can trigger anxiety and defensiveness. Nurses who introduce themselves, explain what they are about to do, and ask permission before entering the intimate zone demonstrate respect and reduce threat responses (Arnold & Boggs, 2020).

**Haptics** refers to touch as communication. Therapeutic touch — a deliberate, appropriate physical gesture such as resting a hand on a patient's arm during a difficult conversation — can communicate empathy, presence, and comfort in ways words cannot. However, touch is among the most culturally variable and personally sensitive nonverbal behaviors. Cultural norms, personal history (including trauma), gender dynamics, and individual preference govern whether touch is experienced as comforting or intrusive. Nurses should observe patient cues, ask permission when in doubt, and never assume that touch is universally welcome. Touch for purposes of physical care does not require the same permission process but does require explanation.

**Paralanguage** — vocal qualities distinct from word content — was introduced in Section 4.2.1 and applies equally to nonverbal analysis. A sigh, a drawn-out "hmm," a change in vocal pitch, or an extended pause all carry meaning that the patient receives and interprets, whether the nurse intends to communicate it or not. Awareness of one's own paralanguage is a component of professional communication self-monitoring.

### 4.2.3 Congruence and Incongruence

Perhaps the most clinically significant principle of verbal-nonverbal interaction is **congruence** — the alignment between what is said and how it is communicated nonverbally. When a nurse says, "I have time for your questions," while glancing repeatedly at the door and holding a chart in front of her body, the verbal and nonverbal messages contradict each other. Research on incongruent messages consistently shows that when verbal and nonverbal signals conflict, receivers weight the nonverbal signal more heavily — because nonverbal behavior is harder to fake deliberately and is perceived as more authentic (Guerrero et al., 2011).

In practice, incongruence creates a specific clinical hazard: the patient who hears "you can ask me anything" but sees a nurse who is physically and emotionally unavailable will feel dismissed and will be less likely to ask questions, disclose concerns, or report symptoms. The nurse may walk away believing communication was effective while the patient is left confused, anxious, and poorly informed.

Congruence requires self-awareness and deliberate practice. Nurses who are hurried, stressed, or emotionally reactive must develop the capacity to modulate their nonverbal behavior consciously — not as a form of deception, but as a professional discipline that prioritizes the patient's experience during the encounter. Strategies include pausing briefly before entering a patient's room to compose oneself, putting down equipment before initiating a sensitive conversation, and sitting rather than standing when the topic warrants unhurried engagement.

*Clinical self-awareness exercise:* The next time you complete a patient interaction, ask yourself: Did my body language match my words? Did I maintain appropriate eye contact? Was my facial expression open and calm? Were there moments when my nonverbal signals might have communicated impatience or discomfort? What would I change?

## Section 4.3 — The Nurse-Patient Relationship: Phases and Boundaries

### 4.3.1 Hildegard Peplau's Interpersonal Theory

The concept of the nurse-patient relationship as a structured, purposeful, and therapeutic entity was most rigorously theorized by Hildegard Peplau, whose 1952 text *Interpersonal Relations in Nursing* established the interpersonal dimension of nursing as a legitimate clinical science. As described in Chapter 1, Peplau departed from the task-oriented, physician-assistant view of nursing that predominated in the mid-twentieth century and argued that the nurse-patient relationship was itself a healing instrument — that what transpired between nurse and patient was not merely the context in which care was delivered but was the care (Peplau, 1952/1991).

Peplau identified four nursing roles within the therapeutic relationship: *stranger* (the initial unfamiliar role), *resource person* (providing specific information), *teacher* (facilitating learning), and *counselor* (helping the patient integrate experience into present circumstances). She also identified the relationship as progressing through three distinct phases — orientation, working, and termination — each with characteristic tasks, nurse roles, and patient responses. This phase model remains the dominant framework for understanding therapeutic nurse-patient relationships in nursing education and practice (Arnold & Boggs, 2020; Forchuk, 2021).

### 4.3.2 Orientation Phase

The **orientation phase** begins at the first nurse-patient encounter and continues until the patient and nurse have established a mutually agreed-upon understanding of the patient's needs and the goals of their work together. During this phase, the patient is often anxious, unfamiliar with the healthcare environment, and uncertain about what to expect from the nurse.

The nurse's primary task in the orientation phase is to establish rapport and trust through consistent, honest, and respectful interaction. This begins with a formal introduction: stating one's name and role clearly, explaining the purpose of the interaction, and inviting the patient's participation. "Hello, Mr. Rivera, my name is Destiny and I am your nurse today. I'd like to spend the next 20 minutes completing your admission assessment so we can understand what brought you in and how we can help you. Is that all right?" This seemingly simple exchange accomplishes several therapeutic functions: it confirms identity and role, sets expectations for the interaction, acknowledges the patient's autonomy by asking permission, and invites a collaborative relationship.

During orientation, the nurse also assesses communication needs: Does the patient require an interpreter? Does the patient have hearing or vision impairment? What is the patient's preferred language and name? What is the patient's health literacy level? Obtaining this information at the outset prevents communication failures throughout the hospital stay (Kripalani et al., 2021).

Trust is built incrementally through behavioral consistency — doing what one says one will do, returning when promised, following through on commitments, and maintaining confidentiality. Patients who have experienced healthcare systems that were dismissive of their concerns, biased against their identity, or unresponsive to their needs bring a deficit of trust into the orientation phase. Building trust with these patients requires greater investment of time and deliberate repair of the relational rupture that prior experiences may have created.

### 4.3.3 Working Phase

The **working phase** is the longest phase of the therapeutic relationship and the period during which the substantive clinical and psychosocial work of nursing occurs. During this phase, the patient and nurse collaborate on identifying problems, pursuing goals, learning new information, developing coping strategies, and implementing the care plan. The nurse functions simultaneously as resource person, teacher, advocate, and counselor (Peplau, 1952/1991).

A key dynamic of the working phase is *transference* — the unconscious process by which a patient redirects feelings and expectations from past relationships onto the nurse. A patient who has been mistreated by authority figures may become hostile toward a nurse whose behavior does not merit hostility. A patient who lost a parent to a medical error may become excessively dependent on nurses as proxies for lost caregivers. Recognizing transference prevents the nurse from taking such responses personally and enables a professional, compassionate response: "I notice that you seem upset when I come in to check your vitals. I wonder if there's something about this situation that's been difficult for you. Would you like to talk about it?"

*Countertransference* is the complementary dynamic: the nurse's emotional responses to a patient that are rooted in the nurse's own personal history rather than in the patient's clinical reality. A nurse who becomes excessively attached to a pediatric patient, or who feels repulsed by a patient whose values differ sharply from her own, is experiencing countertransference. Left unmanaged, countertransference distorts clinical judgment and the therapeutic relationship. Reflective practice, peer consultation, and clinical supervision are the professional tools for managing it (Freshwater, 2019).

### 4.3.4 Termination Phase

The **termination phase** is the planned ending of the therapeutic relationship. In acute care, it most commonly occurs at discharge, shift change, or care transfer. Though brief compared to the working phase, the termination phase has significant clinical importance and is frequently managed poorly.

A well-managed termination prepares the patient for the change: it reviews what was accomplished, reinforces the discharge plan or care transition, acknowledges the patient's progress, and gives the patient an opportunity to ask final questions. It does not end abruptly or without explanation. "Mrs. Chen, I'll be ending my shift in about 30 minutes, so I wanted to take a few minutes now to make sure you know everything you need for tonight. Let me go over your medications one more time, and please tell me if anything feels unclear." This brief communication provides closure and safety while honoring the relational investment of the working phase.

Unplanned terminations — resulting from patient deterioration, unexpected transfer, or death — require different handling. When a patient is rapidly transferred to the ICU, for example, the bedside nurse cannot always complete a verbal termination. In such cases, thorough written documentation of the patient's history, current status, and ongoing needs is the nurse's tool for ensuring relational continuity across the care transition. For student nurses, clinical rotations create serial terminations; students should be encouraged to reflect on the emotional demands of these endings and process them constructively rather than avoiding the feelings they evoke.

### 4.3.5 Professional Boundaries

The therapeutic relationship is defined by its professional nature: unlike social or personal relationships, it exists specifically to serve the patient's health needs and operates within a set of professional and ethical boundaries that protect both parties.

**Professional boundaries** are the limits that define the appropriate scope of the nurse-patient relationship and distinguish it from friendship, romantic involvement, or exploitation. Boundary *crossings* are brief departures from strict therapeutic behavior that may or may not harm the relationship — for example, briefly sharing a personal story to normalize a patient's experience. Boundary *violations* are serious breaches that exploit the power differential inherent in the nurse-patient relationship, harm the patient, and are subject to professional discipline and legal sanction (National Council of State Boards of Nursing, 2018).

The proliferation of social media has created a new domain of boundary vulnerability. Nurses who connect with patients on personal social media accounts, share identifiable patient information online, or accept gifts or personal communication from patients through digital channels are engaging in boundary violations regardless of the apparent benignity of their intent. The American Nurses Association (ANA, 2023) has issued clear guidance that professional boundaries apply equally in digital and physical spaces, and that nurses' online conduct reflects directly on the profession's integrity.

> **Professional Practice: ANA Social Media Guidance**
> The American Nurses Association (2023) explicitly prohibits nurses from: (1) transmitting or placing online individually identifiable patient information; (2) taking photographs of patients without formal written consent; (3) making disparaging remarks about patients, colleagues, or employers; and (4) friending current patients on personal social media platforms. Violations may result in termination of employment, state board discipline, and civil or criminal liability. When in doubt about a social media post, apply this test: *Would I be comfortable if my patient, employer, and nursing board each read this?* If the answer is no, do not post.

## Section 4.4 — Therapeutic Communication Techniques

### 4.4.1 Active Listening

**Active listening** is the foundation on which all therapeutic communication techniques rest. It is not passive receipt of sound but an active, effortful process of attending to everything the patient is communicating — the words, the vocal qualities, the body language, the emotional undertone, the timing of pauses, and the content that is conspicuously absent or avoided (Egan, 2019).

The SOLER model — adapted for nursing as SURETY — provides a behavioral framework for active listening posture (Stickley, 2011). **S** directs the nurse to sit squarely facing the patient, or to position at the same level. **U** calls for uncrossing the arms and legs, maintaining an open, non-defensive posture. **R** emphasizes relaxation — avoiding fidgeting, checking equipment, or looking at documentation while the patient speaks. **E** addresses eye contact, maintaining soft, culturally appropriate eye contact throughout the interaction. **T** reminds the nurse to use therapeutic touch judiciously. **Y** encourages nurses to attend to their own intuition — what they sense as well as what they hear.

Minimizing interruptions is essential. Research on physician-patient communication shows that clinicians typically interrupt patients within 11–23 seconds of the patient beginning to speak (Ospina et al., 2019). Nurses who allow patients to complete their opening narrative without interruption gather substantially more clinically useful information and signal respect in a way that fundamentally changes the relational tone of the encounter. "Tell me in your own words what's been bothering you" followed by attentive silence invites a fuller, more authentic response than a rapid-fire series of closed questions.

### 4.4.2 Verbal Therapeutic Techniques

The following evidence-based verbal techniques constitute the core toolkit for therapeutic nursing communication. Each is presented with a brief rationale, clinical application, and illustrative example.

**Open-ended questions** invite the patient to respond with narrative rather than a yes/no answer. They communicate that the nurse wants to understand the patient's full experience rather than simply collect data. *Example:* "Tell me more about what you've been experiencing at home." *Note:* The classic open-ended question stem "Tell me about..." or "Help me understand..." is often more effective than "Can you describe...?" which technically allows a one-word answer.

**Focused questions** narrow the scope of inquiry without leading the patient toward a particular answer. *Example:* "Of all the things that have been difficult since your surgery, which has bothered you most?" Used after open-ended exploration, focused questions help the nurse efficiently identify priorities for care.

**Reflection** mirrors back the patient's words or feelings to demonstrate understanding and invite deeper exploration. Simple reflection repeats key words verbatim; complex reflection paraphrases or adds meaning. *Example (simple):* Patient: "I'm scared about the surgery." Nurse: "You're scared." *Example (complex):* "It sounds like the uncertainty about what they'll find is the hardest part."

**Clarification** seeks to ensure accurate understanding of an ambiguous or unclear statement. *Example:* "When you say the pain 'comes and goes,' can you tell me more about how often it comes and how long it lasts?" Clarification prevents incorrect assumptions from embedding themselves in the clinical record and care plan.

**Restatement** repeats the essential content of what the patient has said using slightly different wording, signaling that the nurse has heard and prompting the patient to continue. *Example:* Patient: "I've been taking my blood pressure pill but I keep forgetting the other one." Nurse: "You've been consistent with one of your medications but the second one has been harder to remember."

**Summarizing** condenses the content of a longer exchange and confirms mutual understanding before proceeding. It is particularly important at clinical transitions — the end of an assessment, the conclusion of a teaching session, the wrap-up before discharge. *Example:* "So what I'm hearing is that you have been managing the pain fairly well, but the fatigue is really limiting your daily activities, and you're most worried about whether you'll be able to go back to work. Does that capture it?"

**Acknowledging feelings** names the emotional content of the patient's communication explicitly and without judgment. *Example:* "It makes complete sense that you'd feel overwhelmed by all of this." Acknowledgment validates the patient's experience and is a prerequisite for effective teaching — a patient who does not feel heard is not ready to learn.

**Silence** is the therapeutic use of deliberate, attentive pauses. Silence communicates that the nurse has space for the patient's experience and does not need to fill every quiet moment with information. It allows patients to gather their thoughts, control the pace of sensitive disclosures, and experience a quality of unhurried presence that is itself therapeutic. Cultural variation is important: silence has different meanings and tolerable durations across cultures. In Western clinical contexts, pauses of 10–20 seconds — which feel long to the novice clinician — are often productive. *Practice strategy:* If you feel uncomfortable with silence, silently count to ten before speaking. Nine times out of ten, the patient will fill the silence with something clinically important.

**Sharing observations** uses the nurse's own perceptions as clinical data without making diagnostic judgments. *Example:* "I notice that you seem more quiet today than you were yesterday. How are you feeling?" This technique opens a door to emotional assessment without forcing or assuming a particular response.

**Giving information** delivers accurate, timely, plain-language health information that responds to the patient's identified needs or questions. Information-giving is a therapeutic technique only when it is calibrated to the patient's health literacy level, delivered in digestible portions, and accompanied by teach-back verification.

**Confrontation** (caring) — also called *caring challenge* — gently draws attention to an inconsistency between what the patient says and what the patient does, or between the patient's stated goals and observed behavior. *Example:* "You've told me how important it is to you to get back to playing with your grandchildren, and I've also noticed that you haven't been doing your breathing exercises. I wonder if there's something getting in the way that we haven't talked about yet." This is emphatically not a blaming or accusatory technique; it is an invitation to explore the real barriers to behavior change.

### 4.4.3 De-escalation Techniques

Patients in acute distress, pain, or fear can escalate to agitation or verbal aggression. Recognizing and responding to early behavioral cues — rising voice volume, increased motor activity, pacing, clenching, threatening language — is a patient safety competency. Early intervention prevents escalation to physical aggression and protects both patient and staff.

The *LOWLINE approach* provides a structured de-escalation framework: **L**isten without interruption; **O**ffer empathy; **W**ait for a pause before responding; **L**ower your voice rather than raising it; **I**nvite collaboration ("What would help you feel safer right now?"); **N**egotiate a solution; **E**xplain what will happen next (Kynoch et al., 2011). The nurse's own nonverbal behavior is critical: maintaining a calm, open posture, speaking slowly and quietly, avoiding sudden movements, and standing at an angle (rather than directly facing an agitated person, which can feel confrontational) all reduce physiological arousal.

De-escalation is distinct from crisis intervention. When de-escalation is insufficient and safety is at risk, nurses must involve colleagues, security, or emergency response teams in accordance with institutional policy. Documentation of behavioral observations, the de-escalation measures attempted, and the patient's response is a legal and clinical requirement.

## Section 4.5 — Non-Therapeutic Communication: Barriers and Pitfalls

### 4.5.1 Categories of Non-Therapeutic Communication

Non-therapeutic communication is any pattern that impedes the therapeutic relationship, blocks information exchange, undermines patient autonomy, or damages trust. These patterns are not always malicious — most arise from habit, discomfort, time pressure, or educational gaps. But their impact on patients can be profound, and recognizing them is the first step toward replacing them.

**False reassurance** offers comfort that is not supported by clinical reality. *Example:* "You're going to be just fine" said to a patient with a newly diagnosed serious illness. False reassurance closes off the patient's opportunity to express legitimate fears, denies them information they may need to make decisions, and damages trust when the reassurance proves unfounded. The therapeutic alternative acknowledges the patient's feeling without making false promises: "This is a lot to take in. It's completely understandable that you're scared. Let's talk about what we know and what the next steps are."

**Giving advice** substitutes the nurse's judgment for the patient's own decision-making. *Example:* "If I were you, I would definitely choose the surgery." This violates patient autonomy and positions the nurse as the authority over the patient's life choices. The therapeutic alternative uses motivational interviewing techniques to help the patient clarify their own values and reach their own decision.

**Changing the subject** cuts off a patient's emotional expression, usually because the nurse is uncomfortable with the content. *Example:* Patient: "I've been thinking a lot about whether I want to go through with more chemotherapy." Nurse: "Let's check your blood pressure first." Changing the subject communicates that the patient's concern is not welcome and should not be spoken aloud. It reliably suppresses disclosure and forces the patient to manage their distress alone.

**Closed questions** constrain the patient's response to a binary choice and collect minimal information. "Are you in pain?" elicits a yes or no; "Tell me about your discomfort" elicits a narrative. Closed questions have their place in focused clinical assessment, but an intake assessment conducted entirely with closed questions misses the vast majority of what the patient needs to communicate.

**Paternalism** treats patients as incapable of making decisions about their own care and communicates this through language, tone, and behavior. *Examples:* Talking to the patient's adult family members rather than to the patient; simplifying information to the point of infantilization; proceeding with a procedure without explaining or asking permission. Paternalism is particularly damaging in interactions with older adults, individuals with disabilities, and patients from marginalized groups who have experienced systemic disempowerment in healthcare settings.

**Excessive self-disclosure** shifts the focus of the interaction from the patient to the nurse. *Example:* Responding to a patient's account of a difficult pregnancy by sharing a lengthy personal story about one's own pregnancy complications. A brief, relevant self-disclosure can normalize a patient's experience; an extended personal narrative derails the therapeutic encounter.

**Stereotyping** makes clinical assumptions based on group membership — age, race, culture, religion, sexual orientation, body size, or diagnosis — rather than on individual assessment. *Example:* Assuming that an elderly patient cannot learn to use an insulin pen, or that a patient from a specific cultural background does not want to discuss terminal illness directly. Stereotyping is a barrier to individualized care and a driver of health disparities (Betancourt et al., 2022).

**Defensive responses** protect the healthcare system, individual providers, or institutional policies at the patient's expense. *Example:* "The doctor would have told you if that were important." Defensive responses invalidate patient concerns, undermine disclosure, and are closely associated with failure to identify safety problems before they cause harm.

The following table summarizes each non-therapeutic pattern alongside its therapeutic alternative, providing a quick-reference guide for clinical practice and self-assessment.

| Non-Therapeutic Pattern | Example Statement | Therapeutic Alternative |
|-------------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| False reassurance | "Don't worry — everything is going to be fine." | "This is a lot to process. It's understandable to feel scared. Let's talk about what we know." |
| Giving advice | "If I were you, I would just have the surgery." | "What feels most important to you as you think about your options?" |
| Changing the subject | "Let's take your blood pressure first." *(said to cut off an emotional disclosure)* | "I want to hear what you're thinking. Let me sit down for a minute." |
| Closed questions | "Are you in pain?" | "Tell me about any discomfort you've been having." |
| Paternalism | "Your daughter and I have already decided what's best for you." | "This is your decision to make. What questions do you have about your options?" |
| Excessive self-disclosure | "I know exactly how you feel — the same thing happened to me." *(followed by a lengthy personal story)* | A brief normalizing statement, then redirecting: "Many people feel that way. Tell me more about your experience." |
| Stereotyping | "At your age, most people just want to be comfortable." | "What are your priorities for your care at this stage?" |
| Defensive response | "The doctor would have told you if that were important." | "That's an important concern. Let me find out the answer and get back to you." |

### 4.5.2 Environmental and Systemic Barriers

Beyond individual communication patterns, structural factors create persistent barriers to effective therapeutic communication in clinical settings. *Noise and interruptions* are endemic in acute care environments; open nursing stations, overhead paging, visitor traffic, and the physical design of shared rooms all undermine the privacy and focus that therapeutic communication requires. *Time pressure and documentation burden* compress the time available for meaningful patient interaction; nurses managing high patient-to-nurse ratios report that brief, task-focused exchanges often substitute for genuine therapeutic communication simply because there is no time for anything else (Aiken et al., 2020).

**Health literacy mismatch** is the gap between the literacy level at which health information is written and the reading and comprehension skills of the patient. The Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality (2020) estimates that nearly half of American adults have low health literacy, which is associated with worse medication adherence, higher hospitalization rates, and poorer chronic disease management. Nurses who routinely use teach-back methodology — asking patients to explain instructions in their own words — identify and correct health literacy mismatches in real time.

Language differences and inadequate interpreter services represent a structural barrier with documented consequences for patient safety. Section 4.7.4 addresses this in depth.

### 4.5.3 Communication Failure and Patient Safety

The human cost of communication failure in healthcare is substantial. The Joint Commission's (2023) analysis of sentinel events — unexpected deaths and serious injuries — found that communication breakdown was a contributing factor in approximately 70% of cases. Common scenarios include incomplete or inaccurate handoff communication, failure to communicate critical laboratory values in a timely manner, miscommunication about medication orders, and inadequate informed consent discussions.

The mechanisms through which communication failure causes harm are predictable: information about a patient's deteriorating condition is not conveyed because the nurse felt uncertain about interrupting the physician; an allergy is not re-confirmed before a procedure; a discharge instruction is given once, rapidly, in jargon, without teach-back, and without written support. Each of these failures is preventable with the application of the communication skills described in this and subsequent sections. The SBAR framework, described in Section 4.8, was developed specifically to structure urgent clinical communication in a way that ensures completeness and clarity.

## Section 4.6 — Motivational Interviewing: The OARS Technique

### 4.6.1 Foundations of Motivational Interviewing

**Motivational interviewing** (MI) is a collaborative, person-centered counseling style that uses specific conversational techniques to strengthen a patient's own motivation and commitment to change. Developed by clinical psychologists William Miller and Stephen Rollnick in the 1980s and continuously refined through decades of research, MI is one of the most rigorously evidence-based behavioral counseling approaches available in healthcare (Miller & Rollnick, 2023). It is now taught in nursing education as a foundational competency for managing chronic disease, promoting adherence, and supporting the behavior changes that underlie most of the major causes of preventable morbidity and mortality.

The philosophical spirit of MI rests on four interlocking principles captured in the acronym PACE: **P**artnership, **A**cceptance, **C**ompassion, and **E**vocation. Partnership positions the nurse and patient as collaborators, not as expert and recipient. Acceptance involves unconditional positive regard — accepting the patient's current behavior without judgment while believing in the patient's capacity to change. Compassion orients the nurse's agenda entirely toward the patient's well-being and goals, not the nurse's own discomfort with the patient's choices. Evocation rests on the premise that patients already possess the motivation and resources needed for change; the nurse's role is to draw those resources out rather than to instill them from outside (Miller & Rollnick, 2023).

Understanding where a patient is in the change process is foundational to MI application. The Transtheoretical Model of behavior change (Prochaska & DiClemente, 1983) describes a series of stages through which people typically move as they contemplate and make behavioral changes: *precontemplation* (not yet aware of or considering change), *contemplation* (aware of the problem, weighing pros and cons), *preparation* (committed to change and beginning to plan), *action* (actively implementing new behavior), and *maintenance* (sustaining the new behavior over time). Nurses who accurately identify a patient's stage can match their communication strategy accordingly — a precontemplative patient is not ready for action planning and will not benefit from instructions they are not yet motivated to follow.

### 4.6.2 The OARS Core Skills

The **OARS** framework describes the four core conversational skills of motivational interviewing. Together, these skills create the conditions under which patients can articulate, explore, and strengthen their own motivation to change.

**Open questions** are questions that invite patients to reflect and respond with their own thoughts, values, and concerns rather than providing a predetermined answer. In MI, open questions are used strategically to evoke the patient's perspective on their situation and their ambivalence about change. *Examples:* "What would be different about your life if you made this change?" "What worries you most about how things are going?" "What have you already tried, and what did you learn from that?" The nurse listens carefully to what the patient emphasizes, what they avoid, and what their language reveals about motivation versus resistance.

**Affirmations** are statements that recognize the patient's strengths, efforts, and intrinsic worth — not empty praise ("Good job!"), but genuine recognition of something specific and true. *Examples:* "The fact that you kept this appointment, even on a difficult week, tells me something about how seriously you're taking your health." "Coming to terms with this diagnosis has not been easy, and you've shown real resilience." Affirmations reinforce the therapeutic alliance and activate the patient's sense of self-efficacy — the belief that change is possible.

**Reflections** are the most foundational MI skill and the one that most sharply distinguishes MI from conventional interviewing. A reflection is a statement, not a question, that mirrors back what the nurse hears — in both content and meaning. Simple reflections repeat or rephrase the patient's words closely. Complex reflections add meaning, highlight ambivalence, or amplify the emotional content of what the patient has said. *Simple example:* Patient: "I know I should stop drinking so much, but I don't know if I can." Nurse: "Quitting feels important to you, and at the same time, it feels uncertain." *Complex example — double-sided reflection:* "On one hand, you really want to be there for your kids as they grow up; on the other hand, making changes to your diet feels almost impossible right now." Reflections communicate deep understanding and move the conversation forward without directing or pressuring the patient.

**Summaries** collect and present back to the patient the key themes of what they have shared, serving three functions: collecting (grouping important elements), linking (connecting material across the conversation), and transitioning (signaling a shift to a different topic or a decision point). *Transitional summary example:* "Let me make sure I'm understanding where things stand. You've told me that you really want to control your blood sugar for your family's sake, that the biggest barrier has been portion sizes at your workplace, and that you've actually had some success using the app I showed you last time. Is there anything important I've missed? Given all of that, what feels like the most useful next step?"

### 4.6.3 Change Talk and Sustain Talk

A core clinical skill in MI is the ability to distinguish *change talk* — patient statements that favor movement toward a goal — from *sustain talk* — statements that favor the status quo or resistance to change. The DARN-C framework provides language categories for recognizing change talk: **D**esire ("I want to exercise more"), **A**bility ("I think I could do this if I had support"), **R**eason ("I know it would help my blood pressure"), **N**eed ("I have to do something about this"), and **C**ommitment ("I'm going to try the low-sodium diet this week"). Commitment language is the strongest predictor of actual behavioral change and warrants specific reflection and reinforcement (Miller & Rollnick, 2023).

When patients express sustain talk — "I've tried before and failed," "I don't think it will make a difference," "My family eats the same thing, it's not just about me" — the effective MI response is to roll with resistance rather than confront or argue with it. Arguing triggers psychological reactance: the harder a clinician pushes, the more firmly a patient defends the status quo. Rolling with resistance means acknowledging the patient's perspective, reflecting the ambivalence without judgment, and returning gently to the patient's own expressed goals. "You're right that change is hard, especially when it affects the whole family. And I also heard you say earlier that you're worried about your heart. I'm curious what you think would have to happen for a small change to feel possible."

### 4.6.4 Brief MI in Clinical Settings

Full MI sessions are practiced by trained behavioral health counselors in dedicated appointments. Nurses in acute care, primary care, and specialty settings can apply *brief MI* — a condensed adaptation that uses the same core skills within a time-limited clinical encounter. Research demonstrates that even brief MI conversations of 15–20 minutes significantly increase readiness to change for tobacco use, medication adherence, alcohol use, physical activity, and dietary modification (Lundahl et al., 2013).

> **Evidence-Based Practice Spotlight: Motivational Interviewing in Nursing**
> A systematic review by Lundahl et al. (2013) examined 48 clinical trials of MI conducted by nurses, physicians, and other clinicians in a variety of healthcare settings. Findings demonstrated that MI produced statistically significant and clinically meaningful effects on tobacco cessation, medication adherence, physical activity, and alcohol use reduction. Effect sizes were modest but consistent, supporting the conclusion that even brief MI delivered by non-specialist clinicians produces measurable behavior change outcomes. The authors recommend that all nurses receive foundational MI training as part of pre-licensure education.

## Section 4.7 — Communication Across the Lifespan and in Crisis Situations

### 4.7.1 Communicating with Children and Adolescents

Communicating effectively with pediatric patients requires understanding how cognitive and emotional development shapes a child's capacity to understand, process, and respond to clinical communication. Jean Piaget's stages of cognitive development and Erik Erikson's psychosocial stages both provide frameworks that have direct practical implications for nursing communication (Hockenberry et al., 2022).

In early childhood (ages 2–7), children are in Piaget's preoperational stage: they are concrete thinkers, highly egocentric (they interpret events in relation to themselves), and highly susceptible to magical thinking (believing, for example, that their illness is punishment for something they did wrong). Nurses communicating with young children should use simple, concrete language; avoid figures of speech that may be interpreted literally ("We're going to take your blood" may terrify a child who hears it as a threat); and never deceive a child about a painful procedure ("This won't hurt at all" — when it will — destroys trust permanently). Therapeutic play and age-appropriate medical equipment play (letting a child listen to a heartbeat with a toy stethoscope) reduce anxiety and build cooperation.

In middle childhood (ages 7–11), children enter Piaget's concrete operations stage: they can follow logical sequences, understand cause and effect, and handle more complete information. School-age children want explanations, benefit from procedural preparation, and respond well to being given choices ("Would you like the injection in your left arm or your right arm?"). Including parents in communication is essential; simultaneously, the child's growing autonomy should be honored: direct questions to the child before asking the parent.

Adolescents are capable of formal operational thinking — abstract reasoning, hypothetical scenarios, and consideration of future consequences — but are profoundly influenced by peer norms, identity concerns, and emotional volatility characteristic of Erikson's identity versus role confusion stage. Confidentiality concerns are paramount: in many jurisdictions, adolescents have the legal right to consent to care for certain conditions (sexual health, mental health, substance use) without parental knowledge. Nurses must know the relevant state laws and inform adolescents of the limits of confidentiality at the outset of sensitive conversations.

### 4.7.2 Communicating with Older Adults

Aging is associated with sensory changes that directly affect communication: approximately one-third of adults over 65 have significant hearing loss (presbycusis), and a comparable proportion have clinically relevant visual impairment (American Foundation for the Blind, 2022). Nurses should routinely assess hearing and vision status at admission, confirm that patients have and are wearing their corrective devices, and adapt communication accordingly. For patients with hearing loss: face them directly, speak clearly (not excessively loudly, which distorts speech), reduce background noise, and use written backup for key information.

**Elderspeak** — a communication register characterized by simplified vocabulary, shortened sentences, slow rate, exaggerated intonation, high pitch, and terms of endearment ("sweetie," "dear," "honey") directed at older adults — is a well-documented form of ageism that communicates disrespect and infantilizes the recipient. Research by Kemper and colleagues demonstrates that elderspeak elicits behavioral resistance to care, reduces cooperation, and is associated with poorer cognitive performance in patients with dementia (Kemper & Mitzner, 2001; Williams et al., 2019). Nurses must consciously avoid elderspeak regardless of the patient's age or cognitive status, maintaining a respectful, adult-to-adult communication register at all times.

Cognitive impairment presents specific communication challenges. For patients with mild to moderate dementia, evidence-based strategies include: approaching from the front (never approaching from behind, which can startle and distress), using the patient's preferred name, speaking slowly and in short sentences, allowing extra time for processing and response, avoiding arguments about factual errors (gently redirecting rather than correcting), and using validation therapy — acknowledging the emotional reality of what the patient is experiencing even when the factual content is confused (Stokes, 2017). "You miss being home. That makes a lot of sense" is a more therapeutic response to an agitated patient who insists on leaving than "You can't go home, you're in the hospital."

### 4.7.3 Communicating with Patients in Crisis

Crisis communication — the application of communication skills in situations of acute psychological distress — requires a specific adaptation of therapeutic principles. Patients experiencing acute grief (learning of a death, receiving a terminal diagnosis), acute trauma (assault, sudden injury, unexpected hospitalization), or acute psychiatric crisis (suicidal ideation, psychotic episode) are in a state of neurobiological activation that fundamentally limits their cognitive processing. Information delivered in crisis rarely registers; instead, the nurse's presence, tone, and relational availability are the primary therapeutic tools.

The principles of effective crisis communication include: *safety first* — ensuring both physical and psychological safety; *empathic presence* — communicating genuine, unhurried attentiveness; *non-judgment* — making no evaluative statements about the patient's situation or emotional response; and *pacing* — following the patient's lead rather than moving the conversation toward resolution before the patient is ready (Roberts, 2005).

When a patient receives a devastating diagnosis, the nurse's instinct may be to fill the silence with information, reassurance, or action. The evidence supports doing the opposite: sitting with the patient, acknowledging the enormity of the moment, and waiting. "I can stay with you right now" is often the most therapeutic statement available. Information delivery can wait until the acute shock response has subsided; attempting health teaching in the immediate aftermath of a devastating disclosure is generally futile and may be harmful.

Distinguishing a therapeutic communication response from a crisis that requires specialized intervention is a critical competency. Expressions of suicidal ideation, active psychosis, or acute trauma require immediate escalation to mental health crisis services, safety planning, and documentation. The nurse's role is first-responder support and appropriate escalation, not independent crisis counseling.

### 4.7.4 Communication with Non-English-Speaking Patients

Language access in healthcare is both an ethical obligation and a federal legal requirement. Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits discrimination on the basis of national origin and requires federally funded healthcare facilities to provide meaningful access to patients with limited English proficiency (LEP). Section 1557 of the Affordable Care Act (2010) further strengthens and expands these requirements. Patients with LEP who do not receive language-adequate care experience higher rates of adverse events, longer lengths of stay, lower satisfaction, and greater rates of misdiagnosis than English-speaking patients (Divi et al., 2007; Flores, 2020).

The *ad hoc* use of family members or untrained bilingual staff as interpreters — a common but dangerous practice — creates multiple clinical and ethical hazards: family members may filter, edit, or omit information based on their own emotional investment; they may lack the medical vocabulary to interpret accurately; they create HIPAA concerns by exposing protected health information without formal consent; and they place an inappropriate emotional burden on individuals who may themselves need support (Flores, 2020). Minor children should never be used as interpreters under any circumstances.

Professional medical interpreters — whether in person, by video, or by telephone — are trained in medical terminology, confidentiality requirements, and the professional ethics of interpretation. When working with a medical interpreter, nurses should: position themselves facing the patient (not the interpreter); speak to the patient directly in the second person ("You told me...," not "Tell her that..."); speak in short phrases and pause to allow interpretation; avoid complex compound sentences; and confirm understanding using teach-back directed at the patient.

## Section 4.8 — Interdisciplinary Communication: SBAR and I-PASS Handoff Tools

### 4.8.1 The SBAR Framework

Communication between nurses and physicians has historically been characterized by a mismatch in style: physicians are trained to communicate in brief, hierarchically structured problem-statement formats, while nurses are trained to provide contextual narrative. This mismatch creates friction and, more dangerously, information loss at moments when clinical urgency demands precision. The **SBAR** framework was developed to bridge this gap by giving nurses a shared structure for organizing urgent clinical communication that mirrors the concise, structured style that physicians expect (Leonard et al., 2004).

SBAR was adapted for healthcare from communication protocols used in military aviation and nuclear submarine operations — contexts where communication precision under stress is a matter of survival. The Institute for Healthcare Improvement (IHI) has promoted SBAR as a standard for clinical handoffs and escalation calls, and it is now endorsed by The Joint Commission and incorporated into the communication standards of most major health systems.

The four components of SBAR work together to deliver complete clinical information efficiently. **S — Situation** is a one- to two-sentence statement of the immediate, current problem: "I am calling about Mr. Thomas in room 412. He is a 58-year-old post-operative day 2 following a right hip replacement, and I am concerned because his heart rate has been increasing over the past hour and he is now tachycardic at 118." **B — Background** provides the relevant history and clinical context needed to understand the situation: "He has a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes. His last set of vitals two hours ago showed HR 98, BP 134/82, SpO2 97% on room air. He has received two doses of IV ketorolac today. His urine output for the past four hours has been 30 mL/hour." **A — Assessment** conveys the nurse's clinical judgment about what is happening — the working hypothesis: "I am concerned that he may be bleeding internally or developing early hemorrhagic shock. He appears pale and is more anxious than he was this morning." Finally, **R — Recommendation** states what the nurse is requesting or proposing: "I am requesting that you come to evaluate him now, and I would like to draw a STAT CBC and BMP. I have already called the charge nurse and have an IV access ready."

This structured call takes approximately 60–90 seconds to deliver and provides the physician with everything needed to triage the request appropriately. The specificity of the recommendation is critical: research by Leonard et al. (2004) demonstrates that nurses who make explicit recommendations rather than vague requests ("I think something is wrong") receive faster and more appropriate clinical responses.

> **Clinical Tip: Prepare SBAR Before You Call**
> Before placing an urgent call to a physician, gather the following: the patient's name, room number, and medical record number; current vital signs and recent trend; the relevant history; your clinical assessment; and your specific request. Practice delivering your SBAR aloud before you dial. A prepared, confident SBAR call communicates professional competence and is more likely to result in timely action than a disorganized one. Never call a provider with a concern and respond to "What do you want me to do?" with "I don't know — I just thought you should know."

### 4.8.2 The I-PASS Handoff Tool

SBAR is designed for urgent, vertical communication (nurse to physician, nurse to rapid response team). A different tool is needed for *routine horizontal handoffs* — the shift-to-shift transfers of patient care that occur multiple times daily in every inpatient unit. Ineffective shift handoffs are a major source of adverse events: missed information, incomplete task lists, and unclear patient status contribute to medication errors, falls, failure to rescue, and delayed treatment (Starmer et al., 2014).

The **I-PASS** framework was developed by a multidisciplinary team at Boston Children's Hospital through a rigorous quality improvement study (Starmer et al., 2014) and is now adopted across pediatric and adult inpatient settings.

The five components of I-PASS move sequentially through a complete patient handoff. **I — Illness Severity** is a rapid global assessment — "stable," "watcher" (condition may deteriorate), or "unstable" — that immediately orients the receiving nurse to the level of vigilance required. **P — Patient Summary** provides a concise statement of the patient's diagnosis, clinical trajectory, active problems, and relevant history, forming the narrative context of the handoff. **A — Action List** is an explicit, prioritized enumeration of tasks that are pending or ongoing and must be completed, including who is responsible for each item — for example: "CBC results are pending from 1800 — please follow up and call Dr. Reyes if hemoglobin drops below 8. Wound dressing is due at 2200. Physical therapy evaluation scheduled for 0800 tomorrow." **S — Situation Awareness and Contingency Planning** provides anticipatory guidance about what might change, what to watch for, and what to do if it does: "If his pain is not controlled with the current PCA settings after this next hour, the plan is to call anesthesia for reassessment. If he becomes febrile above 38.5°C, draw blood cultures and call the attending." Finally, **S — Synthesis by Receiver** requires the receiving nurse to verbally confirm their understanding of the key points — a read-back that closes the loop and surfaces any misunderstanding before the departing nurse leaves the unit: "So Mr. Thomas is a watcher, the CBC is pending, I need to call Dr. Reyes if the hemoglobin is below 8, and I should watch for fever."

The Starmer et al. (2014) landmark study demonstrated that implementing I-PASS in a pediatric residency program reduced medical errors by 23% and preventable adverse events by 30%, with no significant reduction in resident education time. Subsequent studies in nursing contexts have demonstrated similar safety gains.

### 4.8.3 Escalating Concerns: Chain of Command

There are clinical situations in which the standard communication channels — calling a physician, using SBAR — do not produce an adequate or timely response. A nurse who has called a physician twice about a patient with worsening respiratory distress and received only non-urgent instructions to "continue monitoring" is facing a chain of command situation. Every healthcare institution is required to have a clear chain of command policy that specifies how nurses should escalate unresolved patient safety concerns.

The IESB escalation model provides a structured approach: **I**dentify the concern clearly; **E**xplain the clinical findings and your assessment; **S**eek a response, stating the time frame in which you need one; **B**ring in the next level of authority if the response is inadequate or absent. Typical escalation sequence: attending physician → supervising resident or fellow → attending of record → department chief → hospital administrator on call.

Rapid Response Teams (RRTs), also called Medical Emergency Teams, represent a parallel escalation pathway for patients who are deteriorating but have not yet reached the criteria for a full code. RRTs are designed to bring critical care expertise to the bedside before a life-threatening event occurs. Nurses are not only authorized but expected to activate the RRT when a patient meets established deterioration criteria — rising concern, respiratory rate > 28, heart rate < 40 or > 130, systolic BP < 90, SpO2 < 90%, altered mental status, or a nurse's clinical judgment that the patient "just doesn't look right." After every escalation call, the nurse must document the time, the concerns communicated, the response received, and the clinical outcome.

## Section 4.9 — Documentation Standards: Legal Pitfalls and EHR Best Practices

### 4.9.1 Principles of Nursing Documentation

Documentation is the written record of the communication that occurs between a nurse and the healthcare system on behalf of a patient. It serves multiple simultaneous functions: clinical (communicating patient status and care delivered to the interdisciplinary team), legal (the primary evidence in malpractice and licensing actions), regulatory (demonstrating compliance with accreditation standards), financial (justifying billing and reimbursement), and research (contributing to the data that drives healthcare improvement). The enduring principle of nursing documentation — **if it wasn't documented, it wasn't done** — reflects the legal reality that undocumented care cannot be defended.

The **FACT** acronym describes the core attributes of defensible nursing documentation. **F — Factual** means recording only what was directly observed, measured, or communicated — not inferences or assumptions. For example, "Patient states pain is 7/10 in the right lower quadrant" is factual, while "Patient appears to be in severe pain" is an interpretation that should be supported by observable evidence. **A — Accurate** requires correct spelling, proper medical terminology where appropriate, and precise measurements; nurses should avoid vague language ("a lot," "some," "good," "poor") in favor of specific, measurable descriptors. **C — Complete** means documenting all relevant assessments, interventions, patient responses, and communications with other providers — including recording when and why care was *not* provided; if a patient refused a scheduled medication, for instance, both the refusal and the patient education provided must be documented. **T — Timely** means documenting as contemporaneously as possible with the clinical event, since real-time documentation is more accurate and more defensible than documentation completed hours later from memory.

The distinction between **objective** and **subjective** documentation language is foundational. Objective documentation records observable, measurable phenomena: "BP 92/54, HR 118, patient pale and diaphoretic, reporting chest pain 8/10 radiating to left jaw." Subjective documentation records the patient's own reports of their experience: "Patient states, 'I've had this pain before but it never hurt this badly.'" Both are legitimate and necessary components of nursing documentation; neither should be substituted for the other.

### 4.9.2 Legal Implications of Documentation

Because the clinical record is the primary evidence in malpractice litigation, licensing board investigations, and regulatory audits, nursing documentation carries significant legal weight. Errors of commission (documenting events that did not occur) and errors of omission (failing to document events that did occur) both create legal exposure.

**Late entries and addenda** — documentation completed after the fact — are permissible and sometimes necessary, but they must be identified explicitly as late entries with the current date and time (not backdated to the time of the event) and a statement of the reason for the delay. Backdating documentation, altering a completed entry, or deleting documented information without a proper amendment notation are acts that constitute fraud and tampering with a legal record. These are grounds for immediate termination, licensing revocation, and criminal prosecution.

**Incident reports** (also called occurrence reports or adverse event reports) document unexpected events — falls, medication errors, patient complaints, equipment failures — but are distinct from the patient's clinical record. Incident report content should not be referenced in the clinical chart; doing so subjects the incident report to discovery in litigation, defeating its purpose as a quality-improvement tool. The clinical chart should document the clinical facts: "Patient found on floor beside bed at 1432. Assessment performed. No evidence of injury. Physician notified. Fall prevention interventions reinforced." The incident report documents the institutional response.

**Copy-forward errors** in electronic health records represent a modern documentation hazard. When a nurse copies and pastes yesterday's assessment into today's note without reviewing and updating it, outdated information is propagated through the record as current. This practice has contributed to missed diagnoses, inappropriate treatment decisions, and legal liability. All documentation must reflect the current clinical reality, not the copied past.

### 4.9.3 EHR Communication Features

Modern electronic health record systems provide communication tools that, used correctly, enhance care coordination and patient safety. **Secure messaging** platforms embedded in the EHR allow providers to communicate about patients in a HIPAA-compliant environment with an automatic timestamp and audit trail. Flags and alerts within the EHR call attention to critical values, allergies, fall risk, and isolation precautions; nurses are responsible for acknowledging and acting on these alerts in a timely and documented fashion.

HIPAA governs all electronic communication involving protected health information (PHI). The law prohibits the use of personal email, consumer text messaging applications (even encrypted ones not approved by the institution), and social media platforms for patient-related communication. Violations can result in civil monetary penalties of up to $1.9 million per violation category per year, in addition to institutional sanctions and professional consequences (U.S. Department of Health and Human Services, 2022).

Verbal orders — physician or provider orders communicated verbally to a nurse rather than entered directly into the CPOE (computerized provider order entry) system — require specific documentation protocols. The nurse receiving a verbal order must write it down immediately, read it back verbatim to the prescriber, receive confirmation, and enter it into the EHR labeled as a verbal order with the ordering provider's name. The provider must co-sign the order within the timeframe specified by institutional policy, typically within 24–48 hours.

### 4.9.4 Bedside Shift Report

Bedside shift report (BSR) — conducting the nursing handoff at the patient's bedside rather than at the nursing station — integrates therapeutic communication and safe handoff practice in a single patient-centered intervention. Evidence supports BSR on multiple dimensions: patients who participate in bedside report report higher satisfaction scores, report feeling more informed about their care, and demonstrate better safety outcomes including lower fall rates (Sand-Jecklin & Sherman, 2014).

BSR also involves the patient as an active participant: the nurse invites the patient to ask questions, correct inaccuracies ("Is there anything we got wrong or missed?"), and identify concerns for the incoming shift. This practice directly enacts the principles of patient-centered care and therapeutic communication — it treats the patient as the expert on their own experience and the central stakeholder in their care plan.

> **Quality Improvement: Bedside Shift Report and HCAHPS Scores**
> Hospital Consumer Assessment of Healthcare Providers and Systems (HCAHPS) survey data consistently show that nurse communication is among the top predictors of patient experience scores and is directly linked to reimbursement rates through CMS value-based purchasing. Bedside shift report has been associated with improvements in the HCAHPS domains of "Nurses always communicated well," "Staff always explained medicines before giving them," and "Overall hospital rating." Institutions that implement and sustain structured BSR protocols report measurable and sustained improvements in communication-related quality metrics (Mullen-Fortino et al., 2019).

## Case Study: "Lost in Translation"

**Setting:** Medical-surgical unit, tertiary care hospital

**Scenario:**

Maria Delgado is a 72-year-old Spanish-speaking woman with limited English proficiency who is admitted to the medical-surgical unit for management of newly diagnosed type 2 diabetes. She emigrated from Mexico twenty years ago and speaks conversational English, but her medical vocabulary is minimal and she frequently defers to her son, Eduardo, a 45-year-old bilingual contractor, to navigate healthcare interactions. Eduardo accompanies Maria to the admission assessment and immediately volunteers: "I'll translate for her. It's easier this way, and she doesn't like talking to strangers."

The night nurse, Rosalind, completes the admission history using Eduardo as the interpreter. Maria nods frequently and says "sí, sí" when Eduardo summarizes Rosalind's questions. The assessment is efficient — completed in under 15 minutes. The nursing note reads: "Patient alert and oriented x3, pleasant and cooperative. History obtained with assistance of family member. Patient denies pain. Verbalized understanding of reason for admission."

Later that evening, Rosalind returns to check Maria's blood glucose and finds her sitting upright, wide-eyed, looking at the insulin syringe on the bedside table. Using the hospital's telephone interpreter service, Rosalind begins to speak with Maria directly. Within minutes, the interpreter reveals what Eduardo's translations had not: Maria believes insulin is a sign that her diabetes has reached a terminal stage — a belief rooted in the experience of watching her mother die of complications shortly after starting insulin in Mexico. She is terrified and has decided she would rather die than "go on the needle." She has told Eduardo these fears but he has not relayed them, saying, "I didn't want to worry anyone."

**Scene 2:** The following morning, the diabetes educator, Tomas, arrives for a structured teaching session. He is aware of Maria's fears from Rosalind's amended nursing note. He uses a telephone interpreter, sits at Maria's level, and opens with: "I understand you have some very serious concerns about the insulin. I'd really like to hear what this feels like from your perspective before we talk about anything else." The session runs 45 minutes. By the end, Maria has not committed to insulin but has agreed to review written materials in Spanish and to meet with Tomas again before discharge.

**Scene 3:** At 0700, the night nurse gives morning report to the incoming day nurse, Janelle, at the nursing station. The SBAR handoff takes two minutes: "Maria Delgado, room 614, type 2 DM, stable. Her glucose was 182 this morning. She's refusing insulin. Educator will be back." Janelle does not learn about the language barrier, the cultural belief, or the specifics of the education plan. At 0900, Janelle attempts to administer Maria's morning insulin. Maria shakes her head vigorously and tries to explain in Spanish. Janelle, who speaks no Spanish, documents "Patient non-compliant with insulin administration" and moves to her next patient.

**Discussion Questions:**

1. Why is using a family member as an interpreter problematic in Maria's case, even when the patient appears to consent? What are the nurse's legal obligations under federal law, and what specific harm occurred in this case as a direct result of the ad hoc interpretation?

2. Identify three specific therapeutic communication techniques that Rosalind used effectively in her second interaction with Maria. For each technique, describe how it contributed to Maria's disclosure and what would likely have happened if that technique had not been used.

3. Using the I-PASS framework, reconstruct the morning handoff from Rosalind to Janelle as it should have been conducted. What information was missing from the actual handoff, and how did that omission contribute to the adverse event in Scene 3?

4. Janelle's documentation reads "Patient non-compliant with insulin administration." Identify at least three problems with this documentation entry — including concerns about language, accuracy, and professional implications — and rewrite the entry to meet the FACT criteria.

## Chapter Summary

Therapeutic communication is simultaneously the most foundational clinical skill a nurse possesses and one of the most complex. It encompasses the structural mechanics of how messages are sent and received (Section 4.1), the multilayered dimensions of verbal and nonverbal expression (Section 4.2), the phased architecture of the therapeutic nurse-patient relationship (Section 4.3), a practical toolkit of evidence-based techniques (Section 4.4), and an understanding of the patterns that undermine trust and information exchange (Section 4.5). When applied to the specialized challenge of behavior change, motivational interviewing and the OARS framework provide a structured, person-centered approach that honors patient autonomy while supporting health (Section 4.6). Communication must be adapted thoughtfully across the lifespan, across linguistic and cultural contexts, and in the heightened circumstances of crisis (Section 4.7). In the interdisciplinary team environment, structured tools — SBAR for urgent escalation and I-PASS for routine handoffs — translate therapeutic principles into patient safety practices with demonstrated clinical effectiveness (Section 4.8). Finally, the principles of therapeutic communication extend into every written entry a nurse makes in the clinical record: accurate, objective, complete, and timely documentation is the enduring communicative legacy of every patient encounter (Section 4.9).

Communication failure remains among the leading root causes of preventable harm in healthcare. The skills presented in this chapter are not supplementary soft skills but core clinical competencies with direct consequences for patient safety, health equity, and clinical outcomes. Mastery requires not only knowledge but deliberate practice, honest self-reflection, and a career-long commitment to communicating in ways that honor every patient's dignity, autonomy, and inherent worth.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. Every clinical communication involves a sender, message, channel, receiver, feedback loop, and noise; identifying where a communication has broken down in this model allows for targeted repair.
2. Nonverbal signals — kinesics, proxemics, haptics, and paralanguage — carry the majority of interpersonal meaning; when verbal and nonverbal messages are incongruent, patients will believe the nonverbal.
3. The therapeutic nurse-patient relationship progresses through three phases — orientation, working, and termination — each with specific tasks; professional boundaries protect the integrity of this relationship at every phase.
4. Evidence-based therapeutic communication techniques (open-ended questions, reflection, clarification, acknowledgment of feelings, silence) can be learned, practiced, and applied immediately in clinical settings.
5. Non-therapeutic communication patterns (false reassurance, giving advice, paternalism, changing the subject, stereotyping) damage trust and block therapeutic communication; recognizing them in one's own practice is the first step toward replacing them.
6. Motivational interviewing, using the OARS framework, is an evidence-based approach to supporting behavior change that respects patient autonomy and has demonstrated clinical effectiveness across multiple health behaviors.
7. Communication with children, older adults, patients in crisis, and non-English-speaking patients requires deliberate adaptation; professional interpreters, validated assessment tools, and developmental knowledge are the tools for this adaptation.
8. SBAR structures urgent clinical escalation calls to physicians; I-PASS structures safe shift handoffs; both tools reduce preventable adverse events by ensuring completeness and clarity in high-stakes communications.
9. Documentation that is factual, accurate, complete, and timely (FACT) protects patients, supports the care team, and provides the nurse's primary legal defense; falsification, omission, and copy-forward errors are legally and professionally indefensible.
